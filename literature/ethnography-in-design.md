## Foundational Works

1. **Suchman, L. (1987). Plans and Situated Actions: The Problem of Human-Machine Communication.** Cambridge University Press.
   ISBN: 978-0-521-33739-7
   URL: https://dl.acm.org/doi/abs/10.5555/38407
   - The originating text for ethnography in HCI. Challenged cognitive science models by showing that human action is fundamentally situated, not planned. Demonstrated ethnographic methods' power for understanding technology use. Transformed HCI's understanding of human-computer interaction.

2. **Suchman, L. (2007). Human-Machine Reconfigurations: Plans and Situated Actions** (2nd ed.). Cambridge University Press.
   DOI: 10.1017/CBO9780511808418
   - Revised and expanded edition incorporating 20 years of reflection; added feminist STS and postcolonial perspectives. Extended the situated action framework to encompass human-machine "reconfigurations."

3. **Button, G., & Dourish, P. (1996). Technomethodology: Paradoxes and Possibilities.** *Proceedings of CHI '96*, 19–26.
   DOI: 10.1145/238386.238394
   - Proposed "technomethodology" — combining ethnomethodology with technology design. Argued that ethnographic understanding of work practices should directly inform system architecture, not just interface design.

4. **Dourish, P. (2006). Implications for Design.** *Proceedings of CHI '06*, 541–550.
   DOI: 10.1145/1124772.1124855
   - Influential critique of the "implications for design" convention. Argued ethnography's value in HCI exceeds instrumental design recommendations; ethnography contributes to understanding, not just to design specifications.

## Workplace Studies and CSCW

5. **Heath, C., & Luff, P. (1992). Collaboration and Control: Crisis Management and Multimedia Technology in London Underground Line Control Rooms.** *Computer Supported Cooperative Work*, 1(1–2), 69–94.
   DOI: 10.1007/BF00752451
   - Classic workplace study using video-based ethnography; demonstrated how ethnographic methods reveal collaborative practices invisible to other methods.

6. **Hughes, J. A., Randall, D., & Shapiro, D. (1992). Faltering from Ethnography to Design.** *Proceedings of CSCW '92*, 115–122.
   DOI: 10.1145/143457.143469
   - Early critical examination of the gap between ethnographic findings and design implications; the "faltering" problem that persists.

7. **Anderson, R. J. (1994). Representations and Requirements: The Value of Ethnography in System Design.** *Human-Computer Interaction*, 9(2), 151–182.
   DOI: 10.1207/s15327051hci0902_1
   - Analyzed how ethnographic representations translate into design requirements; bridged sociology and systems design.

8. **Bentley, R., Hughes, J. A., Randall, D., Rodden, T., Sawyer, P., Shapiro, D., & Sommerville, I. (1992). Ethnographically-Informed Systems Design for Air Traffic Control.** *Proceedings of CSCW '92*, 123–129.
   DOI: 10.1145/143457.143470
   - Landmark case study of ethnography informing ATC system design; demonstrated ethnography's practical value for complex system design.

9. **Crabtree, A., Rodden, T., Tolmie, P., & Button, G. (2009). Ethnography Considered Harmful.** *Proceedings of CHI '09*, 879–888.
   DOI: 10.1145/1518701.1518835
   - Provocative critique arguing that ethnography in HCI had become superficial and decorative. Called for return to rigorous ethnomethodological analysis. Reinvigorated debate about ethnography's role in design.

## Design Ethnography as Method

10. **Crabtree, A., Rouncefield, M., & Tolmie, P. (2012). Doing Design Ethnography.** Springer.
    DOI: 10.1007/978-1-4471-2726-0
    - Comprehensive methodological guide to design ethnography. Codified the practice of ethnography specifically oriented toward design — the method that Anticipatory Ethnography and Thing Ethnography later extend.

11. **Blomberg, J., Giacomi, J., Mosher, A., & Swenton-Wall, P. (1993). Ethnographic Field Methods and Their Relation to Design.** In D. Schuler & A. Namioka (Eds.), *Participatory Design: Perspectives on Systems Design*, 123–155. Lawrence Erlbaum.
    ISBN: 978-0-8058-0951-2
    - Connected ethnographic methods to participatory design; positioned ethnography as a tool for inclusive, democratic design processes.

12. **Blomberg, J., & Burrell, M. (2009). An Ethnographic Approach to Design.** In A. Sears & J. A. Jacko (Eds.), *Human-Computer Interaction: Development Process*, 71–94. CRC Press.
    DOI: 10.1201/9781420088892
    - Updated overview of ethnographic approaches in HCI design; situated ethnography within contemporary HCI practice.

13. **Blomberg, J., & Karasti, H. (2013). Positioning Ethnography within Participatory Design.** In J. Simonsen & T. Robertson (Eds.), *Routledge International Handbook of Participatory Design*, 86–116. Routledge.
    DOI: 10.4324/9780203108543-6
    - Positioned ethnography within the broader PD tradition; explored how ethnography and cooperative design connect historically and methodologically.

14. **Salvador, T., Bell, G., & Anderson, K. (1999). Design Ethnography.** *Design Management Journal*, 10(4), 35–41.
    DOI: 10.1111/j.1948-7169.1999.tb00274.x
    - Early industry articulation of design ethnography as a practice distinct from academic ethnography.

15. **Button, G. (2000). The Ethnographic Tradition and Design.** *Design Studies*, 21(4), 319–332.
    DOI: 10.1016/S0142-694X(00)00005-3
    - Positioned the ethnographic tradition within design; argued for ethnomethodological approach as most productive for design.

## Ethnography's Integration into HCI

16. **Dourish, P. (2001). Where the Action Is: The Foundations of Embodied Interaction.** MIT Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-262-54178-7
    URL: https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262541787/
    - Connected ethnographic understanding of situated practice with phenomenological philosophy; laid groundwork for embodied interaction design.

17. **Randall, D., Harper, R., & Rouncefield, M. (2007). Fieldwork for Design: Theory and Practice.** Springer.
    DOI: 10.1007/978-1-84628-768-8
    - Practical guide to fieldwork in design contexts; bridged academic ethnography and design practice.

18. **Millen, D. R. (2000). Rapid Ethnography: Time Deepening Strategies for HCI Field Research.** *Proceedings of DIS '00*, 280–286.
    DOI: 10.1145/347642.347763
    - Proposed methods for conducting ethnography under time constraints typical of design projects; pragmatic adaptation of ethnographic methods.

19. **Nardi, B. A. (Ed.) (1996). Context and Consciousness: Activity Theory and Human-Computer Interaction.** MIT Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-262-14058-4
    URL: https://direct.mit.edu/books/edited-volume/3274/Context-and-ConsciousnessActivity-Theory-and-Human
    - Introduced activity theory as a framework for HCI; provided an alternative to ethnomethodology for understanding technology use in context.

## Digital, Visual, and Sensory Ethnography

20. **Pink, S. (2009). Doing Sensory Ethnography.** SAGE Publications.
    DOI: 10.4135/9781446249383
    - Pioneered sensory ethnography — attending to all senses, not just the visual. Extended ethnographic methods' reach for understanding embodied technology use.

21. **Pink, S. (2013). Doing Visual Ethnography** (3rd ed.). SAGE Publications.
    ISBN: 978-1-4462-1100-9
    - Standard reference for visual ethnographic methods; visual documentation as both method and output.

22. **Hine, C. (2000). Virtual Ethnography.** SAGE Publications.
    ISBN: 978-0-7619-5896-3
    - Foundational text for studying online communities ethnographically; extended ethnography's field site to include virtual spaces.

23. **Hine, C. (2015). Ethnography for the Internet: Embedded, Embodied and Everyday.** Bloomsbury Academic.
    ISBN: 978-0-8578-5570-1
    - Updated framework for internet ethnography as E3 (embedded, embodied, everyday); moved beyond virtual/real divide.

## Critical and Reflexive Ethnography in Design

24. **Dourish, P., & Bell, G. (2011). Divining a Digital Future: Mess and Mythology in Ubiquitous Computing.** MIT Press.
    DOI: 10.7551/mitpress/9780262015554.001.0001
    - Ethnographic critique of ubiquitous computing's narratives; demonstrated how ethnography can challenge design's own mythologies.

25. **Sengers, P., Boehner, K., David, S., & Kaye, J. (2005). Reflective Design.** *Proceedings of the 4th Decennial Aarhus Conference on Critical Computing*, 49–58.
    DOI: 10.1145/1094562.1094569
    - Reflective design as methodology incorporating ethnographic sensibility; designs that encourage critical reflection on technology use.

26. **Marcus, G. E. (1995). Ethnography in/of the World System: The Emergence of Multi-Sited Ethnography.** *Annual Review of Anthropology*, 24, 95–117.
    DOI: 10.1146/annurev.an.24.100195.000523
    - Multi-sited ethnography as methodological innovation; precursor to the temporal and ontological extensions (anticipatory ethnography, thing ethnography) in design research.

27. **Hammersley, M., & Atkinson, P. (2007). Ethnography: Principles in Practice** (3rd ed.). Routledge.
    DOI: 10.4324/9780203944769
    - Standard ethnographic methods reference; the disciplinary foundation that design ethnography draws from and adapts.

## Ethnography's Branches in Design Research

28. **Giaccardi, E., Cila, N., Speed, C., & Caldwell, M. (2016). Thing Ethnography: Doing Design Research with Non-Humans.** *Proceedings of DIS '16*, 377–387.
    DOI: 10.1145/2901790.2901905
    - Thing ethnography: ethnographic methods extended to nonhuman perspectives. Ethnography branches toward more-than-human design.

29. **Lindley, J., Sharma, D., & Potts, R. (2014). Anticipatory Ethnography: Design Fiction as an Input to Design Ethnography.** *Proceedings of EPIC '14*, 237–253.
    DOI: 10.1111/1559-8918.01030
    - Anticipatory ethnography: ethnographic methods extended to fictional/future contexts. Ethnography branches toward design fiction.

30. **Ingold, T. (2013). Making: Anthropology, Archaeology, Art and Architecture.** Routledge.
    DOI: 10.4324/9780203559055
    - Anthropological perspective on making; connects ethnographic traditions with design's material practices.

## Additional Key Works

31. **Suchman, L. (2002). Located Accountabilities in Technology Production.** *Scandinavian Journal of Information Systems*, 14(2), Article 7.
    URL: https://aisel.aisnet.org/sjis/vol14/iss2/7/
    - Feminist politics of accountability in technology design; argued that design responsibility must be "located" within specific social relations of production. Extended situated actions framework to questions of power and ethics in design.

32. **Bell, G., & Dourish, P. (2007). Yesterday's Tomorrows: Notes on Ubiquitous Computing's Dominant Vision.** *Personal and Ubiquitous Computing*, 11(2), 133–143.
    DOI: 10.1007/s00779-006-0071-x

    ISBN: 978-0-472-05098-7
    - Three-year participatory ethnography of WoW; extended ethnographic methods to virtual worlds. Demonstrated ethnography's adaptability to digital cultural contexts.

34. **Laurel, B. (1991/2013). Computers as Theatre** (2nd ed.). Addison-Wesley / Pearson.
    ISBN: 978-0-321-91862-8
    - Framed human-computer interaction through dramatic theory; human experience of technology as theatrical engagement. Influenced ethnographic attention to narrative and experience in design.

35. **Laurel, B. (Ed.) (2003). Design Research: Methods and Perspectives.** MIT Press.

    - Collected diverse design research methods including ethnographic approaches; positioned ethnography within the broader design research toolkit.

## Connections to Other Lineages

- **Participatory Design** (co-evolved): Ethnography provided the observational grounding; PD provided the democratic participation. Blomberg & Karasti (2013) trace the intertwined history.
- **Thing Ethnography** (descendant): Extends ethnographic methods to nonhuman perspectives (Giaccardi et al., 2016)
- **Anticipatory Ethnography** (descendant): Extends ethnographic temporal scope into fictional futures (Lindley et al., 2014)
- **RtD**: Ethnographic observation informs design; RtD generates knowledge through design — complementary epistemologies
- **HCD**: Ethnography is the primary empirical method within HCD; its extensions challenge HCD's boundaries
- **More-than-Human Design**: Thing ethnography provides the methodological bridge from ethnography to posthuman perspectives
- **CSCW**: Workplace studies (Heath & Luff, Bentley et al.) were the original vehicles for ethnography in computing
- **Design Anthropology**: Akama, Pink, and Sumartojo's work extends ethnography through anthropological futures thinking

## Summary

Ethnography entered HCI through Lucy Suchman's (1987) *Plans and Situated Actions*, which demonstrated that human action is fundamentally situated rather than planned — a direct challenge to the cognitive science models underlying early AI and interface design. At Xerox PARC and EuroPARC, researchers including Suchman, Blomberg, and Button developed practical approaches for connecting ethnographic observation to systems design. Button & Dourish's (1996) "technomethodology" proposed that ethnographic understanding should inform system architecture, not just interfaces.

Through the 1990s, workplace studies in CSCW (Heath & Luff 1992; Bentley et al. 1992; Hughes et al. 1992) established ethnography as essential for understanding collaborative technology use. The persistent challenge — translating ethnographic insight into design — was critically examined by Hughes et al. ("Faltering from Ethnography to Design") and later by Dourish (2006), who argued that ethnography's value exceeded mere "implications for design." Crabtree et al.'s (2009) provocative "Ethnography Considered Harmful" reinvigorated this debate by arguing that HCI ethnography had become superficial.

Methodologically, ethnography in design has diversified through digital ethnography (Hine 2000, 2015), sensory ethnography (Pink 2009), rapid ethnography (Millen 2000), and activity theory (Nardi 1996). Most significantly, it has branched into two major extensions: Thing Ethnography (Giaccardi et al. 2016), which decenter the human observer in favor of nonhuman perspectives, and Anticipatory Ethnography (Lindley et al. 2014), which extends ethnographic temporality into fictional futures. These branches make ethnography a root method from which multiple design research lineages grow, positioning it as foundational to the Observatory's genealogical map.
