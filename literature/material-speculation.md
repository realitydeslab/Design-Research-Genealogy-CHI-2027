## Foundational Works

1. **Wakkary, R., Odom, W., Hauser, S., Hertz, G., & Lin, H. (2015). Material Speculation: Actual Artifacts for Critical Inquiry.** *Proceedings of the 5th Decennial Aarhus Conference on Critical Alternatives*, 97–108.
   DOI: 10.7146/aahcc.v1i1.21299
   - The originating paper. Proposed material speculation as complementary to speculative design, using actual, situated design artifacts in the everyday as sites of critical inquiry. Functional deployment distinguishes this from gallery-based speculative design.

2. **Wakkary, R., Odom, W., Hauser, S., Hertz, G., & Lin, H. (2016). A Short Guide to Material Speculation: Actual Artifacts for Critical Inquiry.** *Interactions*, 23(2), 44–48.
   DOI: 10.1145/2889278
   - Accessible introduction to the concept, distilling the 2015 paper into a practical guide for design researchers.

3. **Wakkary, R. (2021). Things We Could Design: For More Than Human-Centered Worlds.** MIT Press.
   ISBN: 978-0-262-04468-6
   URL: https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262044684/things-we-could-design/
   - Book-length elaboration situating material speculation within a broader philosophy of design beyond human-centeredness. Proposes design as "nomadic practices" — a multiplicity of intentionalities and situated knowledges. Extends argument that things have their own forms of agency and temporal existence.

4. **Ratto, M. (2011). Critical Making: Conceptual and Material Studies in Technology and Social Life.** *The Information Society*, 27(4), 252–260.
   DOI: 10.1080/01972243.2011.583819
   - Defined critical making as bridging conceptual critique and material practice. Shares material speculation's emphasis on the material act of construction as knowledge-generating, but focuses more on the making process than long-term deployment.

## Exemplary Material Speculation Projects

5. **Wakkary, R., Oogjes, D., Hauser, S., Lin, H., Cao, C., Ma, L., & Duel, T. (2017). Morse Things: A Design Inquiry into the Gap Between Things and Us.** *Proceedings of DIS '17*, 503–514.
   DOI: 10.1145/3064663.3064734
   - Morse Things: computational objects that communicate with each other in Morse code without human mediation. Deployed in homes; exemplar of material speculation as long-term inquiry into thing-to-thing relations.

6. **Odom, W., Wakkary, R., Lim, Y., Desjardins, A., Hengeveld, B., & Banks, R. (2016). From Research Prototype to Research Product.** *Proceedings of CHI '16*, 2549–2561.
   DOI: 10.1145/2858036.2858167
   - Proposed "research products" — fully functional artifacts deployed long-term in homes — as a form of material speculation extending RtD beyond prototyping.

7. **Odom, W., Wakkary, R., Bertran, I., Harkness, M., Hertz, G., & Hol, J. (2018). Attending to Slowness and Temporality with Olly and Slow Game.** *Proceedings of CHI '18*, Article 77.
   DOI: 10.1145/3173574.3173651
   - Olly and Slow Game: research products deployed in homes to investigate slow, temporal relationships with technology. Combines material speculation with slow technology principles.

8. **Oogjes, D., & Wakkary, R. (2017). Videos of Things: Speculating on, Anticipating and Synthesizing Technological Mediations.** *Proceedings of CHI '17*, 4489–4500.
   DOI: 10.1145/3025453.3025748
   - Videos that portray the mediated, lived world of computational artifacts informed by postphenomenology. A design method for elaborating the mediated qualities of technologies within material speculation.

9. **Oogjes, D., & Wakkary, R. (2022). Weaving Stories: Toward Repertoires for Designing Things.** *Proceedings of CHI '22*, Article 406.
   DOI: 10.1145/3491102.3501930
   - Extended material speculation through narrative approaches; developed "repertoires" for designing things that weave their own stories over time.

10. **Lin, H., Wakkary, R., & Oogjes, D. (2019). The Tilting Bowl: Electronic Design for a Research Product.** *Proceedings of DIS '19*, 345–357.
    DOI: 10.1145/3322276.3323701
    - The Tilting Bowl as counterfactual artifact: a bowl that tilts when objects are placed in it, contradicting expected behavior. Exemplifies material speculation's use of counterfactual design.

11. **Wakkary, R., Oogjes, D., Lin, H., & Hauser, S. (2022). Two Years or More of Co-speculation: Polylogues of Philosophers, Designers, and a Tilting Bowl.** *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 29(4), Article 33.
    DOI: 10.1145/3514235
    - Long-term study of philosophers living with the Tilting Bowl; explores how counterfactual artifacts generate philosophical discourse over extended periods.

12. **Desjardins, A., & Wakkary, R. (2016). Living In A Prototype: A Reconfigured Space.** *Proceedings of CHI '16*, 5274–5285.
    DOI: 10.1145/2858036.2858261
    - Autoethnographic research where the researcher inhabits a materially speculative environment. Extended material speculation into autobiographical design methodology.

13. **Zhong, C., Wakkary, R., Chen, A. Y. S., & Oogjes, D. (2021). deformTable: Crafting a Shape-changing Device for Creative Appropriations Over Time.** *Proceedings of DIS '21*, 1253–1265.
    DOI: 10.1145/3461778.3462138
    - deformTable: a shape-changing artifact with a counterfactual feature (rises when weight is added). Material speculation applied to shape-changing design.

14. **Wakkary, R., Desjardins, A., Hauser, S., & Maestri, L. (2013). A Sustainable Design Fiction: Green Practices.** *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 20(4), Article 23, 1–34.
    DOI: 10.1145/2494265
    - Design fictions as speculative inquiry into sustainable practices. Precursor to material speculation, demonstrating how design fiction can operate through situated artifacts.

## Theoretical Context

15. **Dunne, A., & Raby, F. (2013). Speculative Everything: Design, Fiction, and Social Dreaming.** MIT Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-262-01984-1
    - The canonical speculative design text. Material speculation responds to its reliance on gallery objects and fictional scenarios by insisting on actual, functional artifacts.

16. **Auger, J. (2013). Speculative Design: Crafting the Speculation.** *Digital Creativity*, 24(1), 11–35.
    DOI: 10.1080/14626268.2013.767276
    - Analyzed the craft of speculation; material speculation offers a distinct mode: speculation through functional deployment rather than exhibition.

17. **Pierce, J. (2021). In Tension with Progression: Grasping the Frictional Tendencies of Speculative, Critical, and other Alternative Designs.** *Proceedings of CHI '21*.
    DOI: 10.1145/3411764.3445406
    - Material speculation occupies an unusual position: both frictional (critical, questioning) and progressional (functional, deployed).

18. **Elsden, C., Chatting, D., Durrant, A. C., Garbett, A., Nissen, B., Vines, J., & Kirk, D. S. (2017). On Speculative Enactments.** *Proceedings of CHI '17*, 5386–5399.
    DOI: 10.1145/3025453.3025503
    - Speculative enactments create temporary immersive scenarios; material speculation creates permanent artifacts. Both share the impulse to move beyond gallery-based speculation.

19. **Bardzell, J., Bardzell, S., & Hansen, L. K. (2015). Immodest Proposals: Research through Design and Knowledge.** *Proceedings of CHI '15*, 2093–2102.
    DOI: 10.1145/2702123.2702400
    - Explored how provocative RtD designs generate knowledge; material speculation adds the dimension of long-term, situated knowledge generation.

## Material Philosophy & Thing-Centered Design

20. **Giaccardi, E., Cila, N., Speed, C., & Caldwell, M. (2016). Thing Ethnography: Doing Design Research with Non-Humans.** *Proceedings of DIS '16*, 377–387.
    DOI: 10.1145/2901790.2901905
    - Thing ethnography — using things as co-ethnographers via sensors and data. Shares material speculation's interest in thing perspectives but focuses on ethnographic method rather than speculative deployment.

21. **Giaccardi, E., & Redström, J. (2020). Technology and More-Than-Human Design.** *Design Issues*, 36(4), 33–44.
    DOI: 10.1162/desi_a_00612
    - Material speculation exemplifies more-than-human design by treating deployed artifacts as co-inhabitants with their own temporal rhythms and agencies.

22. **Wiltse, H. (Ed.). (2020). Relating to Things: Design, Technology and the Artificial.** Bloomsbury Academic.
    ISBN: 978-1-350-12425-7
    URL: https://www.bloomsbury.com/uk/relating-to-things-9781350124257/
    - Edited collection exploring our changing relationships with non-human actors. Material speculation is a key approach within this broader philosophical inquiry into thing relations.

23. **Redström, J., & Wiltse, H. (2019). Changing Things: The Future of Objects in a Digital World.** Bloomsbury Visual Arts.
    ISBN: 978-1-350-00435-1
    DOI: 10.5040/9781350004368
    - Describes contemporary things as "fluid assemblages" — dynamic, networked, contextually configured. Provides theoretical framework for understanding the kind of things material speculation creates and investigates.

24. **Wiberg, M. (2018). The Materiality of Interaction: Notes on the Materials of Interaction Design.** MIT Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-262-03751-8
    DOI: 10.7551/mitpress/9780262037518.001.0001
    - Describes the material turn in HCI from representation-driven to material-centered interaction design. Proposes interaction itself as a material. Provides theoretical grounding for material speculation's material focus.

25. **Wakkary, R. (2020). Nomadic Practices: A Posthuman Theory for Knowing Design.** *International Journal of Design*, 14(3), 117–128.
    URL: https://www.ijdesign.org/index.php/IJDesign/article/view/4039/928
    - Proposes design knowledge as nomadic — distributed across human and nonhuman actors. Theoretical foundation for material speculation's posthuman epistemology.

    DOI: 10.1080/07370024.2021.1913416
    - Extended slow technology theory through systematic analysis of material speculation artifacts deployed over years.

27. **Hallnäs, L., & Redström, J. (2001). Slow Technology — Designing for Reflection.** *Personal and Ubiquitous Computing*, 5(3), 201–212.
    DOI: 10.1007/PL00000019
    - Slow technology's emphasis on reflection over efficiency directly informs material speculation's commitment to long-term, temporal engagement with artifacts.

28. **Redström, J. (2017). Making Design Theory.** MIT Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-262-03606-3
    DOI: 10.7551/mitpress/9780262036658.001.0001
    - Theoretical framework for how design practice generates theory; material speculation is a concrete instance of this "making" epistemology.

## Connections to Other Lineages

- **RtD** (parent): Material speculation is a specific approach within RtD, generating knowledge through designed artifacts
- **Speculative Design** (parent): Responds to speculative design's reliance on fictional/gallery contexts by insisting on functional deployment
- **Slow Technology**: Shares commitment to long-term temporal engagement and reflection (Hallnäs & Redström 2001)
- **More-than-Human Design**: Deployed artifacts develop their own agencies and relationships over time (Wakkary 2021, Giaccardi & Redström 2020)
- **Critical Making**: Ratto's (2011) emphasis on material knowledge production, but material speculation extends into long-term deployment
- **Thing Ethnography**: Giaccardi et al. (2016) share interest in thing perspectives; material speculation deploys speculative things, thing ethnography studies existing things
- **Constructionism**: The epistemological claim that making functional things generates knowledge
- **Prototyping as Research**: Material speculation takes prototyping to its extreme — fully functional "research products"
- **Material Philosophy**: Wiltse (2020) and Redström & Wiltse (2019) provide philosophical grounding for understanding designed things as fluid, relational entities

## Summary

Material Speculation (Wakkary et al., 2015) bridges Research through Design and Speculative Design by insisting on actual, functional artifacts deployed in everyday settings as sites of critical inquiry. Unlike gallery-based speculative design (Dunne & Raby, 2013) or narrative-based design fiction, material speculation generates knowledge through long-term, situated encounters between people and things. The approach has been exemplified through research products like Morse Things (2017), Olly and Slow Game (2018), the Tilting Bowl (2019), and deformTable (2021), each deployed in homes for months to years.

The theoretical trajectory has moved from the initial positioning against speculative design (2015) toward a broader posthuman design philosophy. Wakkary's (2021) *Things We Could Design* frames material speculation within "nomadic practices" — design as distributed, posthuman knowledge-making. This connects to Giaccardi's thing ethnography (2016), Wiltse's relational philosophy of things (2020), Wiberg's material turn in interaction design (2018), and Redström & Wiltse's concept of "fluid assemblages" (2019). The common thread is a rejection of purely human-centered design in favor of attending to things' own agencies, temporalities, and material qualities.

Recent work (2020-2026) has extended material speculation into shape-changing artifacts (deformTable), multi-year philosophical co-speculation (Tilting Bowl polylogues), and ecological/sustainable design contexts. The approach remains distinctive in HCI for its insistence on functional deployment as a condition for critical knowledge — speculation that lives with people, not behind glass.
