# Literature Review: Research through Design (RtD)

## Overview

Research through Design (RtD) is a methodological approach in which design activities — making, prototyping, and reflecting on artifacts — serve as a primary means of generating new knowledge. The lineage traces from Frayling's foundational taxonomy (1993) through its formalization in HCI (Zimmerman et al., 2007) to ongoing debates about rigor, evaluation, and contribution types.

---

## Foundational Works

1. **Frayling, C. (1993). Research in Art and Design.** *Royal College of Art Research Papers*, 1(1), 1–5.
   URL: https://researchonline.rca.ac.uk/384/
   - Introduced the tripartite distinction: research *into*, *through*, and *for* art and design. The originating taxonomy for RtD as a concept.

2. **Zimmerman, J., Forlizzi, J., & Evenson, S. (2007). Research through Design as a Method for Interaction Design Research in HCI.** *Proceedings of CHI '07*, 493–502.
   DOI: 10.1145/1240624.1240704
   - Formalized RtD for the HCI community. Argued that design artifacts can be legitimate research contributions alongside empirical findings and theoretical frameworks.

3. **Zimmerman, J., & Forlizzi, J. (2008). The Role of Design Artifacts in Design Theory Construction.** *Artifact*, 2(1), 41–45.
   DOI: 10.1080/17493460802276893
   - Extended the 2007 argument, positioning design artifacts as theory-generating vehicles.

4. **Gaver, W. (2012). What Should We Expect from Research through Design?** *Proceedings of CHI '12*, 937–946.
   DOI: 10.1145/2207676.2208538
   - Argued against premature codification of RtD; advocated for diversity of approaches and annotated portfolios as a way to build knowledge.

5. **Stappers, P. J., & Giaccardi, E. (2017). Research through Design.** In M. Soegaard & R. Friis-Dam (Eds.), *The Encyclopedia of Human-Computer Interaction* (2nd ed.). Interaction Design Foundation.
   URL: https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/book/the-encyclopedia-of-human-computer-interaction-2nd-ed/research-through-design
   - Comprehensive overview chapter mapping the RtD landscape, its variants, and its relation to other research approaches.

## Rigor, Evaluation, and Contribution

6. **Zimmerman, J., Stolterman, E., & Forlizzi, J. (2010). An Analysis and Critique of Research through Design: Towards a Formalization of a Research Approach.** *Proceedings of DIS '10*, 310–319.
   DOI: 10.1145/1858171.1858228
   - Critiqued and analyzed RtD's lack of agreed-upon criteria for evaluation; proposed four lenses for assessing RtD contributions.

7. **Fallman, D. (2003). Design-Oriented Human-Computer Interaction.** *Proceedings of CHI '03*, 225–232.
   DOI: 10.1145/642611.642652
   - Early articulation of design-oriented research in HCI; positioned design as a way of knowing distinct from natural science.

8. **Fallman, D. (2008). The Interaction Design Research Triangle of Design Practice, Design Studies, and Design Exploration.** *Design Issues*, 24(3), 4–18.
   DOI: 10.1162/desi.2008.24.3.4
   - Proposed a triangular model for understanding interaction design research, integrating practice, studies, and exploration.

9. **Höök, K., & Löwgren, J. (2012). Strong Concepts: Intermediate-Level Knowledge in Interaction Design Research.** *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 19(3), Article 23.
   DOI: 10.1145/2362364.2362371
   - Introduced "strong concepts" as an intermediate form of design knowledge between instances and theory.

10. **Löwgren, J. (2013). Annotated Portfolios and Other Forms of Intermediate-Level Knowledge.** *Interactions*, 20(1), 30–34.
    DOI: 10.1145/2405716.2405725
    - Elaborated on annotated portfolios (cf. Gaver & Bowers) and other forms of intermediate knowledge in RtD.

11. **Gaver, W., & Bowers, J. (2012). Annotated Portfolios.** *Interactions*, 19(4), 40–49.
    DOI: 10.1145/2212877.2212889
    - Proposed annotated portfolios as a method for articulating design research knowledge across a body of design work.

12. **Koskinen, I., Zimmerman, J., Binder, T., Redström, J., & Wensveen, S. (2011). Design Research through Practice: From the Lab, Field, and Showroom.** Morgan Kaufmann.
    DOI: 10.1016/C2010-0-65896-2
    - Comprehensive book mapping three modes of constructive design research: lab, field, and showroom.

## Practice and Process

13. **Redström, J. (2017). Making Design Theory.** MIT Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-262-03606-3
    URL: https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262036061/making-design-theory/
    - Examined how design practice generates theory; proposed "design programs" and "design experiments" as theoretical constructs.

14. **Binder, T., & Redström, J. (2006). Exemplary Design Research.** *Proceedings of DRS '06*.
    URL: https://dl.designresearchsociety.org/drs-conference-papers/drs2006/researchpapers/16/
    - Articulated the notion of exemplary design research, where specific design cases carry general insights.

15. **Nelson, H. G., & Stolterman, E. (2012). The Design Way: Intentional Change in an Unpredictable World** (2nd ed.). MIT Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-262-01817-5
    - Philosophical foundation for design as a distinct tradition of inquiry, emphasizing intentionality and the "ultimate particular."

16. **Cross, N. (2001). Designerly Ways of Knowing: Design Discipline versus Design Science.** *Design Issues*, 17(3), 49–55.
    DOI: 10.1162/074793601750357196
    - Foundational argument for design as its own intellectual culture with distinct ways of knowing.

17. **Cross, N. (2006). Designerly Ways of Knowing.** Springer.
    DOI: 10.1007/1-84628-301-9
    - Book-length elaboration of design epistemology and methodology.

18. **Schön, D. A. (1983). The Reflective Practitioner: How Professionals Think in Action.** Basic Books.
    ISBN: 978-0-465-06878-4
    - Classic on reflection-in-action; foundational for understanding design as a reflective practice.

19. **Archer, B. (1995). The Nature of Research.** *Co-Design*, January 1995, 6–13.
    URL: https://archive.org/details/co-design-interdisciplinary-journal-of-design-january-1995
    - Early UK articulation of research in/through/for design, parallel to Frayling.

## RtD in Specific Domains

20. **Odom, W., Zimmerman, J., Davidoff, S., Forlizzi, J., Dey, A. K., & Lee, M. K. (2012). A Fieldwork of the Future with User Enactments.** *Proceedings of DIS '12*, 338–347.
    DOI: 10.1145/2317956.2318008
    - Demonstrated RtD methodology through speculative user enactments exploring slow technology concepts.

21. **Gaver, W., Bowers, J., Boucher, A., Gellerson, H., Pennington, S., Schmidt, A., Steed, A., Villars, N., & Walker, B. (2004). The Drift Table: Designing for Ludic Engagement.** *Proceedings of CHI '04 Extended Abstracts*, 885–900.
    DOI: 10.1145/985921.985947
    - Exemplar of RtD practice: a designed artifact (Drift Table) as research vehicle for exploring ludic engagement.

22. **Gaver, W., Beaver, J., & Benford, S. (2003). Ambiguity as a Resource for Design.** *Proceedings of CHI '03*, 233–240.
    DOI: 10.1145/642611.642653
    - Influential RtD paper arguing ambiguity is productive for design, not a deficiency.

23. **Sengers, P., Boehner, K., David, S., & Kaye, J. (2005). Reflective Design.** *Proceedings of the 4th Decennial Aarhus Conference on Critical Computing*, 49–58.
    DOI: 10.1145/1094562.1094569
    - Combined critical theory with design practice; advocated for designs that encourage reflection.

24. **Pierce, J. (2014). On the Presentation and Production of Design Research Artifacts in HCI.** *Proceedings of DIS '14*, 735–744.
    DOI: 10.1145/2598510.2598525
    - Analyzed how design artifacts are presented in HCI research publications.

25. **Bardzell, J., Bardzell, S., & Hansen, L. K. (2015). Immodest Proposals: Research through Design and Knowledge.** *Proceedings of CHI '15*, 2093–2102.
    DOI: 10.1145/2702123.2702400
    - Explored how RtD generates knowledge through "immodest proposals" — provocative designs that challenge norms.

## Constructive and Programmatic Approaches

26. **Wensveen, S., & Matthews, B. (2015). Prototypes and Prototyping in Design Research.** In P. A. Rodgers & J. Yee (Eds.), *Routledge Companion to Design Research*, 262–276.
    DOI: 10.4324/9781315758466-25
    - Examined the role of prototypes as research instruments in design research.

27. **Dalsgaard, P. (2017). Instruments of Inquiry: Understanding the Nature and Role of Tools in Design.** *International Journal of Design*, 11(1), 21–33.
    URL: http://www.ijdesign.org/index.php/IJDesign/article/view/2275
    - Analyzed design tools as "instruments of inquiry" that shape how designers investigate.

28. **Durrant, A. C., Vines, J., Wallace, J., & Yee, J. S. R. (2017). Research through Design: Twenty-First Century Makers and Materialities.** *Design Issues*, 33(3), 3–10.
    DOI: 10.1162/DESI_a_00447
    - Special issue introduction examining contemporary RtD practices.

29. **Frauenberger, C. (2019). Entanglement HCI the Next Wave?** *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 27(1), Article 2.
    DOI: 10.1145/3364998
    - Proposed "entanglement HCI" drawing on Barad's work; relevant to RtD's philosophical evolution.

30. **Stolterman, E., & Wiberg, M. (2010). Concept-Driven Interaction Design Research.** *Human–Computer Interaction*, 25(2), 95–118.
    DOI: 10.1080/07370020903586696
    - Proposed concept-driven design research as a complement to artifact-driven approaches.

31. **Dow, S., Ju, W., & Mackay, W. (2013). Projection, Place and Point-of-View in Research through Design.** In S. Price, C. Jewitt, & B. Brown (Eds.), *The SAGE Handbook of Digital Technology Research*, 266–285.
    DOI: 10.4135/9781446282229.n18
    - Examined spatial and perspectival dimensions of RtD practice.

32. **Bowers, J. (2012). The Logic of Annotated Portfolios: Communicating the Value of 'Research through Design'.** *Proceedings of DIS '12*, 68–77.
    DOI: 10.1145/2317956.2317968
    - Formalized the logic behind annotated portfolios as a communicative strategy for RtD.

33. **Dalsgaard, P., & Dindler, C. (2014). Between Theory and Practice: Bridging Concepts in HCI Research.** *Proceedings of CHI '14*, 1635–1644.
    DOI: 10.1145/2556288.2557342
    - Examined "bridging concepts" as intermediate knowledge forms connecting theory and practice.

## Summary

RtD emerged from Frayling's 1993 taxonomy, was formalized for HCI by Zimmerman et al. (2007), debated by Gaver (2012), and has since diversified into multiple strands: artifact-centered inquiry, annotated portfolios, strong concepts, concept-driven research, and entanglement approaches. It remains the foundational lineage from which many other design research methodologies branch.
