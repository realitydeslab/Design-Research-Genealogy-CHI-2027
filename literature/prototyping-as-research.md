## Foundational Works

1. **Houde, S., & Hill, C. (1997). What Do Prototypes Prototype?** In M. Helander, T. Landauer, & P. Prabhu (Eds.), *Handbook of Human-Computer Interaction* (2nd ed.), 367–381. Elsevier.
   DOI: 10.1016/B978-044481862-1.50082-0
   - Foundational taxonomy: prototypes explore "role" (function for user), "look and feel" (sensory experience), and "implementation" (technical functioning). Reframed prototyping from product step to design inquiry tool.

2. **Buchenau, M., & Suri, J. F. (2000). Experience Prototyping.** *Proceedings of DIS '00*, 424–433.
   DOI: 10.1145/347642.347802
   - "Experience prototyping" — prototyping holistic experience rather than just the artifact. Prototypes as vehicles for experiencing future conditions firsthand.

3. **Lim, Y.-K., Stolterman, E., & Tenenberg, J. (2008). The Anatomy of Prototypes: Prototypes as Filters, Prototypes as Manifestations of Design Ideas.** *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 15(2), Article 7.
   DOI: 10.1145/1375761.1375762
   - Prototypes as "filters" — selectively manifesting certain design dimensions while bracketing others. This filtering function makes prototyping a mode of inquiry.

4. **Floyd, C. (1984). A Systematic Look at Prototyping.** In R. Budde et al. (Eds.), *Approaches to Prototyping*, 1–18. Springer.
   DOI: 10.1007/978-3-642-69796-8_1
   - Early systematic analysis: exploratory, experimental, and evolutionary prototyping — categories still relevant to design research.

5. **Buxton, B. (2007). Sketching User Experiences: Getting the Design Right and the Right Design.** Morgan Kaufmann / Elsevier.
   ISBN: 978-0-12-374037-3
   URL: https://www.sciencedirect.com/book/9780123740373/sketching-user-experiences
   - Distinguished sketching from prototyping; sketching as pre-prototyping for exploring design ideas. Established sketching as a knowledge-generating practice.

6. **Schön, D. A. (1983). The Reflective Practitioner: How Professionals Think in Action.** Basic Books.
   ISBN: 978-0-465-06878-4
   - Reflection-in-action: practitioners learn through material engagement. Prototyping is the primary site of reflective practice in design research.

## Extensions & Applications: Prototyping as Knowledge Production

7. **Zimmerman, J., Forlizzi, J., & Evenson, S. (2007). Research through Design as a Method for Interaction Design Research in HCI.** *Proceedings of CHI '07*, 493–502.
   DOI: 10.1145/1240624.1240704
   - Formalized RtD; design artifacts (prototypes) as legitimate research contributions.

8. **Koskinen, I., Zimmerman, J., Binder, T., Redström, J., & Wensveen, S. (2011). Design Research through Practice: From the Lab, Field, and Showroom.** Morgan Kaufmann.
   DOI: 10.1016/C2010-0-65896-2
   - Constructive design research uses prototyping as core method; prototypes operate differently across lab, field, and showroom contexts.

9. **Stappers, P. J. (2007). Doing Design as a Part of Doing Research.** In R. Michel (Ed.), *Design Research Now*, 81–91. Birkhäuser.
   DOI: 10.1007/978-3-7643-8472-2_6
   - Prototyping is not subsidiary but a core part of research; prototypes generate questions as much as they test answers.

10. **Wensveen, S., & Matthews, B. (2015). Prototypes and Prototyping in Design Research.** In P. A. Rodgers & J. Yee (Eds.), *Routledge Companion to Design Research*, 262–276. Routledge.
    DOI: 10.4324/9781315758466-25
    - Prototypes as research instruments; distinguished prototyping for exploration, experimentation, and evaluation.

11. **Gaver, W. (2012). What Should We Expect from Research through Design?** *Proceedings of CHI '12*, 937–946.
    DOI: 10.1145/2207676.2208538
    - Prototypes should be valued as open-ended instruments of inquiry, not just validated designs.

12. **Redström, J. (2017). Making Design Theory.** MIT Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-262-03606-3
    - "Making" as theorizing; prototypes embody and generate design theory through "design programs" and "design experiments."

13. **Binder, T., & Redström, J. (2006). Exemplary Design Research.** *Proceedings of DRS '06*.
    URL: https://dl.designresearchsociety.org/drs-conference-papers/drs2006/researchpapers/16/
    - "Exemplary" design research: specific prototypes carry general insights. The prototype as research exemplar.

14. **Beaudouin-Lafon, M., & Mackay, W. E. (2003/2012). Prototyping Tools and Techniques.** In J. Jacko & A. Sears (Eds.), *The Human-Computer Interaction Handbook* (3rd ed.), Ch. 47. CRC Press.
    DOI: 10.1201/b11963-ch-47
    - Comprehensive survey of prototyping tools and techniques; introduced four dimensions for analyzing prototypes: representation, precision, interactivity, evolution.

15. **Dalsgaard, P. (2017). Instruments of Inquiry: Understanding the Nature and Role of Tools in Design.** *International Journal of Design*, 11(1), 21–33.
    URL: http://www.ijdesign.org/index.php/IJDesign/article/view/2275
    - Prototypes as "instruments of inquiry" — tools that shape what designers can discover and know.

## Specialized Prototyping Methods

16. **Höök, K. (2018). Designing with the Body: Somaesthetic Interaction Design.** MIT Press.
    DOI: 10.7551/mitpress/11481.001.0001
    - Body as prototype. Soma design uses bodily experience as the primary prototyping medium; somaesthetic appreciation as design method.

17. **Höök, K., & Löwgren, J. (2012). Strong Concepts: Intermediate-Level Knowledge in Interaction Design Research.** *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 19(3), Article 23.
    DOI: 10.1145/2362364.2362371
    - "Strong concepts" emerge from prototyping across multiple projects; prototypes generate intermediate knowledge between instances and theory.

18. **Odom, W., Wakkary, R., Lim, Y., Desjardins, A., Hengeveld, B., & Banks, R. (2016). From Research Prototype to Research Product.** *Proceedings of CHI '16*, 2549–2561.
    DOI: 10.1145/2858036.2858167
    - "Research products" — fully functional prototypes deployed long-term. Extends prototyping from exploration to long-term field deployment.

19. **Wakkary, R., Odom, W., Hauser, S., Hertz, G., & Lin, H. (2015). Material Speculation: Actual Artifacts for Critical Inquiry.** *Proceedings of Aarhus '15*, Article 12.
    DOI: 10.7146/aahcc.v1i1.21299
    - Material speculation: functional prototypes as sites of critical inquiry. Prototyping at its most ambitious — actual artifacts in everyday life.

20. **Ratto, M. (2011). Critical Making: Conceptual and Material Studies in Technology and Social Life.** *The Information Society*, 27(4), 252–260.
    DOI: 10.1080/01972243.2011.583819
    - Critical making: prototyping as critical thinking. The act of building is the site of critical inquiry.

21. **Dow, S. P., Glassco, A., Kass, J., Schwarz, M., Schwartz, D. L., & Klemmer, S. R. (2010). Parallel Prototyping Leads to Better Design Results, More Divergence, and Increased Self-Efficacy.** *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 17(4), Article 18.
    DOI: 10.1145/1879831.1879836
    - Empirical evidence that parallel (vs. serial) prototyping produces better outcomes; prototyping strategy affects knowledge generation.

22. **Elsden, C., Chatting, D., Durrant, A. C., Garbett, A., Nissen, B., Vines, J., & Kirk, D. S. (2017). On Speculative Enactments.** *Proceedings of CHI '17*, 5386–5399.
    DOI: 10.1145/3025453.3025503
    - Prototyping future experiences through speculative enactments; prototypes as props for participatory speculation.

## Prototyping and Intermediate Knowledge

23. **Gaver, W., & Bowers, J. (2012). Annotated Portfolios.** *Interactions*, 19(4), 40–49.
    DOI: 10.1145/2212877.2212889
    - Annotated portfolios: collections of prototypes become vehicles for articulating design knowledge. The portfolio, not any single prototype, produces insight.

24. **Pierce, J. (2014). On the Presentation and Production of Design Research Artifacts in HCI.** *Proceedings of DIS '14*, 735–744.
    DOI: 10.1145/2598510.2598525
    - How prototypes/artifacts are presented in research publications; documentation is part of the prototype's epistemic function.

25. **Dorst, K. (2015). Frame Innovation: Create New Thinking by Design.** MIT Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-262-32431-1
    URL: https://direct.mit.edu/books/book/2239/Frame-InnovationCreate-New-Thinking-by-Design
    - Frame creation as a design thinking method. Prototyping plays a role in testing and developing new frames for complex problems.

26. **Cross, N. (2001). Designerly Ways of Knowing: Design Discipline versus Design Science.** *Design Issues*, 17(3), 49–55.
    DOI: 10.1162/074793601750357196
    - Design has its own ways of knowing; prototyping is the material practice through which designerly knowledge is produced.

## Recent Developments (2020–2026)

27. **Mueller, F., Semertzidis, N., et al. (2023). Toward Understanding the Design of Intertwined Human–Computer Integrations.** *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 30(3).
    DOI: 10.1145/3590766
    - Human-computer integration through bodily prototyping; bodies and technologies as intertwined design materials.

28. **Stolterman, E., & Wiberg, M. (2010). Concept-Driven Interaction Design Research.** *Human–Computer Interaction*, 25(2), 95–118.
    DOI: 10.1080/07370020903586696
    - Concept-driven approach: constructing concepts through prototyping generates theoretical knowledge.

29. **Wakkary, R., et al. (2024). Unmaking and HCI: Techniques, Technologies, Materials, and Philosophies beyond Making.** *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 31(6).
    DOI: 10.1145/3689047
    - "Unmaking" extends the prototyping paradigm: disassembly, decay, and biodegradation as research methods, complementing making-as-knowing.

30. **Brown, B., Dow, S., Ju, W., & Mackay, W. (2014). Projection, Place, and Point-of-View in Research Through Design.** In *The SAGE Handbook of Digital Technology Research*, 270–285. SAGE.
    DOI: 10.4135/9781446282229.n19
    - How prototyping in RtD involves projection (imagining futures), place (situated context), and point-of-view (researcher perspective).

## Connections to Other Lineages

- **Constructionism** (foundational): Making artifacts generates learning and knowledge — prototyping is constructionism in action
- **Research through Design** (primary): Prototyping is the material practice through which RtD produces knowledge
- **Material Speculation**: Takes prototyping to its extreme: fully functional "research products" in everyday settings
- **Soma Design** (Höök): Body as prototype — extends prototyping from external artifacts to lived bodily experience
- **Critical Making** (Ratto): Prototyping as critical thinking about technology and society
- **HCD to More-than-Human**: Prototyping things that develop their own agencies; "unmaking" as complement to making

## Summary

Prototyping as research was articulated through Houde & Hill's (1997) taxonomy, Buchenau & Suri's (2000) experience prototyping, and Lim et al.'s (2008) filtering framework. In design research, prototypes are not pre-products but epistemological instruments that generate knowledge through material engagement. The practice connects to constructionism (making = knowing), RtD (design practice as research), and material speculation (deployed artifacts as critical inquiry).

Key developments include Höök's (2018) extension of prototyping to the body through soma design, Odom et al.'s (2016) "research products" that extend prototype lifespans, and the emergence of "unmaking" (Wakkary et al., 2024) as a complement to prototyping's making-centered epistemology. The trajectory shows prototyping expanding from a design step into a fully epistemic practice — from what prototypes look like, to what they feel like (experience prototyping), to what they mean for living with (research products), to what happens when they come apart (unmaking).
