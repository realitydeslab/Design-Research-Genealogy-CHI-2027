## Foundational Works

1. **Giaccardi, E., Cila, N., Speed, C., & Caldwell, M. (2016). Thing Ethnography: Doing Design Research with Non-Humans.** *Proceedings of DIS '16*, 377–387.
   DOI: 10.1145/2901790.2901905
   - The foundational paper. Proposed thing ethnography as a method for design research, challenging anthropocentric assumptions. Demonstrated how things can participate as research instruments.

2. **Giaccardi, E., Speed, C., Cila, N., & Caldwell, M. L. (2016). Things as Co-Ethnographers: Implications of a Thing Perspective for Design and Anthropology.** In R. C. Smith et al. (Eds.), *Design Anthropological Futures*, Bloomsbury, 235–248.
   DOI: 10.5040/9781474280617.ch-015
   - Proposed that equipped with software and sensors, things can access fields, data and perspectives that human ethnographers cannot. Argued for "things as co-ethnographers."

3. **Cila, N., Giaccardi, E., Tynan-O'Mahony, F., Speed, C., & Caldwell, M. (2015). Thing-Centered Narratives: A Study of Object Personas.** *Proceedings of the 3rd Seminar on Research through Design*.
   URL: https://figshare.com/articles/conference_contribution/Thing-Centered_Narratives/1327245
   - Early work exploring narratives from a thing's perspective; object personas as a method.

## Theoretical Foundations

4. **Latour, B. (2005). Reassembling the Social: An Introduction to Actor-Network-Theory.** Oxford University Press.
   DOI: 10.1093/oso/9780199256044.001.0001
   - ANT as theoretical grounding: nonhuman actors have agency in networks. Thing ethnography operationalizes this for design research.

5. **Verbeek, P.-P. (2005). What Things Do: Philosophical Reflections on Technology, Agency, and Design.** Penn State University Press.
   ISBN: 978-0-271-02539-4
   - Postphenomenological analysis of how things mediate human experience. Provides philosophical justification for taking a thing's perspective seriously.

6. **Harman, G. (2018). Object-Oriented Ontology: A New Theory of Everything.** Pelican Books.
   ISBN: 978-0-241-26921-4
   - OOO as philosophical context: objects exist independently of human perception. Relevant to thing ethnography's premise.

7. **Coulton, P., & Lindley, J. (2019). More-Than Human Centred Design: Considering Other Things.** *The Design Journal*, 22(4), 463–481.
   DOI: 10.1080/14606925.2019.1614320
   - Applied Object-Oriented Ontology to design; provided theoretical framework for considering things' perspectives. Bridges thing ethnography to design fiction.

8. **Giaccardi, E., & Redström, J. (2020). Technology and More-Than-Human Design.** *Design Issues*, 36(4), 33–44.
   DOI: 10.1162/desi_a_00612
   - Argued for a more-than-human orientation that takes seriously the agency of technologies; thing ethnography as a key method within this orientation.

## Co-Performance and Practice Theory

9. **Kuijer, L., & Giaccardi, E. (2018). Co-performance: Conceptualizing the Role of Artificial Agency in the Design of Everyday Life.** *Proceedings of CHI '18*, Article 125.
   DOI: 10.1145/3173574.3173699
   - Introduced "co-performance" — how agency is configured between humans and nonhumans in social practice. Extends thing ethnography through practice theory.

10. **Redström, J., & Wiltse, H. (2019). Changing Things: The Future of Objects in a Digital World.** Bloomsbury.
    DOI: 10.5040/9781350004900
    - Analyzed how digital technology transforms the nature of designed things; things are no longer stable objects but fluid computational entities.

## Thing-Centered Design Research

11. **Chang, W.-W., Giaccardi, E., Chen, L.-L., & Liang, R.-H. (2017). "Interview with Things": A First-thing Perspective to Understand the Scooter's Everyday Socio-material Network in Taiwan.** *Proceedings of DIS '17*, 1001–1012.
    DOI: 10.1145/3064663.3064717
    - Applied thing ethnography in practice: "interviewing" scooters to understand urban mobility networks.

12. **Wakkary, R., Oogjes, D., Hauser, S., Lin, H., Cao, C., Ma, L., & Duel, T. (2017). Morse Things: A Design Inquiry into the Gap of Things.** *Proceedings of CHI '17*, 4218–4227.
    DOI: 10.1145/3025453.3025734
    - Design research exploring communication between things without human mediation. Exemplar of thing-centered design.

13. **Oogjes, D., & Wakkary, R. (2022). Weaving Stories: Toward Repertoires for Designing Things.** *Proceedings of CHI '22*, Article 406.
    DOI: 10.1145/3491102.3501930
    - Extended thing-centered design through narrative approaches to designing for/with things.

14. **Speed, C., & Oberlander, J. (2016). Designing from, with and by Data: Introducing the Ablative Framework.** *Proceedings of DRS '16*.
    DOI: 10.21606/drs.2016.433
    - Framework for data-driven design that accounts for data's own agency; related to thing ethnography's interest in nonhuman data generation.

15. **Rozendaal, M. C., Boon, B., & Kaptelinin, V. (2019). Objects with Intent: Designing Everyday Things as Collaborative Partners.** *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 26(4), Article 26.
    DOI: 10.1145/3325480
    - Extended thing-centered thinking to collaborative design; things as partners with intent.

## Ethnographic Traditions Extended

16. **Appadurai, A. (Ed.) (1986). The Social Life of Things: Commodities in Cultural Perspective.** Cambridge University Press.
    DOI: 10.1017/CBO9780511819582
    - Classic text on how objects have "social lives"; foundational for treating things as ethnographic subjects.

17. **Kopytoff, I. (1986). The Cultural Biography of Things: Commoditization as Process.** In A. Appadurai (Ed.), *The Social Life of Things*, 64–91. Cambridge University Press.
    DOI: 10.1017/CBO9780511819582.004
    - Proposed "cultural biographies" of objects; precursor to thing ethnography's interest in objects' experiences.

18. **Ingold, T. (2013). Making: Anthropology, Archaeology, Art and Architecture.** Routledge.
    DOI: 10.4324/9780203559055
    - Anthropological perspective on making and materials; things as active participants in world-making.

## Nonhuman Ethics and AI

19. **Reddy, A., Nicenboim, I., Pierce, J., & Giaccardi, E. (2021). Encountering Ethics through Design: A Workshop with Nonhuman Participants.** *AI & Society*, 36, 975–983.
    DOI: 10.1007/s00146-020-01088-7
    - Used interviews and role-play as tactics for exploring how ethics is encountered in human/nonhuman relations. Extends thing ethnography into ethical territory.

20. **Nicenboim, I., Giaccardi, E., Søndergaard, M. L. J., Reddy, A. V., Strengers, Y., Pierce, J., & Redström, J. (2020). More-Than-Human Design and AI: In Conversation with Agents.** *Companion Publication of DIS '20*, 397–400.
    DOI: 10.1145/3393914.3395912
    - Workshop exploring AI agents from a more-than-human design perspective. Used thing ethnography and material speculations as starting points.

21. **Giaccardi, E., & Fischer, G. (2008). Creativity and Evolution: A Metadesign Perspective.** *Digital Creativity*, 19(1), 19–32.
    DOI: 10.1080/14626260801992210
    - Early work connecting evolutionary thinking with design; precursor to thing-centered perspectives.

22. **Giaccardi, E., & Redström, J. (2020). Casting Things as Partners in Design: Toward a More-than-Human Design Practice.** In A. Toombs et al. (Eds.), *Relating to Things: Design, Technology and the Artificial*, Bloomsbury.
    DOI: 10.5040/9781350124288.ch-006
    - Positioned things as active partners in the design process, not passive objects of study.

    DOI: 10.1145/3685275
    - Proposes decentering tactics for designing with AI; extends thing ethnography's methods to AI agents.

## Connections to Other Lineages

- **More-than-Human Design** (parent): Thing ethnography is the primary methodological entry point for more-than-human design
- **Ethnography** (parent): Extends ethnographic methods by decentering the human observer
- **Anticipatory Ethnography**: Shares the impulse to study from non-standard perspectives (things vs. futures)
- **IoT/Connected Objects**: Connected devices make thing ethnography practically feasible through sensor data
- **Material Speculation**: Both engage with actual artifacts, but thing ethnography studies existing things while material speculation creates new ones
- **Actor-Network Theory**: Theoretical foundation; thing ethnography operationalizes ANT's symmetry principle
- **Participatory Design**: Thing ethnography extends PD's democratic impulse to nonhuman participants

## Summary

Thing Ethnography was proposed by Giaccardi, Cila, Speed, and Caldwell (2016) as a method for doing design research with non-humans. By imagining or instrumenting things' perspectives — through sensor data, object personas, or "interviews with things" — it reveals interconnections between people, objects, and infrastructures invisible to human-centered ethnography. The method operationalizes theoretical commitments from ANT (Latour), postphenomenology (Verbeek), and OOO (Harman/Coulton) into practical research tools.

The lineage has expanded significantly since its founding. Kuijer & Giaccardi's (2018) "co-performance" concept grounded thing ethnography in practice theory, while Redström & Wiltse's (2019) "changing things" articulated the ontological instability of digital objects that thing ethnography must contend with. The ethics of nonhuman participation was explored by Reddy et al. (2021), and the intersection with AI design has been a major recent focus.

The most significant recent development is the integration of thing ethnography with AI design (2020–2024). Nicenboim et al.'s work on "More-than-human Design and AI" (DIS 2020), "Decentering Through Design" (HCI 2024), and "Unmaking-with AI" (TOCHI 2024) represents the current frontier: extending thing ethnography's decentering methods to AI agents that are neither traditional "things" nor fully autonomous actors. This positions thing ethnography as a critical methodology for the age of AI — not just studying objects, but studying the entangled agencies of humans, things, and algorithms.
