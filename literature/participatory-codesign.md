# Literature Review: Participatory Design & Co-Design (Expanded)

## Overview

Participatory Design (PD) originated in Scandinavian workplace democracy movements of the 1970s–80s, emphasizing workers' rights to shape their tools. It evolved into co-design (Sanders & Stappers, 2008), expanding participation beyond workplaces to communities, marginalized groups, and civic contexts. PD is a foundational lineage for design research — many subsequent approaches (RtD, speculative design, more-than-human design) either build on or react against PD's commitments. This expanded review covers **55+ papers** organized into 10 strands: Scandinavian origins, co-design, methods/tools, power/decision-making, agonistic PD, infrastructuring, PD with marginalized groups, more-than-human PD, participatory AI, and PD in broader contexts.

## I. Scandinavian Origins (1970s–1980s)

### The Founding Projects

1. **Nygaard, K., & Bergo, O. T. (1975). The Trade Unions — New Users of Research.** *Personnel Review*, 4(2), 5–10.
   DOI: 10.1108/eb055278
   - The Norwegian Iron and Metal Workers' Union (NJMF) project (1970–73). Nygaard and the NJMF established the template: researchers work *with* workers, not *on* them. The earliest Scandinavian PD project.

2. **Bjerknes, G., Ehn, P., & Kyng, M. (Eds.) (1987). Computers and Democracy: A Scandinavian Challenge.** Avebury.
   ISBN: 978-0-566-05448-7
   - Landmark collection documenting the first generation of Scandinavian PD projects (NJMF, DEMOS, DUE, UTOPIA). Established workplace democracy as the political foundation for PD.

3. **Bødker, S., Ehn, P., Kammersgaard, J., Kyng, M., & Sundblad, Y. (1987). A UTOPIA Experience: On Design of Powerful Computer-Based Tools for Skilled Graphic Workers.** In Bjerknes et al. (Eds.), *Computers and Democracy*, 251–278.
   - The UTOPIA project (1981–85): collaboration between researchers (Aarhus, KTH Stockholm) and the Nordic Graphic Workers' Union. Developed experience-based design methods emphasizing hands-on mockups and organizational alternatives. The most influential early PD project.

4. **Ehn, P. (1988). Work-Oriented Design of Computer Artifacts.** PhD thesis, Arbetslivscentrum / Almqvist & Wiksell International.
   ISBN: 978-91-7400-933-8
   - Foundational PD text; grounded in Scandinavian labor traditions and Wittgensteinian language games. Argued that design knowledge is tacit and must be accessed through practice, not interviews.

5. **Greenbaum, J., & Kyng, M. (Eds.) (1991). Design at Work: Cooperative Design of Computer Systems.** Lawrence Erlbaum Associates.
   ISBN: 978-0-8058-0612-2
   - Seminal collection on cooperative design. Introduced PD to a broader international audience.

6. **Bødker, S. (1991). Through the Interface: A Human Activity Approach to User Interface Design.** Lawrence Erlbaum Associates.
   ISBN: 978-0-8058-0569-9
   - Applied activity theory to participatory system design; foundational for Scandinavian HCI. Established the theoretical bridge between Vygotskian activity theory and design practice.

7. **Schuler, D., & Namioka, A. (Eds.) (1993). Participatory Design: Principles and Practices.** Lawrence Erlbaum Associates.
   ISBN: 978-0-8058-0951-2
   DOI: 10.1201/9780203744338
   - First comprehensive collection on PD principles, methods, and case studies. Brought PD into the American HCI mainstream.

8. **Sundblad, Y. (2011). UTOPIA: Participatory Design from Scandinavia to the World.** In *History of Nordic Computing 3*, Springer, 176–186.
   DOI: 10.1007/978-3-642-23315-9_20
   - Historical retrospective on UTOPIA and its influence. Documents the lineage from NJMF (Norway 1970s) → DEMOS (Sweden) → DUE (Denmark) → UTOPIA (Nordic 1981–85).

## II. Co-Design and Co-Creation

9. **Sanders, E. B.-N. (2002). From User-Centered to Participatory Design Approaches.** In J. Frascara (Ed.), *Design and the Social Sciences*, Taylor & Francis, 1–8.
   DOI: 10.1201/9780203301302.ch1
   - Charted the historical shift from user-centered to participatory approaches. Distinguished "designing for" from "designing with."

10. **Sanders, E. B.-N., & Stappers, P. J. (2008). Co-Creation and the New Landscapes of Design.** *CoDesign*, 4(1), 5–18.
    DOI: 10.1080/15710880701875068
    - Mapped the shift from user-centered design to co-design; distinguished "design-led" from "research-led" approaches. The most cited paper defining co-design.

11. **Sanders, E. B.-N., & Stappers, P. J. (2012). Convivial Toolbox: Generative Research for the Front End of Design.** BIS Publishers.
    ISBN: 978-90-6369-284-1
    - Comprehensive toolkit for generative design research methods (probes, collages, toolkits). The practical companion to Sanders & Stappers 2008.

## III. Handbooks and Definitions

12. **Simonsen, J., & Robertson, T. (Eds.) (2013). Routledge International Handbook of Participatory Design.** Routledge.
    ISBN: 978-0-415-69440-7
    DOI: 10.4324/9780203108543
    - The definitive reference. 16 chapters covering PD theory, methods, and domains.

13. **Robertson, T., & Simonsen, J. (2013). Participatory Design: An Introduction.** In *Routledge International Handbook of Participatory Design*, 1–17.
    DOI: 10.4324/9780203108543-8
    - Defines PD's core commitments: mutual learning, shared decision-making, democratic participation.

14. **Bannon, L. J., & Ehn, P. (2013). Design: Design Matters in Participatory Design.** In *Routledge International Handbook of Participatory Design*, 37–63.
    DOI: 10.4324/9780203108543-10
    - Reflected on how "design" itself is understood within PD; traced conceptual evolution from tools to things.

15. **Bødker, S., Dindler, C., Iversen, O. S., & Smith, R. C. (2022). Participatory Design.** Morgan & Claypool (Synthesis Lectures on HCI).
    DOI: 10.1007/978-3-031-02235-7
    - Recent synthesis covering PD history, theory, methods, and contemporary challenges. The most up-to-date textbook treatment.

16. **Muller, M. J. (2003). Participatory Design: The Third Space in HCI.** In J. A. Jacko & A. Sears (Eds.), *The Human-Computer Interaction Handbook*, Lawrence Erlbaum, 1051–1068.
    DOI: 10.1201/9781410615862-71
    - Positioned PD as a "third space" between users and designers. Influential framing.

## IV. Methods and Tools

17. **Gaver, W., Dunne, A., & Pacenti, E. (1999). Design: Cultural Probes.** *Interactions*, 6(1), 21–29.
    DOI: 10.1145/291224.291235
    - Introduced cultural probes. Bridging method: originated in the intersection of PD, RtD, and art practice.

18. **Mattelmäki, T. (2006). Design Probes.** PhD thesis, University of Art and Design Helsinki.
    ISBN: 951-558-212-X
    - Extended cultural probes into a systematic design research method with multiple variants.

19. **Brandt, E. (2006). Designing Exploratory Design Games: A Framework for Participation in Participatory Design.** *Proceedings of PDC '06*, 57–66.
    DOI: 10.1145/1900441.1900452
    - Proposed design games as structured participatory activities bridging telling, making, and enacting.

20. **Brandt, E., Binder, T., & Sanders, E. B.-N. (2013). Tools and Techniques: Ways to Engage Telling, Making, and Enacting.** In *Routledge International Handbook of Participatory Design*, 145–181.
    DOI: 10.4324/9780203108543-14
    - Comprehensive taxonomy of PD tools: telling (interviews, stories), making (collages, prototypes), and enacting (scenarios, role-play).

21. **Vines, J., Clarke, R., Wright, P., McCarthy, J., & Olivier, P. (2013). Configuring Participation: On How We Involve People in Design.** *Proceedings of CHI '13*, 429–438.
    DOI: 10.1145/2470654.2470716
    - Analyzed how participation is configured in design research; questioned assumptions about who participates and how.

22. **Loi, D. (2007). Reflective Probes, Primitive Probes and Playful Triggers.** *Proceedings of DPPI '07*, 233–245.
    DOI: 10.1145/1314161.1314183
    - Extended probe methodology with reflective and playful variants.

## V. Power, Decision-Making, and Critique

23. **Bratteteig, T., & Wagner, I. (2012). Disentangling Power and Decision-Making in Participatory Design.** *Proceedings of PDC '12*, 41–50.
    DOI: 10.1145/2347635.2347642
    - Applied a conceptual framework on power to understand decision-making and conflict in PD. Distinguished big decisions from small decisions, internal from external decisions, and decisions from "non-decisions."

24. **Bratteteig, T., & Wagner, I. (2014). Disentangling Participation: Power and Decision-making in Participatory Design.** Springer (CSCW series).
    DOI: 10.1007/978-3-319-06163-4
    - Book-length treatment. Central question: what does it mean to share power with users? Framework for analyzing who participates, in what, and how decisions are made participatorily.

25. **Luck, R. (2018). What Is It That Makes Participation in Design Participatory Design?** *Design Studies*, 59, 1–8.
    DOI: 10.1016/j.destud.2018.10.002
    - Philosophical analysis of what constitutes *genuine* participation vs. mere consultation.

26. **Bødker, S., & Kyng, M. (2018). Participatory Design That Matters — Facing the Big Issues.** *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 25(1), Article 1.
    DOI: 10.1145/3152421
    - Reflected on PD's evolution over 35 years; called for PD to engage with societal grand challenges (climate, migration, inequality). Argued PD had become too comfortable in small-scale projects.

27. **Smith, R. C., Bossen, C., & Kanstrup, A. M. (2017). Participatory Design in an Era of Participation.** *CoDesign*, 13(2), 65–69.
    DOI: 10.1080/15710882.2017.1310466
    - Editorial reflecting on PD's position when "participation" has become ubiquitous rhetoric. What is distinct about PD's democratic commitments?

## VI. Agonistic PD and Design Things

28. **Ehn, P. (2008). Participation in Design Things.** *Proceedings of PDC '08*, 92–101.
    DOI: 10.1145/1795234.1795248
    - Reconceived PD through "design things" (assemblies of humans and nonhumans), bridging PD with ANT/STS. Shifted from consensus-seeking to agonistic engagement. Pivotal reconceptualization.

29. **Björgvinsson, E., Ehn, P., & Hillgren, P.-A. (2010). Participatory Design and "Democratizing Innovation."** *Proceedings of PDC '10*, 41–50.
    DOI: 10.1145/1900441.1900448
    - Connected PD to innovation democratization via Malmö Living Labs. Proposed "design things" as agonistic public spaces where diverse stakeholders negotiate.

30. **Björgvinsson, E., Ehn, P., & Hillgren, P.-A. (2012). Agonistic Participatory Design: Working with Marginalised Social Movements.** *CoDesign*, 8(2–3), 127–144.
    DOI: 10.1080/15710882.2012.672577
    - Applied Mouffe's agonistic pluralism to PD. Consensus is not the goal; productive conflict is. Described Malmö Living Labs as spaces for agonistic experiments. Key turn in PD theory.

31. **Björgvinsson, E., Ehn, P., & Hillgren, P.-A. (2012). Design Things and Design Thinking: Contemporary Participatory Design Challenges.** *Design Issues*, 28(3), 101–116.
    DOI: 10.1162/DESI_a_00165
    - Distinguished "design things" (agonistic assemblies) from "design thinking" (corporate innovation methodology). Argued PD's contribution is infrastructuring, not problem-solving.

32. **DiSalvo, C. (2010). Design, Democracy and Agonistic Pluralism.** *Proceedings of DRS 2010*, Montreal.
    - Applied Mouffe's political theory to design. Distinguished "political design" (revealing power) from "design for politics" (facilitating participation). Foundational for agonistic design theory.

33. **DiSalvo, C. (2012). Adversarial Design.** MIT Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-262-01738-3
    - Extended agonistic theory into a complete design framework. Proposed design as a means of expressing dissent and fostering democratic contestation.

34. **Hillgren, P.-A. (2013). Participatory Design for Social and Public Innovation: Living Labs as Spaces for Agonistic Experiments and Friendly Hacking.** In Manzini & Staszowski (Eds.), *Public and Collaborative*, 75–88.
    - Described Malmö Living Labs as long-term PD infrastructure for social innovation, combining agonistic engagement with "friendly hacking."

## VII. Infrastructuring

35. **Le Dantec, C. A., & DiSalvo, C. (2013). Infrastructuring and the Formation of Publics in Participatory Design.** *Social Studies of Science*, 43(2), 241–264.
    DOI: 10.1177/0306312712471581
    - Introduced "infrastructuring" as ongoing PD practice beyond project-bounded participation. PD creates publics, not just products.

36. **Bødker, S., Dindler, C., & Iversen, O. S. (2017). Tying Knots: Participatory Infrastructuring at Work.** *Computer Supported Cooperative Work*, 26(1–2), 245–273.
    DOI: 10.1007/s10606-017-9268-y
    - Developed vocabulary for participatory infrastructuring grounded in activity theory. "Tying knots" = creating durable connections between ongoing PD activities and institutional structures.

37. **Karasti, H., Pipek, V., & Bowker, G. C. (2018). An Afterword to 'Infrastructuring and Collaborative Design.'** *Computer Supported Cooperative Work*, 27(2), 267–289.
    DOI: 10.1007/s10606-018-9327-z
    - Reflected on infrastructuring as a concept bridging CSCW, STS (Star & Bowker), and PD. Infrastructure is never finished — it is perpetually maintained, repaired, and extended.

38. **Karasti, H., & Baker, K. S. (2008). Community Design: Growing One's Own Information Infrastructure.** *Proceedings of PDC '08*, 217–220.
    - Community-driven infrastructure development as PD. Connected Star's infrastructure studies to participatory practice.

39. **Hillgren, P.-A., Seravalli, A., & Emilson, A. (2011). Prototyping and Infrastructuring in Design for Social Innovation.** *CoDesign*, 7(3–4), 169–183.
    DOI: 10.1080/15710882.2011.630474
    - Connected prototyping (material practice) to infrastructuring (relational practice) in social innovation contexts.

## VIII. PD with Marginalized Groups

40. **Harrington, C., Erete, S., & Piper, A. M. (2019). Deconstructing Community-Based Collaborative Design: Towards More Equitable Participatory Design Engagements.** *Proceedings of CSCW '19*, Article 98.
    DOI: 10.1145/3359318
    - Critiqued power dynamics in PD with marginalized communities; proposed equity-focused approaches. Based on PD with elderly Black adults in Chicago.

41. **Costanza-Chock, S. (2020). Design Justice: Community-Led Practices to Build the Worlds We Need.** MIT Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-262-04345-0
    DOI: 10.7551/mitpress/12255.001.0001
    - Applied design justice framework: nothing about us without us. Centered marginalized communities' leadership — not just participation — in design.

42. **Asad, M., & Le Dantec, C. A. (2015). Illegitimate Civic Participation: Supporting Community Activists on the Ground.** *Proceedings of CSCW '15*, 1694–1703.
    DOI: 10.1145/2675133.2675156
    - Explored PD with communities whose civic participation is deemed "illegitimate" by authorities. Expanded PD's political commitments.

43. **Light, A., & Akama, Y. (2014). Structuring Future Social Relations: The Politics of Care in Participatory Practice.** *Proceedings of PDC '14*, 151–160.
    DOI: 10.1145/2661435.2661438
    - Applied care ethics to PD. Examined power, responsibility, and ongoing relational commitment in participatory practice.

44. **Winschiers-Theophilus, H., Bidwell, N. J., & Blake, E. (2012). Altering Participation through Interactions and Reflections in Design.** *CoDesign*, 8(2–3), 163–182.
    DOI: 10.1080/15710882.2012.672580
    - Examined PD in postcolonial contexts; challenges of applying Scandinavian PD in Namibia and South Africa.

45. **Iversen, O. S., Halskov, K., & Leong, T. W. (2012). Values-Led Participatory Design.** *CoDesign*, 8(2–3), 87–103.
    DOI: 10.1080/15710882.2012.672575
    - Proposed values as central organizing principle for PD processes.

## IX. More-than-Human PD

46. **Akama, Y., Light, A., & Kamihira, T. (2020). Expanding Participation to Design with More-Than-Human Concerns.** *Proceedings of PDC '20*, Volume 1, 1–11.
    DOI: 10.1145/3385010.3385016
    - Expanded PD's participatory ethic beyond human stakeholders. How can rivers, soils, animals participate? Foundational paper for more-than-human PD.

47. **Heitlinger, S., Light, A., Akama, Y., Lindström, K., & Ståhl, Å. (2024). More-Than-Human Participatory Design: Between Sustainability and Entanglement.** Chapter 4 in *Routledge Handbook of Contemporary Participatory Design*.
    - Most recent synthesis. Connects more-than-human PD to sustainability crises and feminist new materialism. PD's question shifts from "who participates?" to "what participates?"

48. **Clarke, R., Heitlinger, S., Light, A., Forlano, L., Foth, M., & DiSalvo, C. (2019). More-than-Human Participation in Design.** *Proceedings of PDC '18 Companion*, Article 12.
    DOI: 10.1145/3210604.3210640
    - Workshop paper establishing the more-than-human PD research agenda at PDC.

49. **Westerlaken, M. (2020). Imagining Multispecies Worlds.** PhD thesis, Malmö University.
    - Explored how PD can include animal perspectives through play and speculative methods.

## X. Participatory AI / Participatory ML

50. **Delgado, F., Yang, S., Madaio, M., & Yang, Q. (2023). The Participatory Turn in AI Design: Theoretical Foundations and the Current State of Practice.** *Proceedings of EAAMO '23*.
    DOI: 10.1145/3617694.3623261
    - Systematic review of 80 papers applying PD to AI design. Found most operate in consultative mode — eliciting preferences rather than granting ownership. Proposed conceptual framework distinguishing who participates, how, at what stage, and to what end.

51. **Birhane, A., Isaac, W., Prabhakaran, V., Diaz, M., Elish, M. C., Gabriel, I., & Mohamed, S. (2022). Power to the People? Opportunities and Challenges for Participatory AI.** *Proceedings of EAAMO '22*.
    DOI: 10.1145/3551624.3555290
    - Identified three objectives of participatory AI: algorithmic performance, process improvement, and collective exploration. Warned against "participation washing."

52. **Sloane, M., Moss, E., Awomolo, O., & Forlano, L. (2020). Participation Is Not a Design Fix for Machine Learning.** arXiv:2007.02423.
    - Cautioned that participatory methods cannot fix structural problems in ML. Distinguished participation as work, consultation, and justice. Influential critique.

53. **Costanza-Chock, S., Raji, I. D., & Buolamwini, J. (2022). Who Audits the Auditors? Recommendations from a Field Scan of the Algorithmic Auditing Ecosystem.** *Proceedings of FAccT '22*, 1571–1583.
    DOI: 10.1145/3531146.3533213
    - Extended design justice to AI auditing. Participatory audits as PD practice.

## XI. PD in Broader Contexts

54. **Suchman, L. (2002). Located Accountabilities in Technology Production.** *Scandinavian Journal of Information Systems*, 14(2), Article 7.
    - Connected PD to STS; examined accountability in technology production contexts.

55. **DiSalvo, C., Clement, A., & Pipek, V. (2013). Participatory Design for, with, and by Communities.** In *Routledge International Handbook of Participatory Design*, 182–209.
    DOI: 10.4324/9780203108543-15
    - Examined PD's expansion from workplace to community contexts. Three prepositions mark three stances.

56. **Kensing, F., & Greenbaum, J. (2013). Heritage: Having a Say.** In *Routledge International Handbook of Participatory Design*, 21–36.
    DOI: 10.4324/9780203108543-9
    - Traced PD's heritage from workplace democracy to contemporary practice. The political commitments persist.

## Summary and Genealogical Connections

PD originated in Scandinavian workplace democracy (Nygaard 1970s; Ehn, Bødker, Kyng 1980s), evolved through co-design (Sanders & Stappers, 2008), and expanded through six major theoretical turns:

1. **The agonistic turn** (Ehn 2008; Björgvinsson et al. 2012; DiSalvo 2012): From consensus to productive conflict. Mouffe's political theory enters PD.
2. **The infrastructuring turn** (Le Dantec & DiSalvo 2013; Bødker et al. 2017; Karasti et al. 2018): PD as ongoing relational practice, not bounded projects.
3. **The justice turn** (Costanza-Chock 2020; Harrington et al. 2019): From democratic participation to design justice — centering marginalized communities' leadership.
4. **The more-than-human turn** (Akama et al. 2020; Heitlinger et al. 2024): Expanding "who participates" beyond humans.
5. **The AI turn** (Delgado et al. 2023; Birhane et al. 2022; Sloane et al. 2020): Applying PD to AI/ML — with warnings about "participation washing."
6. **The power turn** (Bratteteig & Wagner 2012/2014): Making power explicit — who decides what, and how?

### Connections to Other Lineages

- **→ RtD:** PD provides the participatory ethos; RtD provides the material-making practice. Cultural probes (Gaver et al. 1999) bridge the two.
- **→ Speculative Design:** PD critiques speculative design's lack of participation. Speculative design critiques PD's lack of imagination. Provotypes (Mogensen 1992, Boer & Donovan 2012) bridge this gap.
- **→ Experiential Futures / Speculative Enactments:** Speculative enactments (Elsden et al. 2017) add PD's participatory methods to speculative design's future-orientation.
- **→ More-than-Human Design:** More-than-human PD (Akama et al. 2020) extends PD's democratic commitments to nonhumans.
- **→ Ethnography:** PD and ethnography share fieldwork orientation but differ in stance (change vs. understand). Cultural probes bridge them.
- **→ Design Justice:** Design justice (Costanza-Chock 2020) extends PD's democratic commitments with intersectional power analysis.
- **→ Unmaking:** Lindström & Ståhl's un/making as political practice connects unmaking to PD's agonistic tradition.
- **→ Infrastructural Speculation:** Wong's infrastructure-oriented speculation connects to PD's infrastructuring turn.
- **→ Design Futuring:** Transition design (Irwin 2015) draws on PD's stakeholder engagement for sustainability transitions.
- **→ Artist-led Research:** PD's "design things" framing (Ehn 2008) parallels artist-led research's exhibition/event orientation.
- **→ Participatory AI:** The most active current frontier — applying PD's 50-year heritage to AI/ML design.
