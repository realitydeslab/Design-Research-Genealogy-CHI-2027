# Design Activism — Genealogy Lineage

## Overview

Design activism is a tradition within design research that mobilizes design's material, aesthetic, and participatory capacities toward political contestation, social justice, and systemic change. Its HCI lineage draws on social movement theory, agonistic political philosophy, feminist critique, and prefigurative politics. This file traces the foundational works, key HCI contributions, and recent extensions of design activism as it intersects with human-computer interaction.

## 1. Foundational Works

### Papanek, V. (1971). *Design for the Real World: Human Ecology and Social Change*. Pantheon Books.
- **DOI/URL:** ISBN 978-0-394-47036-7; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victor_Papanek
- **Key contribution:** Foundational polemic arguing that design must serve social and ecological needs rather than consumerism. Positioned design as a political tool and advocated for working with marginalized communities and developing countries.
- **Genealogy relevance:** The ur-text of socially responsible design; establishes the political-ethical imperative that all subsequent design activism builds upon.

### Fuad-Luke, A. (2009). *Design Activism: Beautiful Strangeness for a Sustainable World*. Earthscan/Routledge.
- **DOI/URL:** ISBN 978-1-84407-645-1; https://www.routledge.com/9781849713566
- **Key contribution:** First comprehensive academic study of design activism as a distinct practice. Maps contemporary expressions of design activism from 2000 onward, framing it as a dual project: catalyzing positive sustainability impacts and reinvigorating design praxis through participatory, co-futuring approaches.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Defines the conceptual territory of design activism; introduces the taxonomy of activist designers as facilitators, co-authors, co-creators, and "happeners."

### Julier, G. (2011). Political Economies of Design Activism and the Public Sector. *Nordes*, No. 4.
- **DOI/URL:** https://archive.nordes.org/index.php/n13/article/view/94
- **Key contribution:** Examines the economic and political conditions that sustain design activism, particularly in the context of shrinking public sectors. Argues that design activism has a historical pedigree as long as the design profession itself, from William Morris to contemporary urban regeneration.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Provides the political economy framing; connects design activism to public sector transformation and macro-level political shifts.

### Julier, G. (2013). From Design Culture to Design Activism. *Design and Culture*, 5(2), 215–236 (Guest Editor, Special Issue on Design Activism).
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.2752/175470813X13638640370814
- **Key contribution:** Guest-edited special issue theorizing the shift from design culture (descriptive/analytical) to design activism (interventionist/transformative). Situates design activism within neoliberal critique and the politics of everyday life.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Key theoretical bridge between design studies and activist practice; establishes design activism as a legitimate scholarly domain.

### Thorpe, A. (2012). *Architecture & Design versus Consumerism: How Design Activism Confronts Growth*. Earthscan/Routledge.
- **DOI/URL:** ISBN 978-1-84971-356-6; https://www.routledge.com/9781849713566
- **Key contribution:** Argues that design activism's primary target should be consumerism and growth ideology. Identifies five methods designers use for social change and examines how activist practices are financed. Connects design activism to steady-state economics and post-growth thinking.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Provides the anti-consumerist and post-growth dimension of design activism; links design to broader economic critique.

### Markussen, T. (2013). The Disruptive Aesthetics of Design Activism: Enacting Design Between Art and Politics. *Design Issues*, 29(1), 38–50.
- **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1162/DESI_a_00195
- **Key contribution:** Develops a framework of "disruptive aesthetics" for understanding how design activism operates between art and politics. Argues that design activism creates sensory disruptions that reveal gaps between what people do and what they feel, enabling political transformation through aesthetic experience.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Central theoretical framework connecting design activism to aesthetics and affect; distinguishes design activism from art activism and political design.

## 2. Adversarial and Contestational Design

### DiSalvo, C. (2012). *Adversarial Design*. MIT Press.
- **DOI/URL:** ISBN 978-0-262-01738-1; https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262528221/adversarial-design/
- **Key contribution:** Introduces "adversarial design" as a practice that uses designed artifacts to provoke political contestation. Grounded in Chantal Mouffe's agonistic pluralism, argues that design can create "spaces of agonism" that foster productive dissensus rather than consensus. Examines computational visualizations, therapy robots, and surveillance-circumventing objects.
- **Genealogy relevance:** The defining work for politically engaged design in HCI; establishes agonism as the key political-theoretical framework for design activism in computing.

### DiSalvo, C. (2016). Design and Prefigurative Politics. *The Journal of Design Strategies*, 8(1), 29–35.
- **DOI/URL:** https://www.carldisalvo.com/publications/
- **Key contribution:** Extends adversarial design into prefigurative politics—the idea that activist practices should embody the desired future in present actions. Bridges design, social movement theory, and HCI.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Links adversarial design to prefigurative politics, which becomes central to later HCI activism scholarship (Asad 2019).

### DiSalvo, C. (2022). *Design as Democratic Inquiry: Putting Experimental Civics into Practice*. MIT Press.
- **DOI/URL:** ISBN 978-0-262-54399-7; https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262543996/design-as-democratic-inquiry/
- **Key contribution:** Argues for "doing design otherwise" through experimental civics that contribute to local democracy. Counterpoint to grand narratives of design punditry; emphasizes care and renewal of democratic practice through design.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Evolution of DiSalvo's adversarial design toward more constructive, care-oriented democratic engagement; connects to participatory design's political roots.

### Critical Art Ensemble. (1996). *Electronic Civil Disobedience and Other Unpopular Ideas*. Autonomedia.
- **DOI/URL:** http://critical-art.net/
- **Key contribution:** Theorized electronic civil disobedience as a form of tactical media—using digital tools for political disruption, intervention, and education. CAE's work at the intersection of art, critical theory, technology, and political activism prefigures much of HCI's engagement with adversarial design.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Precursor to adversarial design and HCI activism; establishes tactical media as a design-adjacent activist practice.

### Raley, R. (2009). *Tactical Media*. University of Minnesota Press.
- **DOI/URL:** ISBN 978-0-8166-5143-3; https://muse.jhu.edu/pub/23/monograph/book/27677
- **Key contribution:** Critical exploration of new media art activism emerging from postindustrialism and neoliberal globalization. Analyzes tactical media groups' methods of disruption, intervention, and symbolic performance, arguing they mimic and subvert the operations of global capital.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Provides theoretical grounding for understanding digital activism as design practice; connects HCI to media studies and activist art.

## 3. HCI Activism and Social Justice

### Bardzell, S. (2010). Feminist HCI: Taking Stock and Outlining an Agenda for Design. *Proceedings of CHI 2010*, 1301–1310. ACM.
- **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1145/1753326.1753521
- **Key contribution:** Seminal paper arguing that feminism is a natural ally to interaction design through commitments to agency, fulfillment, identity, equity, empowerment, and social justice. Proposes both critique-based and generative contributions of feminism to HCI.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Foundational text for politically engaged HCI; opens the door for design activism within the CHI community by legitimizing feminist political commitments as design concerns.

### Bardzell, S., & Bardzell, J. (2011). Towards a Feminist HCI Methodology: Social Science, Feminism, and HCI. *Proceedings of CHI 2011*, 675–684. ACM.
- **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1145/1978942.1979041
- **Key contribution:** Develops methodological implications of feminist HCI, bridging social science methods with feminist theory and HCI practice. Extends Bardzell (2010) from agenda-setting to concrete methodological guidance.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Operationalizes feminist activism within HCI methodology; enables subsequent work on social justice-oriented design.

### Dombrowski, L., Harmon, E., & Fox, S. (2016). Social Justice-Oriented Interaction Design: Outlining Key Design Strategies and Commitments. *Proceedings of DIS 2016*, 656–671. ACM.
- **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1145/2901790.2901861
- **Key contribution:** Develops a social justice orientation for interaction design, identifying six dimensions of justice (transformation, recognition, reciprocity, enablement, distribution, accountability) and three commitments (to conflict, reflexivity, and personal ethics). Provides a practical framework for activist HCI.
- **Genealogy relevance:** The most cited framework for social justice in HCI design; directly operationalizes design activism for the DIS/CHI community.

### Fox, S., Asad, M., Lo, K., Dimond, J. P., Dombrowski, L. S., & Bardzell, S. (2016). Exploring Social Justice, Design, and HCI. *CHI EA 2016*, 3293–3300. ACM.
- **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1145/2851581.2856465
- **Key contribution:** Workshop paper bringing together CSCW/CHI scholars to theorize and enact social justice through design. Maps the emerging landscape of justice-oriented HCI research.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Community-building moment that consolidated the social justice turn in HCI; connects feminist HCI to broader activist design agenda.

### Asad, M. (2019). Prefigurative Design as a Method for Research Justice. *Proceedings of the ACM on Human-Computer Interaction*, 3(CSCW), Article 200.
- **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1145/3359302
- **Key contribution:** Introduces prefigurative design as a framework for translating abstract justice concepts into concrete research practices. Drawing on social movement theory, proposes three phases: envisioning, acting presently, and building counter-institutions. Argues for design research as inherently political.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Key bridge between social movement theory and HCI methodology; operationalizes prefigurative politics for design research, extending DiSalvo's theoretical work.

### Keyes, O., Hoy, J., & Drouhard, M. (2019). Human-Computer Insurrection: Notes on an Anarchist HCI. *arXiv:1908.06167*.
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.1908.06167
- **Key contribution:** Proposes an explicitly anarchist political vision for HCI grounded in "emancipatory autonomy"—aimed at dismantling all oppressive systems through suspicion of imbalanced power distributions. Outlines principles for creating prefigurative counterpower.
- **Genealogy relevance:** The most radical political articulation within HCI; pushes design activism beyond reform toward systemic transformation; influential framing for subsequent activist HCI work.

### Khovanskaya, V., Dombrowski, L., Harmon, E., Korn, M., Light, A., Stewart, M., & Voida, A. (2018). Designing Against the Status Quo. *DIS 2018 Companion*.
- **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1145/3197391.3197393
- **Key contribution:** Workshop exploring how design can actively work against existing power structures rather than merely within them. Extends social justice-oriented design by centering structural critique.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Connects Dombrowski et al.'s framework to broader activist ambitions; positions design as resistance rather than accommodation.

## 4. Design Justice and Intersectional Activism

### Costanza-Chock, S. (2020). *Design Justice: Community-Led Practices to Build the Worlds We Need*. MIT Press.
- **DOI/URL:** ISBN 978-0-262-04345-8; https://designjustice.mitpress.mit.edu/
- **Key contribution:** Foundational text of the Design Justice movement. Demonstrates how universalist design principles erase intersectionally disadvantaged groups under the "matrix of domination" (white supremacy, heteropatriarchy, ableism, capitalism, settler colonialism). Documents community-led design practices grounded in social movements. Emerges from the Design Justice Network (founded 2016).
- **Genealogy relevance:** The most comprehensive integration of design activism with intersectional feminist theory, disability justice, and decolonial thought; provides both theoretical framework and practical principles for activist design.

### Asad, M., Dombrowski, L., Costanza-Chock, S., Erete, S. L., & Harrington, C. N. (2019). Academic Accomplices: Practical Strategies for Research Justice. *DIS 2019 Companion*.
- **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1145/3301019.3320002
- **Key contribution:** Develops practical strategies for researchers to act as "accomplices" (not just allies) in justice work, extending prefigurative design into academic institutional practice.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Bridges individual design activism to institutional transformation; connects HCI activism to broader movements for research justice.

## 5. Repair, Maintenance, and Unmaking as Activism

### Jackson, S. J. (2014). Rethinking Repair. In T. Gillespie, P. J. Boczkowski, & K. A. Foot (Eds.), *Media Technologies: Essays on Communication, Materiality, and Society*, 221–240. MIT Press.
- **DOI/URL:** https://sjackson.infosci.cornell.edu/RethinkingRepairPROOFS(reduced)Aug2013.pdf
- **Key contribution:** Proposes "broken world thinking"—taking erosion, breakdown, and decay rather than novelty and progress as starting points for understanding technology. Argues that repair is both a material practice and a political act, revealing the fragility of sociotechnical systems and the care labor that sustains them.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Foundational reorientation from innovation to maintenance; connects to design activism by positioning repair as resistance to planned obsolescence and consumerism.

### Jackson, S. J., & Kang, L. (2014). Breakdown, Obsolescence and Reuse: HCI and the Art of Repair. *Proceedings of CHI 2014*. ACM.
- **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1145/2556288.2557332
- **Key contribution:** Explores posthumanist alternatives to HCI creativity through theoretical, ethnographic, and building work focused on repair. Demonstrates how repair practices reveal novel forms of knowledge and innovation.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Operationalizes broken world thinking for HCI; connects repair activism to broader design research methodology.

### Rosner, D. K., Jackson, S. J., Hertz, G., Houston, L., & Rangaswamy, N. (2013). Reclaiming Repair: Maintenance and Mending as Methods for Design. *CHI 2013 Workshop*.
- **DOI/URL:** http://faculty.washington.edu/dkrosner/repair/index.html
- **Key contribution:** Workshop proposing repair and maintenance as fundamental yet undervalued design methods. Positions mending as innovation and as acts of sustaining against attrition.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Establishes repair as a design research methodology; connects to activism through the politics of care and sustainability.

## 6. Climate, Decolonial, and Refusal Design Activism (2020–2026)

### Light, A. (2022). Ecologies of Subversion—Troubling Interaction Design for Climate Care. *ACM Interactions*, 29(1), 34–38.
- **DOI/URL:** https://interactions.acm.org/archive/view/january-february-2022/ecologies-of-subversion
- **Key contribution:** Argues for subversive interaction design in the context of climate crisis. Draws on queering and troubling as creative deviation, connecting guerrilla tactics with activists to climate care through design.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Extends design activism into climate activism within HCI; bridges feminist/queer theory with environmental politics.

### Soden, R., Agrawaal, T. S., Lord, A., et al. (2025). Climate Data Practices: A Research Approach for HCI and Climate Justice. *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*.
- **DOI/URL:** https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/ecfcf9419f28a30628b13fe86c9a2cf47b82cbf1
- **Key contribution:** Introduces "climate data practices" as a lens for HCI research toward social and environmental justice, located at the intersection of critical data studies and practice theory. Based on collaborative study with activists, city staff, and NGOs.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Most recent articulation of climate activism within HCI; connects data practices to justice frameworks.

### Garcia, J. A., et al. (2022). Decolonial Pathways: Our Manifesto for a Decolonizing Agenda in HCI Research and Design. *INTERACT 2021*, Lecture Notes in Computer Science. Springer.
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-030-85607-6_8
- **Key contribution:** Offers five "decolonial pathways" for HCI: understanding, reconsidering, changing, expanding, and reflecting. A manifesto for navigating the challenge of embracing different knowledge systems without othering.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Key text for decolonial design activism in HCI; connects to participatory design's political roots while centering Global South perspectives.

### Zong, J., et al. (2024). Data Refusal from Below: A Framework for Understanding, Evaluating, and Envisioning Refusal as Design. *ACM Journal on Responsible Computing*.
- **DOI/URL:** https://data-and-design.org/publications/data-refusal/
- **Key contribution:** Introduces a framework for "data refusal from below"—writing from the standpoint of people who refuse rather than institutions seeking compliance. Characterizes refusal across four facets: autonomy, time, power, and cost. Argues refusal is an act of design.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Positions refusal as a design activism practice; connects feminist and indigenous scholarship to HCI; extends adversarial design into the era of AI and big data.

### D'Ignazio, C. (2022). Co-Designing for Counterdata Science. In *Counting Feminicide: Data Feminism in Action*. MIT Press.
- **DOI/URL:** https://mitpressonpubpub.mitpress.mit.edu/pub/cf-chap7/release/2
- **Key contribution:** Case study in participatory design for counterdata science, developing tools to support activists' informatic practices against feminicide. Demonstrates how HCI design can serve liberation movements through co-design with affected communities.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Exemplifies design activism at the intersection of data feminism, counterdata science, and community-led tool development.

### Dogan, A. L., Lin, H., & Kotut, L. (2025). "Down to Earth": Design Considerations for AI for Sustainability from the Environmental and Climate Movement. *Proceedings of DIS 2025*. ACM.
- **DOI/URL:** https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3715336.3735734
- **Key contribution:** Identifies design considerations for AI sustainability tools based on engagement with climate movement advocates. Argues that designing for the climate crisis requires a climate justice orientation, not technosolutionism.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Latest work connecting AI design, climate activism, and justice frameworks in HCI.

## 7. Mutual Aid, Solidarity, and Civic Technology

### Spade, D. (2020). Solidarity Not Charity: Mutual Aid for Mobilization and Survival. *Social Text*, 38(1), 131–151.
- **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1215/01642472-7971139
- **Key contribution:** Theorizes mutual aid as a political practice distinct from charity, emphasizing collective solidarity, horizontal organization, and systemic critique. Written in the context of COVID-19 but drawing on long histories of mutual aid in marginalized communities.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Key theoretical reference for mutual aid platform design in HCI; distinguishes activist solidarity from charity-oriented design.

### Soden, R., & Owen, E. (2021). Dilemmas in Mutual Aid: Lessons for Crisis Informatics from an Emergent Community Response to the Pandemic. *Proceedings of the ACM on Human-Computer Interaction*, 5(CSCW2).
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1145/3479538
- **Key contribution:** Analyzes dilemmas faced by mutual aid groups during COVID-19, drawing lessons for crisis informatics and community technology design.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Connects mutual aid activism to CSCW/HCI; demonstrates tensions between horizontal organizing principles and platform design.

### Qadri, R. (2021). What's in a Network? Infrastructures of Mutual Aid for Digital Platform Workers during COVID-19. *Proceedings of the ACM on Human-Computer Interaction*, 5(CSCW2).
- **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1145/3479563
- **Key contribution:** Examines how informal mutual aid networks mediated precarity for gig workers in Jakarta during COVID-19. Shows how informal labor networks function as essential infrastructure of support, carrying lessons for building solidarity among distributed workforces.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Extends mutual aid activism to platform labor and Global South contexts; connects CSCW to worker solidarity movements.

### Knearem, T., Jo, J., Alliyu, O., & Carroll, J. M. (2024). Solidarity not Charity! Empowering Local Communities for Disaster Relief during COVID-19 through Grassroots Support. *Computer Supported Cooperative Work (CSCW)*, Springer.
- **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10606-023-09484-5
- **Key contribution:** Studies mutual aid as community capacity for disaster preparedness, demonstrating how grassroots technology platforms enabled solidarity-based relief during COVID-19.
- **Genealogy relevance:** Most developed CSCW study of mutual aid platforms; connects community informatics to activist design.

## 8. Lineage Connections

### Connection to Participatory Design (Political Roots)
Design activism shares deep roots with Scandinavian participatory design (PD), which emerged from trade union collaborations in the 1970s–80s (Ehn, Kyng, Bødker). PD's original political commitment to worker empowerment and democratic decision-making is the direct ancestor of design activism's emphasis on community-led processes. Costanza-Chock (2020) explicitly builds on PD traditions while critiquing their insufficient attention to intersectionality.

### Connection to Critical Design
Critical design (Dunne & Raby, 2001; 2013) shares design activism's interest in using design to provoke reflection and challenge assumptions. However, design activism is distinguished by its commitment to *action* and *real-world change* rather than gallery-based speculation. Markussen (2013) theorizes this distinction through "disruptive aesthetics"—design activism operates between art and politics but is oriented toward transformation, not contemplation.

### Connection to Speculative Design
Speculative design's future-oriented methods inform design activism's "co-futuring" practices (Fuad-Luke 2009), but design activism insists on present-tense political engagement alongside future imagination. Light (2022) connects speculative and activist impulses through "ecologies of subversion."

### Connection to Design Justice
Design justice (Costanza-Chock 2020) is both a descendant and a radicalization of design activism. It adds intersectional analysis, centers marginalized communities as designers (not just beneficiaries), and connects to specific social movements (disability justice, racial justice, trans liberation). The Design Justice Network principles (2016) provide the most concrete operationalization of design activism.

### Connection to Unmaking
Unmaking (Pierce 2012; Murer et al. 2015) connects to design activism through shared critique of production-oriented design. Where activism confronts consumerism and growth (Thorpe 2012), unmaking questions the imperative to make at all. Both traditions challenge design's default orientation toward creation and novelty.
