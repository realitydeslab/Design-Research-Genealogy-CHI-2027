# Transition Design — Genealogy Lineage

## Overview

Transition Design is an emerging area of design research, practice, and education proposed by Terry Irwin, Gideon Kossoff, and Cameron Tonkinwise at Carnegie Mellon University's School of Design. It advocates design-led societal transitions toward more sustainable, equitable, and desirable long-term futures. Transition Design draws on sustainability transitions research, systems thinking, wicked problems theory, social innovation, and futures/foresight to address complex, interconnected "wicked problems" such as climate change, biodiversity loss, and inequality. Its intersection with HCI occurs primarily through Sustainable HCI (SHCI), Computing within Limits, collapse informatics, degrowth computing, and post-growth HCI.

## 1. Foundational Works

### Irwin, T. (2015). Transition Design: A Proposal for a New Area of Design Practice, Study, and Research. *Design and Culture*, 7(2), 229–246.
- **DOI:** 10.1080/17547075.2015.1051829
- **Contribution:** Foundational manifesto for Transition Design as a new area of design practice, study, and research. Proposes a framework for design-led societal transition toward more sustainable futures, arguing that new approaches are needed to address 21st-century wicked problems.
- **Relevance:** The origin paper of Transition Design as a named field; establishes the core framework (visions, theories of change, mindset/posture, new ways of designing).

### Irwin, T., Tonkinwise, C., & Kossoff, G. (2015). Transition Design: An Educational Framework for Advancing the Study and Design of Sustainable Transitions. Presented at the 6th International Sustainability Transitions (IST) Conference, Sussex.
- **URL:** https://www.academia.edu/15283122/
- **Contribution:** Argues that design and designers have a key role in transition management and sustainability transitions. Outlines areas of design focus relevant to sustainability transitions including design for service, social innovation, policy, and Transition Design.
- **Relevance:** Bridges Transition Design to the sustainability transitions research community; establishes educational foundations.

### Irwin, T., Tonkinwise, C., & Kossoff, G. (2020). Transition Design: An Educational Framework for Advancing the Study and Design of Sustainable Transitions. *Cuadernos del Centro de Estudios en Diseño y Comunicación*, 105, 31–65.
- **DOI:** 10.18682/cdc.vi105
- **Contribution:** Expanded version detailing design history and theory underpinning Transition Design at Carnegie Mellon. Describes the launch of PhD and DDes programs in Transition Design.
- **Relevance:** Documents the institutionalization of Transition Design as an academic discipline with dedicated doctoral programs.

### Kossoff, G., Irwin, T., & Tonkinwise, C. (2015). Transition Design Overview. Carnegie Mellon University, School of Design.
- **URL:** https://www.academia.edu/13122242/Transition_Design_Overview
- **Contribution:** 32-page overview of Transition Design, its origins, influences, and expanded framework. Serves as a comprehensive introduction to the field.
- **Relevance:** Key reference document providing the fullest early articulation of Transition Design's scope and ambitions.

## 2. Theoretical Roots

### Rittel, H. W. J., & Webber, M. M. (1973). Dilemmas in a General Theory of Planning. *Policy Sciences*, 4(2), 155–169.
- **DOI:** 10.1007/BF01405730
- **Contribution:** Introduced the concept of "wicked problems" — problems that are inherently ill-defined, have no stopping rule, and resist definitive solutions. Distinguished wicked problems from "tame" problems solvable by science and engineering.
- **Relevance:** Foundational theoretical concept for Transition Design; wicked problems are the central problem framing that motivates the entire Transition Design approach.

### Geels, F. W. (2002). Technological Transitions as Evolutionary Reconfiguration Processes: A Multi-Level Perspective and a Case-Study. *Research Policy*, 31(8–9), 1257–1274.
- **DOI:** 10.1016/S0048-7333(02)00062-8
- **Contribution:** Introduced the Multi-Level Perspective (MLP) on socio-technical transitions, distinguishing three analytical levels: niches, regimes, and landscapes. Shows how transitions emerge through interactions between these levels.
- **Relevance:** Core theoretical framework that Transition Design draws upon for understanding how systemic change occurs in socio-technical systems.

### Geels, F. W. (2011). The Multi-Level Perspective on Sustainability Transitions: Responses to Seven Criticisms. *Environmental Innovation and Societal Transitions*, 1(1), 24–40.
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.eist.2011.02.002
- **Contribution:** Defends and elaborates the MLP framework against criticisms, clarifying its ontological positioning and analytical strengths for studying sustainability transitions.
- **Relevance:** Provides refined understanding of the transitions theory that underpins Transition Design's systemic approach.

### Geels, F. W. (2019). Socio-Technical Transitions to Sustainability: A Review of Criticisms and Elaborations of the Multi-Level Perspective. *Current Opinion in Environmental Sustainability*, 39, 187–201.
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.cosust.2019.06.009
- **Contribution:** Comprehensive review of MLP developments, criticisms, and elaborations including agency, power, politics, and demand-side dynamics.
- **Relevance:** Most recent major review of the transitions framework informing Transition Design thinking.

### Manzini, E. (2015). *Design, When Everybody Designs: An Introduction to Design for Social Innovation*. MIT Press.
- **ISBN:** 978-0-262-02860-8
- **Contribution:** Distinguishes between "diffuse design" (performed by everybody) and "expert design" (performed by trained designers). Maps how design experts can trigger and support meaningful social changes through collaborative approaches.
- **Relevance:** Key influence on Transition Design's emphasis on social innovation, participatory approaches, and design's role in facilitating community-led change. Manzini's DESIS network directly informs Transition Design practice.

### Escobar, A. (2018). *Designs for the Pluriverse: Radical Interdependence, Autonomy, and the Making of Worlds*. Duke University Press.
- **DOI:** 10.1215/9780822371816
- **Contribution:** Proposes "autonomous design" that eschews commercial and modernizing aims in favor of collaborative, place-based approaches. Channels design's world-making capacity toward justice and Earth. Explicitly develops "design for transitions" as a framework.
- **Relevance:** Major theoretical influence on Transition Design, especially its decolonial, pluriversal, and relational dimensions. Escobar directly engages with and extends Transition Design in Chapter 5.

### Capra, F., & Luisi, P. L. (2014). *The Systems View of Life: A Unifying Vision*. Cambridge University Press.
- **ISBN:** 978-1-107-01136-6
- **Contribution:** Synthesizes systems thinking, complexity theory, and living systems theory into a coherent framework for understanding life at all scales.
- **Relevance:** Systems thinking is one of the four pillars of the Transition Design framework; this text is a primary resource for the "theories of change" component.

## 3. Transition Design Methods and Frameworks

### Irwin, T. (2018). The Emerging Transition Design Approach. *Cuadernos del Centro de Estudios de Diseño y Comunicación*, 73, 149–181.
- **DOI:** 10.18682/cdc.vi73.1043
- Also presented at DRS 2018: DOI 10.21606/drs.2018.210
- **Contribution:** Outlines the Transition Design Framework's three phases: (1) reframing the problem and future, (2) designing interventions, (3) waiting and observing change. Details practices for mapping wicked problems, identifying stakeholder conflicts, co-creating visions, and finding leverage points.
- **Relevance:** Most comprehensive methodological articulation of how Transition Design works in practice.

### Kossoff, G. (2019). Cosmopolitan Localism: The Planetary Networking of Everyday Life in Place. *Cuadernos del Centro de Estudios de Diseño y Comunicación*, 73, 51–66.
- **DOI:** 10.18682/cdc.vi73.1037
- **Contribution:** Proposes Cosmopolitan Localism as a Transition Design strategy: place-based communities networked at multiple scales from households through neighborhoods, cities, regions, and the planet. Introduces "Domains of Everyday Life" as nested, self-organizing networks.
- **Relevance:** Provides the visioning component of Transition Design — what sustainable, place-based futures might look like. Connects Transition Design to localism, bioregionalism, and Manzini's cosmopolitan localism.

### Irwin, T., & Kossoff, G. (2020). Teaching and Researching Transition Design. *Cuadernos del Centro de Estudios de Diseño y Comunicación*.
- **URL:** https://dspace.palermo.edu/ojs/index.php/cdc/article/download/6853/10818/
- **Contribution:** Describes the pedagogical approach to Transition Design at Carnegie Mellon, including case study methodology, the role of backcasting, stakeholder mapping, and long-horizon design thinking.
- **Relevance:** Documents how Transition Design methods are taught and applied, including connections to participatory design and futures/foresight practices.

### Wahl, D. C. (2016). *Designing Regenerative Cultures*. Triarchy Press.
- **ISBN:** 978-1-909470-77-4
- **Contribution:** Explores how to transition from degenerative to regenerative cultures through design. Frames Transition Design as "holistic science in action," connecting it to ecological design thinking, permaculture, and biomimicry.
- **Relevance:** Extends Transition Design toward regenerative paradigms; provides a bridge between Transition Design and ecological/regenerative design traditions.

## 4. HCI Connections: Sustainable HCI and Computing within Limits

### Blevis, E. (2007). Sustainable Interaction Design: Invention & Disposal, Renewal & Reuse. In *Proceedings of CHI 2007*, 503–512. ACM.
- **DOI:** 10.1145/1240624.1240705
- **Contribution:** Seminal paper establishing Sustainable Interaction Design (SID) as a central focus of interaction design. Proposes a rubric for understanding material effects of interaction design and principles to guide sustainable design.
- **Relevance:** Foundational paper for Sustainable HCI; opens the space that Transition Design later inhabits within the HCI community. First major argument that sustainability should be central to interaction design.

### Mankoff, J. C., Blevis, E., Borning, A., Friedman, B., Fussell, S. R., Hasbrouck, J., Woodruff, A., & Sengers, P. (2007). Environmental Sustainability and Interaction. In *CHI '07 Extended Abstracts*, 2121–2124. ACM.
- **DOI:** 10.1145/1240866.1240963
- **Contribution:** Workshop paper establishing the agenda for environmental sustainability in HCI, arguing that CHI must take sustainability challenges into consideration.
- **Relevance:** Co-authored with Blevis; marks the formal entry of sustainability into the CHI community's agenda.

### Tomlinson, B., Silberman, M. S., Patterson, D., Pan, Y., & Blevis, E. (2012). Collapse Informatics: Augmenting the Sustainability & ICT4D Discourse in HCI. In *Proceedings of CHI 2012*. ACM.
- **DOI:** 10.1145/2207676.2207770
- **Contribution:** Introduces "collapse informatics" — the study, design, and development of sociotechnical systems in the abundant present for use in a future of scarcity. Complements sustainable HCI with adaptation-oriented perspectives.
- **Relevance:** Shift from mitigation to adaptation in HCI sustainability thinking; directly relevant to Transition Design's long-horizon, scenario-based approach to systemic change.

### Tomlinson, B., Blevis, E., Nardi, B., Patterson, D. J., Silberman, M. S., & Pan, Y. (2013). Collapse Informatics and Practice: Theory, Method, and Design. *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 20(4), Article 24.
- **DOI:** 10.1145/2493432
- **Contribution:** Full treatment of collapse informatics as a research area within HCI. Develops theoretical framework and methodological approaches for designing sociotechnical systems for post-scarcity futures.
- **Relevance:** Extends the collapse informatics agenda with rigorous methodology; connects to Transition Design's concern with designing for radically different futures.

### Nardi, B., Tomlinson, B., Patterson, D. J., Chen, J., Pargman, D., Raghavan, B., & Penzenstadler, B. (2018). Computing within Limits. *Communications of the ACM*, 61(10), 86–93.
- **DOI:** 10.1145/3183582
- **Contribution:** Flagship article for the Computing within Limits research community. Argues that computing research must reckon with planetary limits relating to climate change, raw materials, and ecological boundaries.
- **Relevance:** Establishes the LIMITS community as the primary HCI venue engaging with sustainability transitions thinking; directly parallel to Transition Design's framing of planetary-scale challenges.

### Raghavan, B., & Pargman, D. (2017). Means and Ends in Human-Computer Interaction: Sustainability through Disintermediation. In *Proceedings of CHI 2017*. ACM.
- **DOI:** 10.1145/3025453.3025542
- **Contribution:** Proposes "disintermediation" as a unifying design rubric for Sustainable HCI, focusing on ecological limits, non-cornucopian design, and fundamental human needs.
- **Relevance:** Provides a practical design approach that bridges Sustainable HCI and transition thinking; challenges growth-oriented HCI assumptions.

### Cerratto Pargman, T., & Pargman, D. (2015). Rethinking Sustainability in Computing: From Buzzword to Non-Negotiable Limits. In *Proceedings of LIMITS 2015*.
- **URL:** https://computingwithinlimits.org/2015/papers/limits2015-cerratto-pargman.pdf
- **Contribution:** Introduces social ecology perspectives into Sustainable HCI, situating computing at the intersection of ecological, social, and economic limits.
- **Relevance:** Connects Transition Design's systems thinking orientation to HCI sustainability research.

### Friday, A. (2015). Designing for Transitions to Sustainable Lifestyles. *ACM Interactions*, 22(4).
- **DOI:** 10.1145/2743034
- **Contribution:** Early explicit connection between transitions thinking and interaction design, exploring how HCI can support transitions to sustainable lifestyles.
- **Relevance:** One of the earliest papers to explicitly link "transitions" language to HCI/interaction design practice.

## 5. Post-Growth and Degrowth Computing (2021–2025)

### Sharma, V., Kumar, N., & Nardi, B. (2023). Post-Growth Human–Computer Interaction. *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 30(5).
- **DOI:** 10.1145/3624981
- **Contribution:** Comprehensive articulation of post-growth philosophy for HCI, incorporating degrowth, post-development, and steady-state approaches. Proposes seven orientations for HCI: evaluate political economy, normalize redesign and undesign, nurture solidarities, recognize limits, cultivate critical pedagogy, participate in policy-making, and challenge HCI assumptions.
- **Relevance:** Most comprehensive HCI engagement with post-growth/degrowth thinking; directly extends the sustainability transitions agenda into HCI theory and practice.

### Sharma, V., et al. (2025). Advancing Post-Growth HCI. In *CHI 2025 Workshop*.
- **DOI:** 10.1145/3706599.3706731
- **Contribution:** Workshop nurturing the Post-Growth HCI Collective, exploring tangible pathways toward socio-ecologically just and sustainable technology-mediated futures.
- **Relevance:** Demonstrates the growing institutionalization of post-growth/transitions thinking within the CHI community.

### Sutherland, B. (2022). Strategies for Degrowth Computing. In *LIMITS '22: Workshop on Computing within Limits*.
- **URL:** https://computingwithinlimits.org/2022/papers/limits22-final-Sutherland.pdf
- **Contribution:** Applies the concept of degrowth directly to computing, proposing strategies for reducing material throughput in computing systems.
- **Relevance:** Makes explicit the connection between degrowth economics and computing/HCI design.

### Abbing, R. R. (2021). 'This is a Solar-Powered Website, Which Means It Sometimes Goes Offline': A Design Inquiry into Degrowth and ICT. In *LIMITS '21*.
- **URL:** https://computingwithinlimits.org/2021/papers/limits21-abbing.pdf
- **Contribution:** Practical design research applying degrowth principles to web development; demonstrates how LIMITS values translate into concrete design practice.
- **Relevance:** Exemplifies how transition/degrowth thinking produces actionable design alternatives in computing.

### Houston, L., Light, A., & Thornton, C. (2022). The Richness of Designing for Eco-Social Change: Creative Practice, Transformative Futures and Living within Limits. In *LIMITS '22*.
- **URL:** https://limits.pubpub.org/pub/rich
- **Contribution:** Reflects on limits framing in HCI on the 50th anniversary of *Limits to Growth*. Proposes "eco-social transformation" as a fusion of ecological and social sensibilities, arguing for abundance-based framings alongside limits.
- **Relevance:** Bridges LIMITS community with participatory/creative design practice and transitions thinking.

## 6. Design for Sustainability Transitions (DfST) — Bridging Design and Transitions

### Ceschin, F., & Gaziulusoy, İ. (2020). *Design for Sustainability: A Multi-level Framework from Products to Socio-technical Systems*. Routledge.
- **ISBN:** 978-1-138-31516-7
- **Contribution:** Comprehensive evolutionary framework for Design for Sustainability (DfS), mapping approaches across four innovation levels: product, product-service system, spatio-social, and socio-technical system. Includes dedicated chapter on "Design for Sustainability Transitions."
- **Relevance:** Provides the most complete mapping of where Transition Design sits within the broader DfS landscape; essential reference for understanding the field's evolution.

### Ceschin, F., & Gaziulusoy, İ. (2016). Evolution of Design for Sustainability: From Product Design to Design for System Innovations and Transitions. *Design Studies*, 47, 118–163.
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.destud.2016.09.002
- **Contribution:** Traces the evolution of design's response to sustainability from product-level eco-design to system-level transition design, proposing a framework synthesizing the field.
- **Relevance:** Key review paper showing the trajectory from green design → eco-design → product-service systems → systemic design → Transition Design.

### Gaziulusoy, İ., Veselova, E., Hodson, E., Berglund, E., Erdoğan Öztekin, E., Houtbeckers, E., Hernberg, H., Jalas, M., Fodor, K., & Ferreira, M. (2021). Design for Sustainability Transformations: A Prototype Research Agenda. *Strategic Design Research Journal*, 14(1).
- **DOI:** 10.4013/sdrj.2021.141.02
- **Contribution:** Proposes a research agenda for Design for Sustainability Transformations (DfST), situated at spatio-social, socio-technical, and socio-ecological-technical innovation levels.
- **Relevance:** Extends Transition Design toward transformation and socio-ecological-technical framing; reflects the field's maturation.

### Besana, N., Krogh, P. G., Petersen, M. G., & Spallazzo, D. (2024). Expanding the Concept of Sustainable Interaction Design: A Systematic Review. *Sustainability*, 16.
- **URL:** https://core.ac.uk/download/pdf/638784342.pdf
- **Contribution:** Systematic literature review of Sustainable Interaction Design since Blevis (2007), tracking how the concept has evolved across HCI.
- **Relevance:** Documents the trajectory from Blevis's foundational SID concept through to contemporary sustainability and transition approaches in HCI.

## 7. DRS Conference Tracks: Designing for Transitions

### Boehnert, J., Lockton, D., & Mulder, I. (2018). Editorial: Designing for Transitions. In *DRS 2018*, Limerick, Ireland.
- **DOI:** 10.21606/drs.2018.008
- **Contribution:** Established the first "Designing for Transitions" track at DRS, bringing together researchers working on design's role in systemic change.
- **Relevance:** Marks the formal recognition of transitions-oriented design research within the Design Research Society.

### Coops, F., Hummels, C., Dorst, K., Goldstein, B. E., Loorbach, D., & Gaziulusoy, İ. (2022). Designing for Transitions and Transformations. In *DRS 2022*, Bilbao.
- **DOI:** 10.21606/drs.2022.897
- **Contribution:** Conversation track exploring how design can evoke sustainable and just transitions, building on DRS 2018 foundations.
- **Relevance:** Demonstrates the growing community around transitions-oriented design research.

### Coops, F., Lockton, D., Gaziulusoy, İ., Tonkinwise, C., Boehnert, J., et al. (2024). Designing (for) Transitions and Transformations: Imagination, Climate Futures, and Everyday Lives. In *DRS 2024*, Boston.
- **DOI:** 10.21606/drs.2024.167
- **Contribution:** Track of 21 papers on design's role in transformative change. Moves toward establishing a DRS Special Interest Group on transitions.
- **Relevance:** Most recent major DRS engagement with Transition Design; shows the field's expansion and institutionalization.

### van der Bijl-Brouwer, M., Celik, S., de Koning, J., Nieuwborg, A., & Tromp, N. (2024). Systemic Design Reasoning for Societal Transitions. In *DRS 2024*, Boston.
- **DOI:** 10.21606/drs.2024.585
- **Contribution:** Introduces "systemic design reasoning" as a third perspective complementing systemic visualization and collective designing. Develops six principles for reasoning about societal transitions through design.
- **Relevance:** Connects systemic design methods to Transition Design's goals; advances the methodological toolkit.

## 8. Systemic Design and Sustainability in HCI

### Laetitia, et al. (2023). A Methodology and a Tool to Support the Sustainable Design of Interactive Systems. In *IHM 2023*.
- **URL:** https://enac.hal.science/hal-04088784/document
- **Contribution:** Proposes adapting systemic design methods to HCI, using causal loop diagrams for "quali-quantitative" modeling of sustainability in interactive system design.
- **Relevance:** Directly bridges systemic design (a close cousin of Transition Design) with HCI methods.

### Nardi, B. (2019). Design in the Age of Climate Change. *She Ji: The Journal of Design, Economics, and Innovation*, 5(1), 5–14.
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.sheji.2019.01.001
- **Contribution:** Urges the HCI community to fundamentally reconsider computing's role in the face of climate change, rather than merely optimizing current systems.
- **Relevance:** Key call-to-action connecting HCI researchers to the urgency of transitions and systemic change.

### Boehnert, J. (2018). Anthropocene Economics and Design: Heterodox Economics for Design Transitions. *She Ji*, 4(4), 355–374.
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.sheji.2018.10.002
- **Contribution:** Connects heterodox economics (ecological economics, degrowth) to design transitions, arguing that designers must engage with economic paradigm shifts.
- **Relevance:** Provides the economic theory foundations for Transition Design practice; bridges design and political economy.

## 9. Lineage Connections to Adjacent Fields

### Futures/Foresight
Transition Design draws heavily on futures and foresight methods, particularly **backcasting** (Robinson, 1990) and **visioning** practices. The Transition Design Framework's "visions" component explicitly uses long-horizon future visioning as a design tool. See also: *experiential-futures.md*, *foresight-design.md*, *design-futuring-exhaustive.md*.

### Systemic Design
Transition Design shares significant overlap with systemic design (Sevaldson, Jones, Birger). Both integrate systems thinking with design practice, but Transition Design emphasizes temporal scale and sustainability transitions more explicitly. See DRS 2024 systemic design tracks above.

### Speculative and Critical Design
Transition Design uses speculative methods for future visioning but differs from Speculative & Critical Design (Dunne & Raby) in its explicit commitment to actionable, community-based transition rather than gallery-based provocation. See: *speculative-critical.md*.

### Participatory Design
Transition Design's emphasis on stakeholder engagement, co-creation, and community-based design connects to participatory design traditions (Schuler & Namioka, 1993; Robertson & Simonsen, 2012). However, Transition Design extends PD's scope to systemic, multi-stakeholder, long-horizon challenges. See: *participatory-codesign.md*.

### Design for Social Innovation
Manzini's work on social innovation (2015) is a direct ancestor. Transition Design extends social innovation design toward longer time horizons and deeper systemic engagement. See also DESIS network contributions.

### Design Activism
Transition Design shares with design activism (Fuad-Luke, 2009; Markussen, 2013) a commitment to societal change, but frames this through transitions theory rather than direct political action. See: *design-activism.md*.
