# Literature Review: The Design Methods Movement (1960s–1980s)

## Overview

The Design Methods Movement is the deep root of all design research. Beginning with the 1962 Conference on Design Methods in London, it sought to make design systematic and scientific. The movement produced foundational concepts — wicked problems, designerly ways of knowing, reflective practice — that define the field's epistemological terrain. It also generated the backlash (including from its own founders) that led to Research through Design, participatory design, and practice-based approaches. Understanding this pre-history is essential for the genealogy: every lineage we map is either an extension of, or a reaction against, the design methods movement.

**Three phases:**
1. **Systematic methods (1962–1972):** Jones, Alexander, Archer, Simon — design as problem-solving
2. **Anti-method backlash (1971–1983):** Jones and Alexander reject their own movement; Rittel's "wicked problems"; Schön's reflective practitioner
3. **Design as discipline (1982–2001):** Cross, Buchanan, Frayling — design's own ways of knowing

## Phase 1: Systematic Design Methods (1962–1972)

### The 1962 Conference

1. **Jones, J. C., & Thornley, D. G. (Eds.) (1963). Conference on Design Methods.** Pergamon Press, Oxford.
   - The founding event: "Conference on Systematic and Intuitive Methods in Engineering, Industrial Design, Architecture and Communications," London, September 1962. Organized by Jones and Archer. Launched the design methods movement.

2. **Jones, J. C. (1970). Design Methods: Seeds of Human Futures.** John Wiley & Sons.
   ISBN: 978-0-471-44370-5
   - The movement's comprehensive text. Catalogued systematic design methods: brainstorming, morphological charts, decision matrices, systems analysis. The 1992 second edition added a remarkable preface where Jones distanced himself from the rationalist program.

3. **Alexander, C. (1964). Notes on the Synthesis of Form.** Harvard University Press.
   ISBN: 978-0-674-62751-2
   - Proposed mathematical decomposition of design problems into independent sub-problems. Hugely influential, then repudiated by Alexander himself in his 1971 preface: "I have disassociated myself from the field... I am very sorry that this book has been used... to justify methods which are, in my view, absurd."

4. **Archer, L. B. (1965). Systematic Method for Designers.** *Design* magazine series, reprinted by the Council of Industrial Design.
   - Proposed a step-by-step systematic design process. Most complete articulation of first-generation design methods.

5. **Simon, H. A. (1969). The Sciences of the Artificial.** MIT Press.
   ISBN: 978-0-262-69191-8
   - Defined design as the transformation of existing situations into preferred ones. Proposed "design science" — studying artificial (human-made) phenomena scientifically. "Satisficing" over optimizing. Foundational but contested: design researchers both cite and reject Simon's rationalism.

6. **Archer, L. B. (1979). Whatever Became of Design Methodology?** *Design Studies*, 1(1), 17–18.
   DOI: 10.1016/0142-694X(79)90023-1
   - Reflected on the first decade of *Design Studies* (which Archer co-founded). Asked what happened to the systematic methods program.

## Phase 2: The Anti-Method Backlash (1971–1983)

7. **Alexander, C. (1971). Preface to paperback edition of *Notes on the Synthesis of Form*.** Harvard University Press.
   - Alexander's famous recantation: rejected his own mathematical approach. "I feel it is important to say why I have disassociated myself from the field of design methods." Argued methods had become divorced from the reality of design practice.

8. **Jones, J. C. (1977). How My Thoughts About Design Methods Have Changed During the Years.** *Design Methods and Theories*, 11(1), 48–62.
   - Jones's own retreat from systematic methods. Advocated for intuitive, artistic approaches instead.

9. **Rittel, H. W. J., & Webber, M. M. (1973). Dilemmas in a General Theory of Planning.** *Policy Sciences*, 4(2), 155–169.
   DOI: 10.1007/BF01405730
   - Defined "wicked problems" — problems that resist systematic solution because they have no definitive formulation, no stopping rule, and every solution attempt changes the problem. Devastating critique of first-generation (Simonian) design methods. Among the most cited papers in design research.

10. **Rittel, H. W. J. (1972). On the Planning Crisis: Systems Analysis of the 'First and Second Generations.'** *Bedriftsøkonomen*, 8, 390–396.
    - Distinguished first-generation design methods (systematic, optimization-oriented) from second-generation (argumentative, stakeholder-inclusive). The second generation leads directly to participatory approaches.

11. **Schön, D. A. (1983). The Reflective Practitioner: How Professionals Think in Action.** Basic Books.
    ISBN: 978-0-465-06874-6
    - Countered "technical rationality" (Simon's model) with "reflection-in-action" — professionals think *through* practice, not before it. Hugely influential across design, education, and management. Provides the epistemological basis for Research through Design: if professionals learn by doing, then doing design produces knowledge.

## Phase 3: Design as Its Own Discipline (1982–2001)

12. **Cross, N. (1982). Designerly Ways of Knowing.** *Design Studies*, 3(4), 221–227.
    DOI: 10.1016/0142-694X(82)90040-0
    - Landmark paper arguing design is a "third culture" alongside science and humanities, with its own ways of knowing. Not applied science, not craft — design has its own epistemology grounded in making and material engagement.

13. **Cross, N. (2001). Designerly Ways of Knowing: Design Discipline Versus Design Science.** *Design Issues*, 17(3), 49–55.
    DOI: 10.1162/074793601750357196
    - Updated the 1982 argument. Distinguished "design science" (Simon's program) from "design discipline" (design's own knowledge). Rejected the scientization of design in favor of understanding design on its own terms.

14. **Cross, N. (2006). Designerly Ways of Knowing.** Springer.
    ISBN: 978-1-84628-300-4
    DOI: 10.1007/1-84628-301-9
    - Book-length treatment. Collected Cross's major papers on design epistemology, cognition, and methodology.

15. **Buchanan, R. (1992). Wicked Problems in Design Thinking.** *Design Issues*, 8(2), 5–21.
    DOI: 10.2307/1511637
    - Brought Rittel's wicked problems into design discourse. Proposed four orders of design: symbols, things, actions, environments. Argued design thinking is inherently about addressing wicked problems.

16. **Frayling, C. (1993). Research in Art and Design.** *Royal College of Art Research Papers*, 1(1), 1–5.
    - The taxonomy that launched Research through Design: research *into* art/design (historical/theoretical), research *through* art/design (using practice as method), research *for* art/design (producing artifacts). Brief but enormously influential — every RtD paper since cites Frayling.

17. **Archer, L. B. (1981). A View of the Nature of Design Research.** In R. Jacques & J. Powell (Eds.), *Design: Science: Method*, Westbury House, 30–47.
    - Defined design research as "systematic enquiry whose goal is knowledge of, or in, the embodiment of configuration, composition, structure, purpose, value and meaning in man-made things and systems."

## DRS as Institutional Infrastructure

18. **Cross, N. (Ed.) (1984). Developments in Design Methodology.** John Wiley & Sons.
    ISBN: 978-0-471-10248-5
    - Collected key papers from the design methods movement's first two decades. Essential reader.

19. **Cross, N., Dorst, K., & Roozenburg, N. (Eds.) (1992). Research in Design Thinking.** Delft University Press.
    - Proceedings of the Delft protocol workshop on design thinking. Established design cognition as a research field.

20. **Friedman, K. (2003). Theory Construction in Design Research: Criteria, Approaches, and Methods.** *Design Studies*, 24(6), 507–522.
    DOI: 10.1016/S0142-694X(03)00039-5
    - Proposed six domains of design knowledge: natural sciences, humanities/liberal arts, social/behavioral sciences, human professions/services, creative/applied arts, technology/engineering. Argued for rigorous theory in design research.

21. **Margolin, V., & Buchanan, R. (Eds.) (1995). The Idea of Design.** MIT Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-262-63166-2
    - Collected foundational essays from *Design Issues*. Bridged design history, theory, and practice.

## Connections to Genealogy Lineages

### What the Design Methods Movement Produced

| Concept from Movement | What It Became in the Genealogy |
|---|---|
| Simon's "design science" | Rejected → but "making as knowing" persists in Constructionism |
| Rittel's "wicked problems" | → Buchanan → design thinking → speculative design's provocation orientation |
| Schön's reflection-in-action | → RtD's epistemological justification: designing *is* researching |
| Cross's "designerly ways of knowing" | → RtD's claim that design produces *its own kind* of knowledge |
| Frayling's research through/into/for | → RtD formalized (Zimmerman et al. 2007) |
| Alexander's pattern languages | → design patterns → prototyping taxonomies |
| Jones's anti-method turn | → practice-based research, artistic research |
| Rittel's second-generation methods | → Participatory Design's stakeholder inclusion |
| DRS as institution | → *Design Studies*, *Design Issues*, DRS conferences as publication infrastructure |

### The Key Dialectic

The design methods movement contains within itself the seed of its own critique:
- **Thesis:** Design should be systematic (Simon, Archer, early Jones/Alexander)
- **Antithesis:** Design is wicked, reflective, embodied (Rittel, Schön, later Jones/Alexander)
- **Synthesis:** Design is its own discipline with its own knowledge (Cross, Frayling, Buchanan) → leads to RtD, constructive design research, and the entire genealogy we are mapping
