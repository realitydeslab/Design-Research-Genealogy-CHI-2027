# Literature Review: Intermediate-Level Knowledge in Design Research

## Overview

A central challenge for Research through Design: what *kind* of knowledge does designing produce? Design artifacts are particular — a specific prototype for a specific context. Theories are general. Between them lies **intermediate-level knowledge**: abstracted beyond a single instance but not claiming to be universal theory. This meta-methodological strand addresses the knowledge production question that legitimizes (or threatens) RtD as a research approach. Without intermediate-level knowledge forms, every RtD project is "just" a design project; with them, design research can claim to build cumulative knowledge.

**Key forms:**
1. **Annotated portfolios** (Gaver & Bowers 2012; Löwgren 2013)
2. **Strong concepts** (Höök & Löwgren 2012)
3. **Bridging concepts** (Dalsgaard & Dindler 2014)
4. **Research products** (Odom et al. 2016)
5. **Ultimate particulars** (Stolterman 2008)
6. **Design patterns** (Alexander 1977; Borchers 2001)

## The Core Debate

### The Problem

1. **Zimmerman, J., Forlizzi, J., & Evenson, S. (2007). Research Through Design as a Method for Interaction Design Research in HCI.** *Proceedings of CHI '07*, 493–502.
   DOI: 10.1145/1240624.1240704
   - Formalized RtD for HCI. Proposed that design artifacts can serve as "research contributions" — but what exactly is the contribution? This paper opened the knowledge question without fully resolving it.

2. **Zimmerman, J., Stolterman, E., & Forlizzi, J. (2010). An Analysis and Critique of Research through Design: Towards a Formalization of a Research Approach.** *Proceedings of DIS '10*, 310–319.
   DOI: 10.1145/1858171.1858228
   - Follow-up critique. Argued RtD lacks clear criteria for evaluating contributions. Proposed "extensibility" as criterion — can others build on this?

3. **Gaver, W. (2012). What Should We Expect from Research Through Design?** *Proceedings of CHI '12*, 937–946.
   DOI: 10.1145/2207676.2208538
   - The counter-position. Argued RtD should *not* converge on standardized methods or theory types. Design's value lies in "proliferating new realities, not generalizing from them." Proposed annotated portfolios as an alternative to theory. Advocated for "theory as annotation of realised design examples." Hugely influential — 1000+ citations.

## The Six Knowledge Forms

### Annotated Portfolios

4. **Gaver, W., & Bowers, J. (2012). Annotated Portfolios.** *Interactions*, 19(4), 40–49.
   DOI: 10.1145/2212877.2212889
   - Proposed annotated portfolios: collections of design examples with written annotations that draw out themes, commonalities, and contrasts. Knowledge emerges from *comparison across designs*, not from individual artifacts. Countered the demand for propositional theory.

5. **Löwgren, J. (2013). Annotated Portfolios and Other Forms of Intermediate-Level Knowledge.** *Interactions*, 20(1), 30–34.
   DOI: 10.1145/2405716.2405725
   - Extended annotated portfolios with a taxonomy of intermediate knowledge forms. Situated portfolios alongside strong concepts, design patterns, and experiential qualities. Mapped the space between instances and theory.

### Strong Concepts

6. **Höök, K., & Löwgren, J. (2012). Strong Concepts: Intermediate-Level Knowledge in Interaction Design Research.** *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 19(3), Article 23.
   DOI: 10.1145/2362364.2362371
   - Defined strong concepts: "design elements abstracted beyond particular instances which have the generative potential to be applied to new design situations." More abstract than a single artifact, less abstract than theory. Examples: "seamfulness," "social navigation," "feed." The most cited paper on intermediate-level knowledge.

### Bridging Concepts

7. **Dalsgaard, P., & Dindler, C. (2014). Between Theory and Practice: Bridging Concepts in HCI Research.** *Proceedings of CHI '14*, 1635–1644.
   DOI: 10.1145/2556288.2557342
   - Proposed bridging concepts: concepts that connect theoretical frameworks to design practice. Unlike strong concepts (which abstract from practice), bridging concepts translate *from theory into* practice. Examples: "peepholes," "pliability." Complementary to Höök & Löwgren.

### Research Products

8. **Odom, W., Wakkary, R., Lim, Y., Desjardins, A., Hengeveld, B., & Banks, R. (2016). From Research Prototype to Research Product.** *Proceedings of CHI '16*, 2549–2561.
   DOI: 10.1145/2858036.2858167
   - Proposed research products: designed artifacts deployed in real homes for extended periods (weeks to months), generating knowledge through lived experience rather than lab evaluation. Bridges prototyping, autoethnographic RtD, and material speculation. Products *are* the knowledge contribution — they embody design decisions that can be analyzed, annotated, and compared.

### Ultimate Particulars

9. **Stolterman, E. (2008). The Nature of Design Practice and Implications for Interaction Design Research.** *International Journal of Design*, 2(1), 55–65.
   - Argued design knowledge is fundamentally about "the ultimate particular" — the specific, situated design judgment for *this* situation. Resisted abstraction. Design's unique contribution is precisely its attention to the particular, not the general. Philosophical counterpoint to the strong concepts program.

### Design Patterns

10. **Alexander, C., Ishikawa, S., & Silverstein, M. (1977). A Pattern Language: Towns, Buildings, Construction.** Oxford University Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-19-501919-3
    - The original pattern language. 253 patterns for architecture. Hugely influential on software engineering (Gang of Four 1994) and interaction design.

11. **Borchers, J. (2001). A Pattern Approach to Interaction Design.** AI & Society, 15(4), 359–376.
    DOI: 10.1007/BF01206115
    - Adapted Alexander's patterns for interaction design. Patterns as intermediate-level knowledge: more general than a single design, more specific than theory.

## Meta-Level Reflection

12. **Höök, K., Dalsgaard, P., Reeves, S., Bardzell, J., Löwgren, J., Stolterman, E., & Rogers, Y. (2015). Knowledge Production in Interaction Design.** *CHI '15 Extended Abstracts*, 2429–2432.
   DOI: 10.1145/2702613.2702653
   - Panel at CHI 2015 bringing together the major voices. Debated: What counts as knowledge? How do we evaluate it? Can design knowledge be cumulative? No consensus reached — the productive tension continues.

13. **Redström, J. (2017). Making Design Theory.** MIT Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-262-03613-4
    - Book-length treatment of how design research produces theory. Proposed "programmatic design research": series of design experiments organized by an explicit design program. Connected to Koskinen et al.'s constructive design research and Binder & Redström's design programs.

14. **Fallman, D. (2008). The Interaction Design Research Triangle of Design Practice, Design Studies, and Design Exploration.** *Design Issues*, 24(3), 4–18.
    DOI: 10.1162/desi.2008.24.3.4
    - Mapped three orientations of interaction design research: practice (making for clients), studies (analyzing design), exploration (making to explore). Research through design lives in the exploration corner. Influential spatial model.

15. **Koskinen, I., Zimmerman, J., Binder, T., Redström, J., & Wensveen, S. (2011). Design Research Through Practice: From the Lab, Field, and Showroom.** Morgan Kaufmann.
    ISBN: 978-0-12-385502-2
    - The constructive design research textbook. Organized knowledge production into three settings: lab (controlled experiments with prototypes), field (ethnographic deployment), showroom (exhibition/provocation). Each setting produces different kinds of knowledge.

16. **Stappers, P. J., & Giaccardi, E. (2017). Research through Design.** In M. Soegaard & R. Friis-Dam (Eds.), *The Encyclopedia of Human-Computer Interaction* (2nd ed.). Interaction Design Foundation.
    - Encyclopedia entry synthesizing RtD's knowledge production for a broad audience. Covered prototyping, design spaces, research products, and the role of making in generating understanding.

## Connections to Other Lineages

- **→ RtD (Zimmerman et al.):** Intermediate-level knowledge is *the* meta-question for RtD. Without it, RtD is practice; with it, RtD is research.
- **→ Prototyping as Research (Houde & Hill):** Prototypes embody design knowledge. Research products (Odom et al.) extend prototypes temporally.
- **→ Design Methods Movement:** The knowledge debate reprises Simon vs. Schön. Simon wanted general design science; Schön valued particular reflective knowledge. Strong concepts vs. ultimate particulars recapitulates this tension.
- **→ Artist-Led Research (Benford):** Annotated portfolios mirror how artists document bodies of work. Gaver's Interaction Research Studio explicitly draws on this tradition.
- **→ Soma Design (Höök):** Höök co-authored the strong concepts paper. Somaesthetic interaction concepts *are* strong concepts.
- **→ Experiential Futures / Speculative Enactments:** Research products (Odom) bridge to speculative enactments (Elsden) — both deploy designed artifacts in real settings to generate knowledge through lived experience.
- **→ Constructive Design Research (Koskinen et al.):** CDR's lab/field/showroom is an organizational framework for knowledge production settings.
- **→ First-Person Methods:** Autoethnographic RtD generates knowledge through personal experience with designed artifacts — the knowledge is embodied and particular (Stolterman's "ultimate particular").
