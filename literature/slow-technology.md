## Foundational Works

1. **Weiser, M. (1991). The Computer for the 21st Century.** *Scientific American*, 265(3), 94–104.
   DOI: 10.1038/scientificamerican0991-94
   - Seminal vision of ubiquitous computing where technology recedes into the background. Direct precursor to both calm technology and slow technology.

2. **Weiser, M., & Brown, J. S. (1995). Designing Calm Technology.** Xerox PARC.
   URL: https://calmtech.com/papers/designing-calm-technology
   - Coined "calm technology" — technology that engages both the center and periphery of attention. Key concept: moving information between periphery and center. Hallnäs & Redström explicitly build on this.

3. **Weiser, M., & Brown, J. S. (1996). The Coming Age of Calm Technology.** Xerox PARC.
   URL: https://calmtech.com/papers/coming-age-calm-technology
   - Extended calm technology vision; proposed three waves of computing culminating in calm, ubiquitous technology.

4. **Hallnäs, L., & Redström, J. (2001). Slow Technology — Designing for Reflection.** *Personal and Ubiquitous Computing*, 5(3), 201–212.
   DOI: 10.1007/PL00000019
   - The originating paper. Defined slow technology as "technology aimed at reflection and moments of mental rest rather than efficiency in performance." Proposed design principles for temporal, reflective interaction.

5. **Hallnäs, L., Jaksetic, P., Ljungstrand, P., Redström, J., & Skog, T. (2001). Expressions: Towards a Design Practice of Slow Technology.** *Proceedings of INTERACT '01*, 447–454.
   URL: http://redstrom.se/johan/papers/expressions.pdf
   - Design explorations demonstrating slow technology in practice; created a basic collection of computational expressions for systematic investigation.

6. **Hallnäs, L., & Redström, J. (2002). From Use to Presence: On the Expressions and Aesthetics of Everyday Computational Things.** *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 9(2), 106–124.
   DOI: 10.1145/513665.513668
   - Extended slow technology into computational aesthetics; things as expressive presences rather than tools for use.

7. **Redström, J. (2001). Designing Everyday Computational Things.** PhD thesis, Göteborg University.
   ISBN: 91-628-4873-4
   URL: https://gupea.ub.gu.se/handle/2077/934
   - Doctoral work establishing the theoretical framework for slow technology and computational aesthetics.

## Extensions & Applications

8. **Redström, J. (2017). Making Design Theory.** MIT Press.
   ISBN: 978-0-262-03606-3
   DOI: 10.7551/mitpress/9780262036658.001.0001
   - Theoretical framework for how design practice generates theory. Proposes "transitional theory" — theory as something made in and through design. Slow technology is a concrete instance of this epistemology.

9. **Höök, K., & Löwgren, J. (2012). Strong Concepts: Intermediate-Level Knowledge in Interaction Design Research.** *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 19(3), Article 23.
   DOI: 10.1145/2362364.2362371
   - "Strong concepts" as intermediate knowledge; slow technology itself functions as a strong concept in design research.

10. **Höök, K., Jonsson, M., Ståhl, A., & Mercurio, J. (2016). Somaesthetic Appreciation Design.** *Proceedings of CHI '16*, 3131–3142.
    DOI: 10.1145/2858036.2858583
    - Proposed somaesthetic appreciation as a strong concept; shares slow technology's emphasis on felt, reflective bodily experience over efficient task completion.

11. **Höök, K. (2018). Designing with the Body: Somaesthetic Interaction Design.** MIT Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-262-03856-2
    URL: https://direct.mit.edu/books/monograph/4131/Designing-with-the-BodySomaesthetic-Interaction
    - Book-length treatment of soma design. Shares slow technology's emphasis on felt, reflective experience; proposes qualitative shift to experiential, aesthetic design stance.

12. **Case, A. (2015). Calm Technology: Principles and Patterns for Non-Intrusive Design.** O'Reilly Media.
    ISBN: 978-1-491-92588-1
    URL: https://www.oreilly.com/library/view/calm-technology/9781491925874/
    - Formalized Weiser & Brown's calm technology ideas into actionable design principles: peripheral attention, context, ambient awareness. Popularized calm/slow design thinking for practitioners.

13. **Vallgårda, A. (2009). Computational Composites: Understanding the Materiality of Computational Technology.** PhD thesis, IT University of Copenhagen.
    DOI: 10.13140/2.1.4769.2487
    - Proposed computational composites — materials combining computational and physical properties. Foundational for understanding how temporal form emerges from material properties of computation.

14. **Vallgårda, A. (2014). Giving Form to Computational Things: Developing a Practice of Interaction Design.** *Personal and Ubiquitous Computing*, 18(3), 577–592.
    DOI: 10.1007/s00779-013-0685-8
    - Proposed trinity of forms (physical form, interaction gestalt, temporal form) as framework for interaction design practice. Demonstrated how computational composites enable working with temporal form.

15. **Vallgårda, A., & Fernaeus, Y. (2015). Interaction Design as a Bricolage Practice.** *Proceedings of TEI '15*, 173–180.
    DOI: 10.1145/2677199.2680594
    - Proposed bricolage as interaction design practice; promotes design qualities tuned to tangible and material computing, sensitive to the unstable physical world.

16. **Tsaknaki, V., Cohn, M., Boer, L., Fernaeus, Y., & Vallgårda, A. (2016). Things Fall Apart: Unpacking the Temporalities of Impermanence for HCI.** *Proceedings of NordiCHI '16*, 141–150.
    DOI: 10.1145/2971485.2971559
    - Examined how things age, decay, and obsolesce; proposed impermanence as a design resource. Direct engagement with slow technology's temporal concerns — things changing over time.

## Slow Technology in Practice

17. **Odom, W., Wakkary, R., Lim, Y., Desjardins, A., Hengeveld, B., & Banks, R. (2016). From Research Prototype to Research Product.** *Proceedings of CHI '16*, 2549–2561.
    DOI: 10.1145/2858036.2858167
    - Proposed "research products" deployed over long periods; slow technology principles applied to long-term design research.

18. **Odom, W., Wakkary, R., Bertran, I., Harkness, M., Hertz, G., & Hol, J. (2018). Attending to Slowness and Temporality with Olly and Slow Game.** *Proceedings of CHI '18*, Article 77.
    DOI: 10.1145/3173574.3173651
    - Olly: a slow technology artifact deployed in homes; demonstrated how slowness supports longer-term relationships with computational objects.

19. **Odom, W., Wakkary, R., Hol, J., Thoelen, B., Verber, S., & Lin, T. (2022). Extending a Theory of Slow Technology for Design through Artifact Analysis.** *Human–Computer Interaction*, 37(2), 150–179.
    DOI: 10.1080/07370024.2021.1913416
    - Extended Hallnäs & Redström's original theory through systematic artifact analysis; updated slow technology for contemporary design research.

20. **Gaver, W., Bowers, J., Boucher, A., Gellerson, H., Pennington, S., Schmidt, A., Steed, A., Villars, N., & Walker, B. (2004). The Drift Table: Designing for Ludic Engagement.** *Proceedings of CHI '04 Extended Abstracts*, 885–900.
    DOI: 10.1145/985921.985947
    - The Drift Table as a slow technology exemplar: a table that slowly reveals aerial photographs as objects drift across it. Ludic engagement as alternative to task-oriented interaction.

    DOI: 10.1145/2470654.2466472
    - Applied slow design principles to interaction design; connected Slow Technology to the broader Slow movement.

22. **Strauss, C., & Fuad-Luke, A. (2008). The Slow Design Principles.** In *Changing the Change: Design, Visions, Proposals and Tools*, proceedings.
    URL: https://slowlab.net/the-slow-design-principles/
    - Six principles of slow design: reveal, expand, reflect, engage, participate, evolve. Broader framework within which Slow Technology sits.

23. **Fuad-Luke, A. (2002). 'Slow Design' — A Paradigm Shift in Design Philosophy?** *Proceedings of Development by Design (d4d)*, Bangalore.
    URL: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/273769457
    - Early articulation of slow design as a design philosophy; preceded and influenced Slow Technology.

## Connections to Other Lineages

- **RtD** (parent): Slow Technology is a design philosophy within RtD, producing knowledge through reflective artifacts
- **Somaesthetic Design**: Shares emphasis on felt, bodily, reflective experience (Höök 2016, 2018)
- **Material Speculation**: Long-term deployment of actual artifacts connects both approaches (Wakkary et al. 2015)
- **Computational Composites**: Vallgårda's temporal form framework provides material basis for slow technology's temporality
- **Calm Technology**: Weiser & Brown's calm technology (1995) is the direct precursor; Hallnäs & Redström extend it from peripheral attention to deep reflection
- **More-than-Human Design**: Slow technology's attention to things' temporal rhythms resonates with thing-centered thinking
- **Constructionism**: Slow making as deep, reflective knowledge production

## Summary

Slow Technology (Hallnäs & Redström, 2001) proposed a design philosophy prioritizing reflection over efficiency, building on Weiser & Brown's calm technology (1995) but shifting from peripheral attention to deep contemplation. The foundational work established three interconnected themes — reflective technology, time in technology, and the aesthetics of computational presence — that have been extended in two major directions.

First, a practical strand led by Odom, Wakkary, and colleagues at Simon Fraser University developed slow technology through long-term deployed "research products" (Olly, Slow Game, Morse Things), culminating in Odom et al.'s (2022) systematic extension of the original theory through artifact analysis. Second, a material-temporal strand led by Vallgårda, Fernaeus, and colleagues at ITU Copenhagen and KTH explored the computational material basis of temporality through computational composites (2009), temporal form (2014), and impermanence (2016).

The lineage connects to somaesthetic design (Höök 2016, 2018) through shared emphasis on felt experience, to material speculation through long-term deployment methods, and to more-than-human design through attention to things' own temporal rhythms. Amber Case's popularization of calm technology principles (2015) carried slow/calm design thinking into industry practice. The approach continues to offer a critical alternative to speed-obsessed technology design, with recent work (2020-2026) extending it into shape-changing artifacts, ecological design, and AI-mediated temporalities.
