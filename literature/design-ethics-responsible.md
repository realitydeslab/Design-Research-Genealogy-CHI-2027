# Design Ethics and Responsible Design — Genealogy Lineage

This lineage traces how ethical reasoning has become embedded in HCI and design research, from early work on bias and values in computing to contemporary movements around design justice, AI ethics, dark patterns regulation, and trauma-informed design. Design ethics in HCI is not a single thread but a convergence of value-oriented, critical, feminist, postcolonial, and justice-oriented traditions that have progressively moved ethical reflection from a peripheral concern to a central design commitment.

---

## 1. Foundations: Bias, Values, and Ethical Reflection in Computing

### 1.1 Bias in Computer Systems

- **Friedman, B., & Nissenbaum, H. (1996).** Bias in Computer Systems. *ACM Transactions on Information Systems*, 14(3), 330–347. DOI: [10.1145/230538.230561](https://doi.org/10.1145/230538.230561)
  - **Contribution:** Developed the foundational taxonomy of three categories of bias in computing—preexisting, technical, and emergent—establishing that bias is not incidental but systematically embedded in design. This paper is the intellectual precursor to Value Sensitive Design and all subsequent work on algorithmic fairness.
  - **Relevance:** Origin point for the entire lineage; frames bias as a design problem rather than a purely social or technical one.

### 1.2 Value Sensitive Design (VSD)

- **Friedman, B., Kahn, P. H., Jr., & Borning, A. (2003).** Value Sensitive Design: Theory and Methods. University of Washington Technical Report. Later published in: Zhang, P. & Galletta, D. (Eds.), *Human-Computer Interaction in Management Information Systems: Foundations*, M.E. Sharpe.
  - **Contribution:** The definitive early account of VSD as a theoretically grounded approach with a tripartite methodology (conceptual, empirical, technical investigations). Introduced stakeholder analysis, value conflicts, and iterative design as core methods.
  - **Relevance:** Established the dominant framework for integrating human values into technology design; cited across virtually all subsequent ethics-in-design work.

- **Friedman, B., Hendry, D. G., & Borning, A. (2017).** A Survey of Value Sensitive Design Methods. *Foundations and Trends in Human–Computer Interaction*, 11(2), 63–125. DOI: [10.1561/1100000015](https://doi.org/10.1561/1100000015)
  - **Contribution:** Comprehensive survey of 14 VSD methods including stakeholder analysis, value scenarios, value sketches, and envisioning cards. Codified two decades of methodological development.
  - **Relevance:** The methodological handbook for VSD practitioners; bridges theory and practice.

- **Friedman, B., & Hendry, D. G. (2019).** *Value Sensitive Design: Shaping Technology with Moral Imagination*. MIT Press. ISBN: 978-0-262-03953-6. (2nd edition 2026, ISBN: 978-0-262-55325-4)
  - **Contribution:** The definitive book-length treatment of VSD, integrating theory, 17 methods, and 10 application domains. Introduced the concept of "moral imagination" in design.
  - **Relevance:** Canonical reference; positions VSD as mature design framework rather than ad hoc ethics checklist.

- **Hendry, D. G., Friedman, B., & Ballard, S. (2021).** Value Sensitive Design as a Formative Framework. *Ethics and Information Technology*, 23, 39–44. DOI: [10.1007/s10676-021-09579-x](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10676-021-09579-x)
  - **Contribution:** Articulated VSD as a formative (not summative) framework, distinguishing three types of design knowledge: know-about, know-that, know-how.
  - **Relevance:** Theoretical maturation of VSD; clarifies its role relative to other design frameworks.

### 1.3 Reflective Design

- **Sengers, P., Boehner, K., David, S., & Kaye, J. (2005).** Reflective Design. In *Proceedings of the 4th Decennial Conference on Critical Computing: Between Sense and Sensibility* (CC '05), 49–58. ACM. DOI: [10.1145/1094562.1094569](https://doi.org/10.1145/1094562.1094569)
  - **Contribution:** Argued that critical reflection on unconscious values embedded in technology should be a core design principle. Synthesized critical technical practice (Agre), participatory design, critical design (Dunne & Raby), and VSD into a unified reflective framework. Proposed six principles for reflective design.
  - **Relevance:** Bridges critical theory and design practice; complements VSD's proactive value integration with a reflexive, questioning stance. Key ancestor of critical design approaches in HCI.

## 2. Feminist HCI and Justice-Oriented Design

### 2.1 Feminist HCI

- **Bardzell, S. (2010).** Feminist HCI: Taking Stock and Outlining an Agenda for Design. In *Proceedings of the SIGCHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems* (CHI '10), 1301–1310. ACM. DOI: [10.1145/1753326.1753521](https://doi.org/10.1145/1753326.1753521)
  - **Contribution:** Landmark paper establishing feminism as a productive framework for HCI, proposing qualities for feminist interaction design: pluralism, participation, advocacy, ecology, embodiment, and self-disclosure. Demonstrated how feminist epistemology reveals hidden values in HCI's dominant paradigms.
  - **Relevance:** Foundational text for all subsequent work connecting gender, power, and design in HCI. Directly influenced design justice, care ethics, and critical HCI.

- **Bardzell, S., & Bardzell, J. (2011).** Towards a Feminist HCI Methodology: Social Science, Feminism, and HCI. In *Proceedings of the SIGCHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems* (CHI '11), 675–684. ACM. DOI: [10.1145/1978942.1979041](https://doi.org/10.1145/1978942.1979041)
  - **Contribution:** Extended the feminist HCI agenda with methodological specifics, arguing for integration of feminist social science methods into HCI research practice.
  - **Relevance:** Methodological companion to Bardzell (2010); operationalizes feminist theory for design research.

### 2.2 Postcolonial Computing

- **Irani, L., Vertesi, J., Dourish, P., Philip, K., & Grinter, R. E. (2010).** Postcolonial Computing: A Lens on Design and Development. In *Proceedings of the SIGCHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems* (CHI '10), 1311–1320. ACM. DOI: [10.1145/1753326.1753522](https://doi.org/10.1145/1753326.1753522)
  - **Contribution:** Introduced postcolonial theory as an analytical orientation for HCI, proposing four shifts: generative models of culture, development as historical program, attention to uneven economic relations, and engagement with cultural encounters. Critiqued universalist assumptions in HCI4D.
  - **Relevance:** Foundational for decolonial HCI; challenged the Global North centrism of design methods and exposed how "user-centered" design can reproduce colonial power dynamics.

- **Philip, K., Irani, L., & Dourish, P. (2012).** Postcolonial Computing: A Tactical Survey. *Science, Technology, & Human Values*, 37(1), 3–29. DOI: [10.1177/0162243910389594](https://doi.org/10.1177/0162243910389594)
  - **Contribution:** Extended the postcolonial computing framework with deeper engagement with postcolonial theory, STS, and development studies.
  - **Relevance:** Theoretical deepening of the 2010 CHI paper; bridges HCI with postcolonial STS.

### 2.3 Design Justice

- **Costanza-Chock, S. (2018).** Design Justice: Towards an Intersectional Feminist Framework for Design Theory and Practice. In *Proceedings of the Design Research Society 2018*. DOI: [10.21606/drs.2018.679](https://doi.org/10.21606/drs.2018.679)
  - **Contribution:** Proposed design justice as a field concerned with how design distributes risks, harms, and benefits, drawing on Black feminist thought (Collins's matrix of domination) and intersectionality.
  - **Relevance:** Theoretical foundation paper for the design justice movement; bridges participatory design with social justice frameworks.

- **Costanza-Chock, S. (2020).** *Design Justice: Community-Led Practices to Build the Worlds We Need*. MIT Press. ISBN: 978-0-262-04345-8. URL: [https://designjustice.mitpress.mit.edu/](https://designjustice.mitpress.mit.edu/)
  - **Contribution:** Book-length treatment exploring how design reproduces/challenges the matrix of domination across values, practices, narratives, sites, and pedagogies. Open access. Grounded in the Design Justice Network's 10 principles.
  - **Relevance:** The defining text of the design justice movement; synthesizes feminist HCI, participatory design, postcolonial computing, and social movement theory into a coherent design framework.

### 2.4 Anti-Racist and Race-Critical Design

- **Benjamin, R. (2019).** *Race After Technology: Abolitionist Tools for the New Jim Code*. Polity Press. ISBN: 978-1-509-52640-6.
  - **Contribution:** Introduced the concept of the "New Jim Code"—how algorithms and design encode racial hierarchies while appearing neutral. Analyzed discriminatory design across domains.
  - **Relevance:** Bridged critical race studies and technology design; widely cited in HCI for centering race in design critique. Complements Costanza-Chock's intersectional framework with specific focus on anti-Black racism in tech.

- **Fox, S., Irani, L., Rosner, D. K., Bardzell, J., Bardzell, S., Lampe, C., & Austin, D. (2017).** Social Justice and Design: Power and Oppression in Collaborative Systems. In *Companion of the 2017 ACM Conference on Computer Supported Cooperative Work and Social Computing* (CSCW '17 Companion), 117–122. ACM. DOI: [10.1145/3022198.3022201](https://doi.org/10.1145/3022198.3022201)
  - **Contribution:** Panel bringing together CSCW scholars to examine power, oppression, and design-oriented social change.
  - **Relevance:** Crystallized social justice as an explicit agenda for CSCW/HCI design research.

- **Rankin, Y. A., Thomas, J. O., & Irish, I. (2024).** Social Justice in HCI: A Systematic Literature Review. In *Proceedings of the 2024 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems* (CHI '24). ACM. DOI: [10.1145/3613904.3642704](https://doi.org/10.1145/3613904.3642704)
  - **Contribution:** Systematic review of social justice work across CHI, mapping frameworks, methods, and gaps. Identified five "mosaics of justice" including transformative, reproductive, economic, racial, and design justice.
  - **Relevance:** State-of-the-art survey showing how deeply justice-oriented work has permeated HCI by the mid-2020s.

## 3. Ethics of Care in Design

- **Light, A., & Akama, Y. (2014).** Structuring Future Social Relations: The Politics of Care in Participatory Practice. In *Proceedings of the 13th Participatory Design Conference* (PDC '14), Vol. 1, 151–160. ACM. DOI: [10.1145/2661435.2661438](https://doi.org/10.1145/2661435.2661438)
  - **Contribution:** Applied ethics of care (drawing on Tronto and Puig de la Bellacasa) to participatory design, reframing the designer as "custodian of care" and arguing for attention to relational, political, and affective dimensions of design practice.
  - **Relevance:** Foundational text connecting care ethics to design methodology; influenced subsequent more-than-human and feminist PD work.

- **Akama, Y., & Light, A. (2020).** Expanding Participation to Design with More-Than-Human Concerns. In *Proceedings of the 16th Participatory Design Conference* (PDC '20), Vol. 2, 1–5. ACM. DOI: [10.1145/3384772.3385162](https://doi.org/10.1145/3384772.3385162)
  - **Contribution:** Extended care-ethical PD to more-than-human contexts, drawing on feminist technoscience and Japanese philosophy.
  - **Relevance:** Bridges care ethics with posthumanist and more-than-human design traditions.

- **Key, C., Gatehouse, C., & Taylor, N. (2022).** Feminist Care in the Anthropocene: Packing and Unpacking Tensions in Posthumanist HCI. In *Proceedings of the 2022 ACM Designing Interactive Systems Conference* (DIS '22). ACM.
  - **Contribution:** Explored tensions between care ethics and posthumanism in HCI, surfacing challenges for researchers working at this intersection.
  - **Relevance:** Shows the ongoing productive tension between care-centered and posthumanist approaches in contemporary design ethics.

## 4. Dark Patterns and Deceptive Design

### 4.1 Foundational Work

- **Brignull, H. (2010).** Dark Patterns: User Interfaces Designed to Trick People. *darkpatterns.org* (now deceptive.design). URL: [https://www.deceptive.design/](https://www.deceptive.design/)
  - **Contribution:** Coined the term "dark patterns" to describe user interfaces deliberately crafted to trick users. Created initial taxonomy of deceptive design strategies (roach motel, bait and switch, forced continuity, etc.).
  - **Relevance:** Practitioner-originated concept that became a major research area in HCI ethics; the starting point for all subsequent academic dark patterns research.

### 4.2 Academic Dark Patterns Research

- **Gray, C. M., Kou, Y., Battles, B., Hoggatt, J., & Toombs, A. L. (2018).** The Dark (Patterns) Side of UX Design. In *Proceedings of the 2018 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems* (CHI '18), Paper No. 534. ACM. DOI: [10.1145/3173574.3174108](https://doi.org/10.1145/3173574.3174108)
  - **Contribution:** First major CHI paper on dark patterns; performed content analysis of practitioner-identified examples, producing a broader taxonomy of five strategies: nagging, obstruction, sneaking, interface interference, and forced action. Connected dark patterns to designer ethics and UX practice.
  - **Relevance:** Established dark patterns as a legitimate HCI research topic; the most-cited academic paper on the subject.

- **Mathur, A., Acar, G., Friedman, M. J., Lucherini, E., Mayer, J., Chetty, M., & Narayanan, A. (2019).** Dark Patterns at Scale: Findings from a Crawl of 11K Shopping Websites. *Proceedings of the ACM on Human-Computer Interaction*, 3(CSCW), Article 81. DOI: [10.1145/3359183](https://doi.org/10.1145/3359183)
  - **Contribution:** Large-scale empirical study using automated crawling to identify dark patterns across 11,000 shopping websites. Found 1,818 instances across 1,254 websites, demonstrating the pervasiveness of deceptive design. Built on Gray et al.'s taxonomy.
  - **Relevance:** Shifted dark patterns research from case studies to empirical measurement at scale; provided evidence base for regulatory action.

- **Mathur, A., Kshirsagar, M., & Mayer, J. (2021).** What Makes a Dark Pattern... Dark? Design Attributes, Normative Considerations, and Measurement Methods. In *Proceedings of the 2021 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems* (CHI '21). ACM. DOI: [10.1145/3411764.3445610](https://doi.org/10.1145/3411764.3445610)
  - **Contribution:** Proposed conceptual unification of dark patterns definitions, identifying common attributes (asymmetric, covert, deceptive, information-hiding, restrictive) and normative frameworks.
  - **Relevance:** Theoretical consolidation enabling cross-disciplinary communication between HCI, law, and policy.

### 4.3 Consent Design and Regulation

- **Utz, C., Degeling, M., Fahl, S., Schaub, F., & Holz, T. (2019).** (Un)informed Consent: Studying GDPR Consent Notices in the Field. In *Proceedings of the 2019 ACM SIGSAC Conference on Computer and Communications Security* (CCS '19), 973–990. ACM. DOI: [10.1145/3319535.3354212](https://doi.org/10.1145/3319535.3354212)
  - **Contribution:** Field experiment with 80,000+ users showing how consent notice design (position, choice type, framing) substantially impacts consent behavior. Demonstrated nudging effects in cookie banners.
  - **Relevance:** Empirical evidence that design choices in consent interfaces have measurable effects on user autonomy; bridged HCI and privacy regulation.

- **Nouwens, M., Liccardi, I., Veale, M., Karger, D., & Kagal, L. (2020).** Dark Patterns after the GDPR: Scraping Consent Pop-ups and Demonstrating their Influence. In *Proceedings of the 2020 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems* (CHI '20). ACM. DOI: [10.1145/3313831.3376321](https://doi.org/10.1145/3313831.3376321)
  - **Contribution:** Scraped consent management platforms on top 10,000 UK websites; found only 11.8% met minimal GDPR requirements. Demonstrated that removing opt-out buttons increases consent by 22–23 percentage points.
  - **Relevance:** Definitive empirical study on dark patterns in GDPR consent interfaces; directly informed regulatory guidance.

### 4.4 Dark Patterns Ontology and Recent Consolidation

- **Gray, C. M., Santos, C. T., Bielova, N., & Mildner, T. (2024).** An Ontology of Dark Patterns Knowledge: Foundations, Definitions, and a Pathway for Shared Knowledge-Building. In *Proceedings of the 2024 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems* (CHI '24). ACM.
  - **Contribution:** Developed a formal ontology unifying dark patterns knowledge across academic, regulatory, and practitioner sources. Mapped relationships between high-level strategies and low-level patterns.
  - **Relevance:** Consolidation work enabling systematic comparison across the rapidly growing dark patterns literature.

## 5. AI Ethics in Design

### 5.1 Fairness, Accountability, and Transparency in Design Practice

- **Madaio, M. A., Stark, L., Wortman Vaughan, J., & Wallach, H. (2020).** Co-Designing Checklists to Understand Organizational Challenges and Opportunities around Fairness in AI. In *Proceedings of the 2020 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems* (CHI '20), Paper 318. ACM. DOI: [10.1145/3313831.3376445](https://doi.org/10.1145/3313831.3376445)
  - **Contribution:** Co-designed an AI fairness checklist with 48 practitioners across 12 teams. Found that AI fairness efforts are often ad hoc, that checklists need organizational support, and that team-level processes are more effective than individual tools.
  - **Relevance:** Key paper on the *design research* angle of AI ethics—not just ML metrics but how practitioners actually engage with fairness in practice.

- **Veale, M., Van Kleek, M., & Binns, R. (2018).** Fairness and Accountability Design Needs for Algorithmic Support in High-Stakes Public Sector Decision-Making. In *Proceedings of the 2018 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems* (CHI '18), Paper 440. ACM. DOI: [10.1145/3173574.3174014](https://doi.org/10.1145/3173574.3174014)
  - **Contribution:** Interviewed public sector machine learning practitioners, identifying design needs for fairness and accountability in algorithmic decision-making. Highlighted gaps between technical fairness tools and real-world organizational constraints.
  - **Relevance:** Grounded AI ethics in actual design practice; pioneered the practitioner-centered perspective in responsible AI.

### 5.2 Responsible AI Toolkits and Design

- **Lee, M. K., Grgić-Hlača, N., et al. (2020).** Human-Centered Approaches to Fair and Responsible AI. In *Extended Abstracts of the 2020 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems* (CHI EA '20). ACM. DOI: [10.1145/3334480.3375158](https://doi.org/10.1145/3334480.3375158)
  - **Contribution:** Workshop bringing together HCI, ML, and social science researchers to devise a cross-disciplinary research agenda for fair and responsible AI systems beyond statistical fairness.
  - **Relevance:** Catalyzed the human-centered responsible AI research community at CHI.

- **Wong, R. Y., Madaio, M. A., & Merrill, N. (2023).** Seeing Like a Toolkit: How Toolkits Envision the Work of AI Ethics. *Proceedings of the ACM on Human-Computer Interaction*, 7(CSCW1). DOI: [10.1145/3579621](https://doi.org/10.1145/3579621)
  - **Contribution:** Critical analysis of how RAI toolkits frame and construct the work of AI ethics, revealing how their "decontextualized approach to ethics" can flatten critical nuance.
  - **Relevance:** Important reflexive critique of the toolkit-ification of AI ethics from within HCI.

- **Deng, W. H., et al. (2023).** Understanding Practices, Challenges, and Opportunities for User-Engaged Algorithm Auditing in Industry Practices. In *Proceedings of the 2023 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems* (CHI '23). ACM.
  - **Contribution:** Investigated how practitioners contextualize general-purpose RAI resources for specific organizational settings, identifying tensions around ownership and accountability.
  - **Relevance:** Empirical grounding of RAI tool adoption challenges.

### 5.3 VSD Meets Responsible AI

- **Sanctis, M., et al. (2024).** Integrating Value Sensitive Design into Responsible AI Toolkits: A Case Study with the Nokia AI Design Toolkit. In *Proceedings of the 2024 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems* (CHI '24). ACM. DOI: [10.1145/3613904.3642810](https://doi.org/10.1145/3613904.3642810)
  - **Contribution:** Mapped VSD values onto the Nokia AI Design toolkit's responsible AI pillars, formulating six design guidelines for integrating VSD into RAI toolkit development.
  - **Relevance:** Directly bridges the VSD tradition with the responsible AI toolkit movement; demonstrates how established design ethics frameworks can strengthen AI ethics practice.

## 6. Sustainability Ethics in HCI

- **Blevis, E. (2007).** Sustainable Interaction Design: Invention & Disposal, Renewal & Reuse. In *Proceedings of the SIGCHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems* (CHI '07), 503–512. ACM. DOI: [10.1145/1240624.1240705](https://doi.org/10.1145/1240624.1240705)
  - **Contribution:** Established Sustainable Interaction Design (SID) as a design perspective, proposing a rubric for assessing material effects (disposal, salvage, recycling, reuse, repair) and principles linking invention to disposal.
  - **Relevance:** Foundational paper connecting sustainability to HCI design values; ancestor of all subsequent sustainable HCI work.

- **DiSalvo, C., Sengers, P., & Brynjarsdóttir, H. (2010).** Mapping the Landscape of Sustainable HCI. In *Proceedings of the SIGCHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems* (CHI '10), 1975–1984. ACM. DOI: [10.1145/1753326.1753625](https://doi.org/10.1145/1753326.1753625)
  - **Contribution:** Mapped the emerging field of sustainable HCI, identifying five genres of research: persuasive technology, ambient awareness, sustainable interaction design, formative sustainability, and pervasive/ubiquitous sustainability.
  - **Relevance:** Organized the sustainability-HCI space; showed how ethical commitments to sustainability produce distinct design approaches.

## 7. Trauma-Informed and Consent-Centered Design (2022–2026)

- **Chen, J., et al. (2022).** Trauma-Informed Computing: Towards Safer Technology Experiences for All. In *Proceedings of the 2022 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems* (CHI '22). ACM.
  - **Contribution:** Introduced trauma-informed computing (TIC) to HCI, adapting SAMHSA's six principles (safety, trustworthiness, peer support, collaboration, empowerment, cultural sensitivity) for technology design.
  - **Relevance:** Established trauma-informed design as a formal HCI research area.

- **Randazzo, C. (2023).** "If Someone Downvoted My Posts—That'd Be the End of the World": Designing Safer Online Spaces for Trauma Survivors. In *Proceedings of the 2023 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems* (CHI '23). ACM.
  - **Contribution:** Empirical study of trauma survivors' needs in online spaces, proposing design principles for safer platforms.
  - **Relevance:** Grounded trauma-informed design in survivor experiences.

- **Randazzo, C., et al. (2024).** Trauma-Informed Design: A Collaborative Approach to Building Safer Online Spaces. *CSCW 2024 Workshop*.
  - **Contribution:** Workshop bringing together researchers and practitioners to examine measurement and application of trauma-informed approaches to social media.
  - **Relevance:** Community-building for trauma-informed design practice.

- **Agha, Z., et al. (2024).** "It's Not What We Were Trying to Get At, but I Think Maybe It Should Be": Learning How to Do Trauma-Informed Design With a Data Donation Platform. In *Proceedings of the 2024 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems* (CHI '24). ACM. DOI: [10.1145/3613904.3642045](https://doi.org/10.1145/3613904.3642045)
  - **Contribution:** Documented the process of integrating trauma-informed principles into the design of a data donation platform for online dating sexual consent research. Proposed three elements: adapted design goals, sensitizing concepts, and consequent design choices.
  - **Relevance:** First detailed design case study of trauma-informed computing methodology.

- **Wisniewski, P., et al. (2024).** Toward Trauma-Informed Research Practices with Youth in HCI: Caring for Participants and Research Assistants When Studying Sensitive Topics. In *Proceedings of the 2024 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems* (CHI '24). ACM.
  - **Contribution:** Applied trauma-informed principles to HCI research methodology itself (not just design outputs), focusing on protecting both participants and research assistants.
  - **Relevance:** Extended trauma-informed thinking beyond product design to research ethics.

- **Rossi, S., Huyskes, D., & Jorgensen, M. (2026).** Resisting Humanization: Ethical Front-End Design Choices for Conversational AI Supporting GBV Survivors. In *Proceedings of the 2026 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems* (CHI '26). ACM.
  - **Contribution:** Case study of Chayn's trauma-informed design approach for AI systems serving gender-based violence survivors; examined ethical tensions around humanization of AI chatbots.
  - **Relevance:** Cutting-edge intersection of trauma-informed design, AI ethics, and care ethics at CHI 2026.

## 8. Lineage Connections

### 8.1 Connections to Participatory Design
Design ethics is deeply intertwined with participatory design (PD). VSD's stakeholder analysis methods drew from Scandinavian PD traditions (Schuler & Namioka, 1993; Muller & Kuhn, 1993). Light & Akama's care ethics work emerged directly from PD practice. Design justice explicitly builds on PD's "nothing about us without us" commitment while critiquing its limitations when applied without attention to power.

### 8.2 Connections to Critical and Speculative Design
Reflective design (Sengers et al., 2005) explicitly drew on critical design (Dunne & Raby) and ludic design (Gaver) as strategies for provoking ethical reflection. The lineage runs: critical design → reflective design → adversarial design (DiSalvo, 2012) → speculative ethics in design.

### 8.3 Connections to Human-Centered Design
Design ethics represents both an extension of and a challenge to HCD. VSD extends HCD by foregrounding values; feminist HCI and postcolonial computing challenge HCD's universalist assumptions; design justice critiques how "user-centered" design can center some users at the expense of others.

### 8.4 Convergence in the 2020s
By the mid-2020s, previously distinct threads—VSD, feminist HCI, postcolonial computing, design justice, dark patterns regulation, responsible AI, trauma-informed design, sustainability ethics—are converging into a broad "design ethics" orientation that is increasingly mainstream at CHI and CSCW. Key markers include:
- CHI's growing ethics tracks and social justice papers
- The integration of VSD with RAI toolkits (Sanctis et al., 2024)
- Dark patterns moving from academic concern to regulatory mandate (EU Digital Services Act, FTC enforcement)
- Trauma-informed and consent-centered design becoming established subfields
- Social justice systematic reviews mapping the field's expansion (Rankin et al., 2024)
