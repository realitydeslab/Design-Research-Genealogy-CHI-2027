# Design Anthropology: Lineage and Genealogy

## Overview

Design anthropology is an emergent interdisciplinary field that moves beyond using ethnography to *inform* design, toward understanding anthropology *as* a form of design practice — and vice versa. Where ethnography-in-design (Suchman 1987; Dourish 2006) treats anthropological methods as tools to generate "implications for design," design anthropology reframes the relationship entirely: design and anthropology become co-constitutive practices of inquiry, intervention, and future-making. The field emerged primarily from Danish participatory design traditions and British social anthropology, crystallizing in the 2010s through a series of landmark edited volumes.

## Foundational Works

1. **Clarke, A. J. (Ed.) (2011). Design Anthropology: Object Culture in the 21st Century.** Springer Vienna.
   DOI: 10.1007/978-3-7091-0234-3
   - Early edited volume connecting design anthropology to material culture studies and consumption. Positioned the field at the intersection of design history, social anthropology, and object culture. Includes contributions on IKEA, Papanek's legacy, and materials.

2. **Gunn, W. & Donovan, J. (Eds.) (2012). Design and Anthropology.** Ashgate (Anthropological Studies of Creativity and Perception series).
   ISBN: 978-1-4094-2158-0
   - Framed the field through three orientations: anthropology *for* design, anthropology *of* design, and design *for* anthropology. Established the relational vocabulary that subsequent volumes built upon.

3. **Gunn, W., Otto, T. & Smith, R. C. (Eds.) (2013). Design Anthropology: Theory and Practice.** Bloomsbury Academic, London.
   ISBN: 978-0-8578-5369-1
   - The defining volume for the field. Argued design anthropology constitutes "a distinct style of knowing" (Otto & Smith, ch. 1). Organized around four themes: concepts/methods/practices, materiality, temporality, and relationality. Includes foundational chapters by Gatt & Ingold on correspondence, Halse on ethnographies of the possible, Tunstall on decolonizing design innovation, and Murphy & Marcus on the ethnography–design relationship.

4. **Halse, J., Brandt, E., Clark, B. & Binder, T. (Eds.) (2010). Rehearsing the Future.** The Danish Design School Press, Copenhagen.
   ISBN: 87-92016-16-2
   - Introduced the "Design Anthropological Innovation Model" (DAIM). Positioned design anthropology within the Danish participatory design tradition, framing design as a collaborative process of "rehearsing" possible futures rather than predicting them. Foreword by Melissa Cefkin.

5. **Smith, R. C., Vangkilde, K. T., Kjærsgaard, M. G., Otto, T., Halse, J. & Binder, T. (Eds.) (2016). Design Anthropological Futures.** Bloomsbury Academic, London.
   ISBN: 978-1-4742-8062-4 | DOI: 10.4324/9781003085188
   - Pushed design anthropology toward explicit engagement with futures and future-making. Organized around four sections: Ethnographies of the Possible, Interventionist Speculation, Collaborative Formation of Issues, and Engaging Things. Includes Giaccardi et al.'s "Things as Co-Ethnographers" and Lenskjold & Olander on ontological exploration and inter-species engagement.

## The Key Move: From Ethnography *for* Design to Anthropology *as* Design

6. **Suchman, L. (2011). Anthropological Relocations and the Limits of Design.** *Annual Review of Anthropology*, 40, 1–18.
   DOI: 10.1146/annurev.anthro.041608.105640
   - Argued against reinventing anthropology *as* design; instead called for "a critical anthropology *of* design" that examines design's cultural imaginaries and micropolitics. Positioned design as a problematic object for anthropological inquiry rather than a model for anthropology's future.

7. **Murphy, K. M. (2016). Design and Anthropology.** *Annual Review of Anthropology*, 45, 433–449.
   DOI: 10.1146/annurev-anthro-102215-100224
   - Comprehensive review organizing the field into three configurations: anthropology *of* design (design as ethnographic object), anthropology *for* design (methods mobilized in design process), and design *for* anthropology (design concepts enhancing ethnographic practice). Synthesized Clarke, Gunn et al., and Suchman's perspectives.

8. **Gatt, C. & Ingold, T. (2013). From Description to Correspondence: Anthropology in Real Time.** In Gunn, W., Otto, T. & Smith, R. C. (Eds.), *Design Anthropology: Theory and Practice*, 139–158. Bloomsbury.
   DOI: 10.4324/9781003085195 (chapter 11)
   - Pivotal chapter proposing "correspondence" as the method of design anthropology — moving from retrospective ethnographic *description* to forward-moving *correspondence* with people's lives and aspirations. Argued for anthropology *by means of* design rather than anthropology *of* design.

9. **Ingold, T. (2013). Making: Anthropology, Archaeology, Art and Architecture.** Routledge.
   DOI: 10.4324/9780203559055
   - Proposed that making is a "correspondence between maker and material," rejecting the hylomorphic model (form imposed on matter). Unified anthropology, archaeology, art, and architecture as ways of making/knowing. The concept of correspondence became central to design anthropology's theoretical vocabulary.

## Methods and Methodological Contributions

10. **Halse, J. (2013). Ethnographies of the Possible.** In Gunn, W., Otto, T. & Smith, R. C. (Eds.), *Design Anthropology: Theory and Practice*, 180–196. Bloomsbury.
    - Proposed "ethnographies of the possible" as a design anthropological method — ethnographic inquiry oriented not toward documenting what *is* but toward exploring what *could be*. Extended participatory design's future-orientation with anthropological reflexivity.

11. **Kjærsgaard, M. G. & Otto, T. (2012). Anthropological Fieldwork and Designing Potentials.** In Gunn, W. & Donovan, J. (Eds.), *Design and Anthropology*, 177–191. Ashgate.
    ISBN: 978-1-4094-2158-0
    - Articulated how anthropological fieldwork can be reframed as a generative design practice oriented toward "designing potentials" rather than merely documenting existing practices.

12. **Kjærsgaard, M. G., Knutz, E. & Markussen, T. (2021). Design Games as Fieldwork: Re-visiting Design Games from a Design Anthropological Perspective.** *Design Studies*, 73, 100994.
    DOI: 10.1016/j.destud.2021.100994
    - Reframed participatory design games through a design anthropological lens. Demonstrated how design games function as fieldwork instruments that generate anthropological knowledge while simultaneously advancing design inquiry.

13. **Giaccardi, E., Cila, N., Speed, C. & Caldwell, M. L. (2016). Thing Ethnography: Doing Design Research with Non-Humans.** *Proceedings of DIS '16*, 377–387. ACM.
    DOI: 10.1145/2901790.2901905
    - Introduced "thing ethnography" — equipping objects with sensors to generate ethnographic data from a non-human perspective. Challenged anthropocentric assumptions in both design and anthropology. A key bridge between design anthropology and more-than-human design.

14. **Giaccardi, E., Speed, C., Cila, N. & Caldwell, M. L. (2016). Things as Co-Ethnographers: Implications of a Thing Perspective for Design and Anthropology.** In Smith, R. C. et al. (Eds.), *Design Anthropological Futures*, ch. 15. Bloomsbury.
    DOI: 10.5040/9781474280617.ch-015
    - Extended thing ethnography into a theoretical framework where things serve as co-ethnographers, accessing fields and perspectives invisible to human researchers. Connected design anthropology to IoT, post-humanism, and more-than-human design.

15. **Pink, S. (2014). Digital-Visual-Sensory-Design Anthropology: Ethnography, Imagination and Intervention.** *Arts and Humanities in Higher Education*, 13(4), 412–427.
    DOI: 10.1177/1474022214542353
    - Argued for integrating visual and sensory ethnographic methods with design anthropology's future orientation. Proposed a "digital-visual-sensory" approach as a bridge between applied visual anthropology and design anthropology.

## Textbooks and Comprehensive Introductions

16. **Drazin, A. (2021). Design Anthropology in Context: An Introduction to Design Materiality and Collaborative Thinking.** Routledge.
    ISBN: 978-1-138-91799-6
    - First comprehensive textbook for design anthropology. Organized around design materiality and collaborative knowledge production ("heuristic ways of knowing"). Positioned the field through material culture literature while covering contexts, value, and futures.

17. **Murphy, K. M. (2015). Swedish Design: An Ethnography.** Cornell University Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-8014-5358-2
    - Ethnography of Swedish design culture examining how design forms, discourses, and ideologies shape political worlds. Demonstrated what an "anthropology of design" looks like in practice — design as an object of sustained ethnographic inquiry.

## Decolonizing and Critical Perspectives

18. **Tunstall, E. (Dori). (2013). Decolonizing Design Innovation: Design Anthropology, Critical Anthropology, and Indigenous Knowledge.** In Gunn, W., Otto, T. & Smith, R. C. (Eds.), *Design Anthropology: Theory and Practice*, 232–250. Bloomsbury.
    - Argued that design anthropology must confront its own colonial entanglements. Proposed "respectful design" centered on indigenous knowledge systems and decolonial practice. One of the earliest explicit calls to decolonize design anthropology.

19. **Tunstall, E. (Dori). (2023). Decolonizing Design: A Cultural Justice Guidebook.** MIT Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-262-54395-0
    - Full-length treatment of decolonizing design through a design anthropological lens. Draws on 15+ years of institutional transformation work. Frames decolonization through five principles: putting indigenous first, dismantling tech bias, dismantling racist bias, making amends beyond DEI, and reprioritizing resources.

20. **Clarke, A. J. (ongoing). Design Anthropology: Cold War Industrial Design and Development.** FWF-funded project, University of Applied Arts Vienna.
    URL: https://designanthropology.org
    - Critical historical project examining design anthropology's origins in Cold War development policies and decolonizing nations. Challenges progressive narratives of the field by revealing how social science–design entanglements served geopolitical agendas.

## Design Studies Special Issue on Design Anthropology (2022)

21. **Smith, R. C. (2022). Editorial: Design Anthropology.** *Design Studies*, 80, 101081.
    DOI: 10.1016/j.destud.2022.101081
    - Editorial introducing the first Design Studies special issue on design anthropology. Described the field as a "transformational research field and practice" and argued for widening its scope and reach within the broader design research community.

22. **Kjærsgaard, M. G., Knutz, E. & Markussen, T. (2021). Design Games as Fieldwork.** *Design Studies*, 73, 100994.
    DOI: 10.1016/j.destud.2021.100994
    - (See entry 12 above.) Part of the Design Studies special issue; reframed design games through design anthropological theory.

## CHI / DIS / CSCW Presence

    DOI: 10.1145/2901790.2901905
    - (See entry 13.) The most direct design anthropology paper in a major HCI venue, introducing thing ethnography at DIS.

24. **Kjærsgaard, M. G., Mosleh, W. S., Buur, J. & Sorenson, J. (2021). Anticipating Connectivity in (UX) Design Practices: Reframing Challenges by Introducing Theory Cards.** *EPIC Proceedings*, 159–173.
    DOI: (ISSN 1559-8918)
    - Design anthropological study of UX design departments, deploying "theory cards" as instruments for reframing design problems. Published in EPIC (Ethnographic Praxis in Industry Conference), a key venue bridging design anthropology and HCI/industry practice.

25. **Lenskjold, T. U. & Olander, S. (2016). Design Anthropology as Ontological Exploration and Inter-Species Engagement.** In Smith, R. C. et al. (Eds.), *Design Anthropological Futures*, ch. 16. Bloomsbury.
    DOI: 10.4324/9781003085188
    - Extended design anthropology toward more-than-human and inter-species engagement, connecting to posthumanist and multispecies HCI agendas. A key bridge between design anthropology and the more-than-human design lineage.

26. **Sanchez, C., Wang, S., Savolainen, K., Epp, F. A. & Salovaara, A. (2025). Let's Talk Futures: A Literature Review of HCI's Future-Orientation.** *CHI '25*.
    DOI: 10.1145/3706598.3713759
    - Comprehensive review mapping futuring in HCI across CHI, DIS, CSCW, UIST and other venues. Documents the growing influence of design anthropological futures thinking within mainstream HCI research.

## Lineage Connections

### → Ethnography-in-Design
Design anthropology diverges from the ethnography-in-design tradition (Suchman 1987; Blomberg et al. 1993; Crabtree et al. 2009) in a fundamental way: where ethnography-in-design treats anthropological methods as data-gathering tools that *inform* design decisions, design anthropology reframes the entire relationship. The key move is from ethnography as a *service* to design toward anthropology and design as *co-constitutive* practices. Gatt & Ingold's (2013) shift from "description" to "correspondence" captures this precisely — design anthropology does not describe practices for designers to act upon, but corresponds with people in the ongoing making of their worlds.

### → Participatory Design
Design anthropology shares deep roots with Scandinavian participatory design (PD), especially through the Danish institutional connections (Royal Danish Academy, Aarhus University). Halse et al.'s (2010) *Rehearsing the Future* and Binder's involvement in both PD and design anthropology exemplify this link. However, design anthropology brings anthropological theoretical resources (material culture, temporality, ontology) that expand PD beyond its democratic-political framing toward broader questions of knowledge production and cultural inquiry.

### → More-than-Human Design
Giaccardi et al.'s (2016) thing ethnography and Lenskjold & Olander's (2016) inter-species design anthropology provide direct bridges to the more-than-human design lineage. The move from human-centered ethnography to "things as co-ethnographers" parallels and feeds into posthumanist and multispecies approaches in HCI.

### → Speculative Design
Design anthropology's concern with "ethnographies of the possible" (Halse 2013) and "design anthropological futures" (Smith et al. 2016) connects to speculative and critical design, but with a crucial difference: where speculative design often works through provocation and critique, design anthropology grounds future-making in ethnographic engagement with actual communities and their lived aspirations.

### → Material Culture Studies / STS
Through Drazin (UCL), Clarke (Vienna), and the broader British social anthropology tradition, design anthropology maintains strong connections to material culture studies and STS. The field's concern with objects, materials, and their social lives draws directly from this intellectual heritage.

### → Decolonizing Design
Tunstall's work (2013, 2023) positions design anthropology as a site where decolonial practice and design intersect. This connects to the broader decolonizing design movement while grounding it in anthropological traditions of reflexivity and cultural critique.
