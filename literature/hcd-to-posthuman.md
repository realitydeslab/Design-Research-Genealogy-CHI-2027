## Foundations of Human-Centered Design

1. **Norman, D. A. (1988/2013). The Design of Everyday Things.** Basic Books. (Originally *The Psychology of Everyday Things*; revised 2013.)
   ISBN: 978-0-465-05065-9
   URL: https://mitpress.mit.edu/author/donald-a-norman/
   - The canonical HCD text. Usability, affordances, and feedback centered on human cognition. The 2013 edition explicitly adopts "human-centered design."

2. **Norman, D. A. (2005). Human-Centered Design Considered Harmful.** *Interactions*, 12(4), 14–19.
   DOI: 10.1145/1070960.1070976
   - Norman's own critique: HCD focuses too narrowly on individual users at the expense of activity-centered and systems-level concerns. HCD questioned from within.

3. **ISO 9241-210:2019. Ergonomics of Human-System Interaction — Part 210: Human-Centred Design for Interactive Systems.** International Organization for Standardization.
   URL: https://www.iso.org/standard/77520.html
   - International standard defining HCD process. Codifies the iterative cycle. Represents institutional consolidation of HCD.

4. **IDEO (2015). The Field Guide to Human-Centered Design.** IDEO.org.
   URL: https://www.designkit.org/resources/1
   - Popular practitioner guide; disseminated HCD methodology globally. HCD's expansion from academy to industry standard.

5. **Gould, J. D., & Lewis, C. (1985). Designing for Usability: Key Principles and What Designers Think.** *Communications of the ACM*, 28(3), 300–311.
   DOI: 10.1145/3166.3170
   - Early user-centered design principles: early focus on users, empirical measurement, iterative design.

6. **Nielsen, J. (1993). Usability Engineering.** Morgan Kaufmann.
   ISBN: 978-0-12-518406-9
   URL: https://www.sciencedirect.com/book/9780125184069/usability-engineering
   - Formalized usability as measurable property of systems; established user-centered evaluation paradigm.

7. **Shneiderman, B. (1986). Designing the User Interface: Strategies for Effective Human-Computer Interaction.** Addison-Wesley.
   ISBN: 978-0-201-16505-7
   - Interface design principles centered on human cognitive capabilities.

8. **Greenbaum, J., & Kyng, M. (Eds.) (1991). Design at Work: Cooperative Design of Computer Systems.** Lawrence Erlbaum.
   ISBN: 978-0-8058-0612-2
   - Participatory design tradition; Scandinavian root that pushed HCD toward democratic inclusion.

## Critiques from Within HCI

9. **Bannon, L. J. (1991). From Human Factors to Human Actors.** In J. Greenbaum & M. Kyng (Eds.), *Design at Work*, 25–44.
   ISBN: 978-0-8058-0612-2
   - Early critique of passive "human factors" model; argued for humans as active agents, not just factors to be optimized.

10. **Dourish, P. (2006). Implications for Design.** *Proceedings of CHI '06*, 541–550.
    DOI: 10.1145/1124772.1124855
    - Critiqued HCD's demand for "implications for design" from ethnography; argued this reduces ethnography to a service function.

11. **Harrison, S., Tatar, D., & Sengers, P. (2007). The Three Paradigms of HCI.** *Proceedings of alt.chi '07*.
    URL: https://people.cs.vt.edu/~srh/Downloads/TheThreeParadigmsofHCI.pdf
    - HCI's three paradigms: engineering, cognitive science, phenomenological. HCD spans the first two; more-than-human design emerges from critiques of all three.

12. **Bødker, S. (2006). When Second Wave HCI Meets Third Wave Challenges.** *Proceedings of NordiCHI '06*, 1–8.
    DOI: 10.1145/1182475.1182476
    - Shift from workplace-focused second-wave HCI to experience-oriented third wave. More-than-human design represents a further wave.

## The Posthuman Turn in Design

13. **Forlano, L. (2017). Posthumanism and Design.** *She Ji: The Journal of Design, Economics, and Innovation*, 3(1), 16–29.
    DOI: 10.1016/j.sheji.2017.08.001
    - Mapped posthumanism's implications for design. Key articulation of the move beyond HCD, drawing on feminist new materialism and STS.

14. **Forlano, L. (2016). Decentering the Human in the Design of Collaborative Cities.** *Design Issues*, 32(3), 42–54.
    DOI: 10.1162/DESI_a_00398
    - Applied posthumanist thinking to urban design; explicitly decenters the human subject at HCD's core.

15. **Giaccardi, E., & Redström, J. (2020). Technology and More-Than-Human Design.** *Design Issues*, 36(4), 33–44.
    DOI: 10.1162/desi_a_00612
    - Named and theorized "more-than-human design" as deliberate departure from HCD, taking seriously the agency of technologies.

16. **Wakkary, R. (2021). Things We Could Design: For More Than Human-Centered Worlds.** MIT Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-262-04468-6
    URL: https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262544221/things-we-could-design/
    - Book-length argument for design beyond human-centeredness. Critiques HCD's assumption that design serves only human purposes.

17. **Coulton, P., & Lindley, J. (2019). More-Than Human Centred Design: Considering Other Things.** *The Design Journal*, 22(4), 463–481.
    DOI: 10.1080/14606925.2019.1614320
    - Object-Oriented Ontology applied to design; flat ontology where things have equal ontological status.

18. **Frauenberger, C. (2019). Entanglement HCI The Next Wave?** *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 27(1), Article 2.
    DOI: 10.1145/3364998
    - "Entanglement HCI" as post-humanist wave, drawing on Barad's agential realism. Most explicit articulation of HCI after HCD.

## Philosophical Foundations for the Shift

19. **Latour, B. (2005). Reassembling the Social: An Introduction to Actor-Network-Theory.** Oxford University Press.
    DOI: 10.1093/oso/9780199256044.001.0001
    - ANT's flat ontology — human and nonhuman actors symmetrically — provides theoretical basis for moving beyond HCD.

20. **Haraway, D. J. (2016). Staying with the Trouble: Making Kin in the Chthulucene.** Duke University Press.
    DOI: 10.1215/9780822373780
    - Posthumanist multispecies entanglements; widely cited philosophical resource for more-than-human design.

21. **Barad, K. (2007). Meeting the Universe Halfway: Quantum Physics and the Entanglement of Matter and Meaning.** Duke University Press.
    DOI: 10.1215/9780822388128
    - "Intra-action" and "agential realism": human and nonhuman agencies emerge together. Challenges HCD's ontological separation of human subject and designed object.

22. **Braidotti, R. (2013). The Posthuman.** Polity Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-7456-4158-4
    URL: https://www.wiley.com/en-us/The+Posthuman-p-9780745641584
    - Maps the posthuman condition; philosophical vocabulary for design beyond humanist assumptions.

## Design Responses and Methods

23. **Clarke, R., Heitlinger, S., Light, A., Forlano, L., Foth, M., & DiSalvo, C. (2019). More-than-Human Participation: Design for Sustainable Smart City Futures.** *Interactions*, 26(3), 60–63.
    DOI: 10.1145/3319075
    - Participation extended beyond human stakeholders to nonhuman entities in smart city design.

24. **Akama, Y., Light, A., & Kamihira, T. (2020). Expanding Participation to Design with More-Than-Human Concerns.** *Proceedings of PDC '20*, 1–11.
    DOI: 10.1145/3385010.3385016
    - Extended participatory design from human-centered to more-than-human; practical methodology for the shift.

## Recent Developments (2020–2026)

25. **Coskun, A., Cila, N., Nicenboim, I., Frauenberger, C., Wakkary, R., Hassenzahl, M., Mancini, C., Giaccardi, E., & Forlano, L. (2022). More-than-Human Concepts, Methodologies, and Practices in HCI.** *CHI '22 Extended Abstracts*.
    DOI: 10.1145/3491101.3516503
    - Major CHI 2022 panel bringing together all key more-than-human design researchers. Mapped concepts, methodologies, and practices.

26. **Coskun, A., Cila, N., Nicenboim, I., et al. (2023). More-Than-Human Perspectives and Values in Human-Computer Interaction.** *CHI '23 Extended Abstracts*.
    DOI: 10.1145/3544549.3583174
    - Follow-up CHI 2023 workshop extending the more-than-human HCI agenda to values and ethics.

27. **Coskun, A., Cila, N., Nicenboim, I., et al. (2024). The Making(s) of More-than-Human Design: Introduction to the Special Issue on More-than-Human Design and HCI.** *Human–Computer Interaction*, 1–24.
    DOI: 10.1080/07370024.2024.2353357
    - Special issue introduction synthesizing the field; the most comprehensive recent overview of more-than-human design in HCI.

28. **Barendregt, W., et al. (2025). The Future of More-Than-Human Design: A Computing Practice in Crisis?** *Adjunct Proceedings of the Aarhus Conference*.
    DOI: 10.1145/3737609.3747092
    - Critical reflection on more-than-human design's future — questioning whether it has reached a crisis point as a computing practice.

29. **Wakkary, R., et al. (2025). Backyard Practices: A Liminal Approach to Designing in More-than-Human Worlds.** *Proceedings of CHI '25*.
    DOI: 10.1145/3706598.3713291
    - "Backyard practices" as liminal design approach — designing at the threshold between human domestic space and more-than-human worlds.

30. **Wakkary, R., et al. (2026). Living Probes in Place: Exploring More-Than-Human Care Through Mycoremediation.** *Proceedings of CHI '26*.
    DOI: 10.1145/3772318.3791217
    - "Living probes" — design probes that are literally alive (fungi). Extends design research methodology into more-than-human territory.

31. **Wakkary, R., et al. (2024). Unmaking and HCI: Techniques, Technologies, Materials, and Philosophies beyond Making.** *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 31(6).
    DOI: 10.1145/3689047
    - "Unmaking" challenges HCD's implicit productivist logic; decay and disassembly as design practices.

32. **Wakkary, R., et al. (2024). Caring through: Engaging with Temporality of Care in More-than-Human Design.** *Proceedings of Halfway to the Future Symposium*.
    DOI: 10.1145/3686169.3686211
    - Care ethics applied to more-than-human design; temporality as a design concern beyond human timescales.

## Connections to Other Lineages

- **Participatory Design**: PD expanded HCD's "user" to "community"; more-than-human design further expands to "nonhuman"
- **Constructionism / RtD**: HCD frames design as serving human needs; RtD reframes as knowledge production, opening space for posthuman concerns
- **Prototyping as Research**: More-than-human prototyping includes "living probes" and "research products" that develop their own agencies
- **Thing Ethnography**: A practical method for moving beyond HCD's human-centered observation
- **Material Speculation**: Constructing artifacts that resist human-centered assumptions

## Summary

Human-Centered Design (Norman 1988; ISO 9241) established the dominant paradigm placing human needs at design's center. Through internal critiques (Norman 2005; Dourish 2006; Bødker 2006) and external philosophical challenges (Latour 2005; Haraway 2016; Barad 2007; Braidotti 2013), the field moved toward posthuman orientations. More-than-Human Design (Forlano 2017; Giaccardi & Redström 2020; Wakkary 2021) represents the explicit departure, arguing that design must account for nonhuman agencies — technologies, animals, ecosystems, materials.

The trajectory accelerated dramatically from 2020 onward: CHI panels and workshops (Coskun et al. 2022, 2023), a dedicated special issue in Human–Computer Interaction journal (2024), and an increasing body of design practice involving living systems (Wakkary et al. 2026). Recent critical reflections (Barendregt et al. 2025) now ask whether more-than-human design has reached a crisis point — suggesting the field has matured enough to generate self-critical discourse. The genealogical line runs clearly: HCD → internal critique → posthumanist philosophy → more-than-human design → institutionalization and self-reflection.
