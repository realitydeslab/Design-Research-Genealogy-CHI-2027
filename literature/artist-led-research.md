# Literature Review: Artist-led / Performance-led Research

## Overview

Artist-led and performance-led research represents a distinctive lineage within design research where creative practice — performance, installation, musical experience, interactive art — serves as the primary vehicle for generating HCI knowledge. Rather than studying users or building prototypes for evaluation, researchers in this tradition create, tour, and study public artworks and performances that demonstrate future possibilities while revealing profound challenges for human-computer interaction.

This lineage has three intertwined strands: (1) Steve Benford's performance-led research program at Nottingham's Mixed Reality Laboratory, conducted through 20+ years of collaboration with artists including Blast Theory; (2) Bill Gaver's ludic design and the Interaction Research Studio's "showroom" tradition at Goldsmiths/Northumbria, which produces provocative designed artifacts for deployment in everyday life; and (3) Kristina Höök's soma design at KTH, which centers the body and somaesthetic experience as the basis for interaction design. All three connect to a broader epistemological tradition of practice-based/practice-as research (Nelson, Haseman, Borgdorff) that argues artistic practice constitutes a legitimate mode of knowledge production.

The Design Research Observatory does not list artist-led research as a distinct concept, but it is implicitly present in the "showroom" tradition of constructive design research (Koskinen et al. 2011) and bridges RtD, speculative design, and more-than-human design.

## Steve Benford's Research Program

### Performance-Led Research Method

1. **Benford, S., Adams, M., Tandavanitj, N., Row Farr, J., Greenhalgh, C., Crabtree, A., Flintham, M., Walker, B., Marshall, J., Koleva, B., Rennick Egglestone, S., & Giannachi, G. (2013). Performance-Led Research in the Wild.** *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 20(3), 1-22.
   DOI: 10.1145/2491500.2491502
   - The methodological manifesto. Defines performance-led research as creating, touring, and studying public performances and installations as HCI research. Based on 10+ years of Blast Theory collaborations. Argues that artistic performances in the wild reveal interaction challenges that lab studies cannot.

2. **Benford, S. & Giannachi, G. (2011). Performing Mixed Reality.** MIT Press.
   ISBN: 978-0-262-01576-9
   - Book-length treatment of the Nottingham-Blast Theory collaboration. Documents how mixed reality performances serve as research vehicles, evolving a distinctive interdisciplinary approach combining artistic practice with HCI research.

### Trajectories and Experience Design

3. **Benford, S., Giannachi, G., Koleva, B. & Rodden, T. (2009). From Interaction to Trajectories: Designing Coherent Journeys through User Experiences.** *Proceedings of CHI '09*, 709-718.
   DOI: 10.1145/1518701.1518812
   - Introduced the "trajectories" framework — designing coherent journeys through user experiences that span time, space, interfaces, and roles. Drawn from performance experience; became influential beyond art contexts.

4. **Benford, S. & Giannachi, G. (2008). Temporal Trajectories in Shared Interactive Narratives.** *Proceedings of CHI '08*, 73-82.
   DOI: 10.1145/1357054.1357067
   - Earlier articulation of temporal trajectories in shared narrative experiences. Foundation for the 2009 framework.

### Uncomfortable Interactions and Provocation

5. **Benford, S., Greenhalgh, C., Giannachi, G., Walker, B., Marshall, J. & Rodden, T. (2012). Uncomfortable Interactions.** *Proceedings of CHI '12*, 2005-2014.
   DOI: 10.1145/2207676.2208347
   - **CHI Best Paper (top 1%).** Argues for deliberately and systematically creating uncomfortable interactions as part of powerful cultural experiences. Identifies tactics: visceral discomfort, cultural discomfort, control challenges, and intimacy. Directly challenges the usability paradigm.

6. **Hazzard, A., Greenhalgh, C., Kallionpaa, M., Benford, S., Veinberg, A., Kanga, Z. & McPherson, A. (2019). Failing with Style: Designing for Aesthetic Failure in Interactive Performance.** *Proceedings of CHI '19*, 1-14.
   DOI: 10.1145/3290605.3300260
   - Extends uncomfortable interactions into musical performance. "Aesthetic failure" as a deliberate design strategy — failure becomes expressive rather than problematic.

### Sensing and Interaction Frameworks

7. **Benford, S., Schnädelbach, H., Koleva, B., Anastasi, R., Greenhalgh, C., Rodden, T., Green, J., Ghali, A., Pridmore, T., Gaver, B., Boucher, A., Walker, B., Pennington, S., Schmidt, A., Gellersen, H. & Steed, A. (2005). Expected, Sensed, and Desired: A Framework for Designing Sensing-Based Interaction.** *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 12(1), 3-30.
   DOI: 10.1145/1057237.1057239
   - Introduces a design framework comparing expected physical movements with those sensed by computers and desired by applications. Applied to pointing flashlights, the Augurscope II, and the Drift Table. Bridges artistic and systematic design approaches.

### Ambiguity and Meaning-Making

8. **Gaver, W., Beaver, J. & Benford, S. (2003). Ambiguity as a Resource for Design.** *Proceedings of CHI '03*, 233-240.
   DOI: 10.1145/642611.642653
   - Seminal paper arguing that ambiguity in design is not a deficiency but a resource. Identifies information ambiguity, context ambiguity, and relationship ambiguity. Foundational for both Benford's and Gaver's programs.

9. **Sivertsen, C., et al. (2024). Machine Learning Processes As Sources of Ambiguity: Insights from AI Art.** *Proceedings of CHI '24*.
   - Extends ambiguity framework to AI/ML contexts, connecting the artist-led tradition to contemporary AI research.

### Embodied and Soma-Connected Work

10. **Benford, S., et al. (2021). Contesting Control: Journeys through Surrender, Self-Awareness and Looseness of Control in Embodied Interaction.** *Human-Computer Interaction*, Taylor & Francis.
   DOI: 10.1080/07370024.2020.1754214
   - Maps the design space of control in embodied interaction. Connects Benford's performance tradition with Höök's soma design.

11. **Höök, K., et al. (2021). Unpacking Non-Dualistic Design: The Soma Design Case.** *ACM Transactions on CHI*.
    DOI: 10.1145/3462448
    - Joint work between Höök and Benford teams. Articulates soma design as non-dualistic (refusing mind-body split). Shows convergence of performance-led and body-centered research.

12. **Tennent, P., et al. (2021). Articulating Soma Experiences Using Trajectories.** *Proceedings of CHI '21*.
    DOI: 10.1145/3411764.3445482
    - **Honourable Mention (top 5%).** Explicitly bridges Benford's trajectories framework with Höök's soma design. Shows how these two strands are converging into "somabotics."

13. **Marshall, J., Benford, S., Byrne, R. & Tennent, P. (2019). Sensory Alignment in Immersive Entertainment.** *Proceedings of CHI '19*, 1-13.
    DOI: 10.1145/3290605.3300930
    - Maps how sensory modalities align and misalign in immersive entertainment experiences. Connects embodied design to the performance tradition.

### Recent: AI, Multispecies, and Ethics

14. **Schneiders, E., et al. (2024). Designing Multispecies Worlds for Robots, Cats and Humans.** *Proceedings of CHI '24*.
    URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.15431
    - Extends artist-led research into multispecies design. Connects to more-than-human design lineage.

15. **Benford, S., et al. (2024). Charting Ethical Tensions in Multispecies Technology Research through Beneficiary-Epistemology Space.** *Proceedings of CHI '24*.
    URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.15439
    - Ethical framework for artist-led multispecies research. Maps the ethical dimensions of performance-led research as a method.

16. **Benford, S., et al. (2015). The Ethical Implications of HCI's Turn to the Cultural.** *ACM Transactions on CHI*.
    DOI: 10.1145/2775107
    - Foundational paper on ethics in cultural HCI research. Addresses what happens when HCI research enters artistic/cultural domains.

## Blast Theory & Pervasive Games as Research

17. **Koleva, B., Taylor, I., Benford, S., Fraser, M., Greenhalgh, C., Schnädelbach, H., vom Lehn, D., Row-Farr, J. & Adams, M. (2001). Orchestrating a Mixed Reality Performance.** *Proceedings of CHI '01*, 38-45.
    DOI: 10.1145/365024.365033
    - Study of Desert Rain (1999), the first major Benford–Blast Theory collaboration. Reveals how performers orchestrate player engagement across physical and virtual sets, with a water-spray screen acting as a traversable interface.

18. **Flintham, M., Anastasi, R., Benford, S., Hemmings, T., Crabtree, A., Greenhalgh, C., Rodden, T., Tandavanitj, N., Adams, M. & Row-Farr, J. (2003). Where On-Line Meets On-The-Streets: Experiences with Mobile Mixed Reality Games.** *Proceedings of CHI '03*, 569-576.
    DOI: 10.1145/642611.642710
    - Documents "Can You See Me Now?" — the landmark mixed reality chase game played simultaneously online and on city streets. Prix Ars Electronica Golden Nica winner (2003).

19. **Benford, S., Crabtree, A., Flintham, M., Drozd, A., Anastasi, R., Paxton, M., Tandavanitj, N., Adams, M. & Row-Farr, J. (2006). Can You See Me Now?** *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 13(1), 100-133.
    DOI: 10.1145/1143518.1143522
    - Extended TOCHI treatment of the landmark mixed reality game. Detailed analysis of how online players and street-based runners interact across virtual and physical spaces.

20. **Benford, S., Anastasi, R., Flintham, M., Greenhalgh, C., Tandavanitj, N., Adams, M. & Row-Farr, J. (2003). Coping with Uncertainty in a Location-Based Game.** *IEEE Pervasive Computing*, 2(3), 34-41.
    - Analysis of how players cope with technical uncertainty (GPS drift, network latency) in "Can You See Me Now?" — uncertainty becomes a design resource rather than a problem.

21. **Benford, S., Crabtree, A., Reeves, S., Sheridan, J., Dix, A., Flintham, M. & Drozd, A. (2006). The Frame of the Game: Blurring the Boundary between Fiction and Reality in Mobile Experiences.** *Proceedings of CHI '06*, 427-436.
    - Theoretical framework for understanding the blurred boundary between fiction and reality in pervasive games. Draws on Goffman's frame analysis.

22. **Benford, S., Magerkurth, C. & Ljungstrand, P. (2005). Bridging the Physical and Digital in Pervasive Gaming.** *Communications of the ACM*, 48(3), 54-57.
    - Overview of the emerging pervasive gaming field and its implications for HCI.

## Ludic Design & the Showroom Tradition (Gaver)

23. **Gaver, W., Dunne, T. & Pacenti, E. (1999). Cultural Probes.** *Interactions*, 6(1), 21-29.
    DOI: 10.1145/291224.291235
    - Introduced cultural probes — designed packages of maps, postcards, cameras sent to participants to provoke imaginative responses. One of the most influential methods in design research. Bridges participatory and artistic approaches.

24. **Gaver, W., Boucher, A., Pennington, S. & Walker, B. (2004). Cultural Probes and the Value of Uncertainty.** *Interactions*, 11(5), 53-56.
    DOI: 10.1145/1015530.1015555
    - Defends the artistic, ambiguous nature of cultural probes against attempts to "rationalize" them into systematic tools.

25. **Gaver, W., Bowers, J., Boucher, A., Gellerson, H., Pennington, S., Schmidt, A., Steed, A., Villars, N. & Walker, B. (2004). The Drift Table: Designing for Ludic Engagement.** *Proceedings of CHI '04 Design Expo*.
    - The Drift Table — a coffee table with aerial photography viewed through a peephole, navigated by shifting weight. Paradigmatic "ludic design" artifact.

26. **Gaver, W., Bowers, J., Boucher, A., Law, A., Pennington, S. & Walker, B. (2007). Electronic Furniture for the Curious Home: Assessing Ludic Designs in the Field.** *International Journal of Human-Computer Studies*, 65(2), 128-152.
    - Long-term field deployment of ludic designs. Documents how provocative artifacts function in everyday domestic life over months.

27. **Gaver, W. (2011). Making Spaces: How Design Workbooks Work.** *Proceedings of CHI '11*, 1551-1560.
    DOI: 10.1145/1978942.1979169
    - **Honourable Mention.** Introduces design workbooks as a method for exploring design spaces through juxtaposition of proposals, precedents, and provocations. Documents how the Interaction Research Studio uses workbooks to open creative possibilities.

28. **Gaver, W. (2012). What Should We Expect from Research Through Design?** *Proceedings of CHI '12*.
    DOI: 10.1145/2207676.2208538
    - Argues RtD should resist convergence and standardization. Proposes "annotated portfolios" — theory as annotation of realized design examples — as the appropriate knowledge form.

29. **Gaver, W. & Bowers, J. (2012). Annotated Portfolios.** *Interactions*, 19(4), 40-49.
    DOI: 10.1145/2212877.2212889
    - Formalizes annotated portfolios as an intermediate knowledge form for design research. Design artifacts + reflective annotation = knowledge contribution.

30. **Gaver, W. (2022). The Presence Project (2nd ed.).** Goldsmiths Press.
    ISBN: 978-1-912685-46-2
    - Documents the Presence Project's development of key concepts: cultural probes, design workbooks, and speculative design. With Phoebe Sengers.

### Research Products (Odom)

31. **Odom, W., Wakkary, R., Lim, Y., Desjardins, A., Hengeveld, B. & Banks, R. (2016). From Research Prototype to Research Product.** *Proceedings of CHI '16*, 2549-2561.
    DOI: 10.1145/2858036.2858447
    - Proposes the "research product" as an evolution of the research prototype. Articulates four qualities: inquiry-driven, finish, fit, and independent. Connects to Gaver's deployed artifacts tradition.

32. **Odom, W., Wakkary, R., Hol, Y. & Thorn, J. (2022). Extending a Theory of Slow Technology for Design through Artifact Analysis.** *Human–Computer Interaction*, 37(2), 150-179.
    DOI: 10.1080/07370024.2021.1913416
    - Extends slow technology theory through analysis of deployed design artifacts. Connects the showroom tradition to long-term, reflective interaction design.

### Reflective Design (Sengers)

33. **Sengers, P., Boehner, K., David, S. & Kaye, J. (2005). Reflective Design.** *Proceedings of the 4th Decennial Aarhus Conference on Critical Computing*, 49-58.
    DOI: 10.1145/1094562.1094569
    - Introduced "reflective design" — designing technology that encourages users to reflect on their own values and assumptions. Bridges critical theory and design practice.

## Soma Design & Embodied Research (Höök)

34. **Höök, K. (2018). Designing with the Body: Somaesthetic Interaction Design.** MIT Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-262-03856-0
    - The definitive book on soma design. Outlines theory, motivations, methods, and tools. Draws on Shusterman's somaesthetics (pragmatist aesthetics of the body). Concludes with "A Soma Design Manifesto" challenging designers to focus on bodies and perception rather than reasoning and intellect.

35. **Höök, K., et al. (2017). Soma-Based Design Theory.** *Proceedings of CHI '17*, 550-557.
    DOI: 10.1145/3025453.3025504
    - Foundational CHI paper articulating soma design theory — designing with and for the felt, living body.

36. **Höök, K., et al. (2016). Move to Be Moved.** *Proceedings of CHI '16*, 3301-3308.
    - Design explorations in body-movement as interaction. Shows how soma design methods generate novel interaction possibilities.

37. **Höök, K. & Löwgren, J. (2012). Strong Concepts: Intermediate-Level Knowledge in Interaction Design Research.** *ACM Transactions on CHI*, 19(3), 1-18.
    DOI: 10.1145/2362364.2362371
    - Proposes "strong concepts" as intermediate knowledge form (parallel to Gaver & Bowers' annotated portfolios). Both address the question: what kind of knowledge does design research produce?

## Practice-Based Research Epistemology

38. **Frayling, C. (1993). Research in Art and Design.** *Royal College of Art Research Papers*, 1(1), 1-5.
    URL: https://researchonline.rca.ac.uk/384/
    - The foundational taxonomy: research into/through/for art and design. "Research through design" became the basis for RtD as a field. All practice-based and artist-led research traditions trace epistemological lineage to this paper.

39. **Nelson, R. (2013). Practice as Research in the Arts: Principles, Protocols, Pedagogies, Resistances.** Palgrave Macmillan.
    ISBN: 978-1-137-28290-2
    - Comprehensive framework for practice-as-research (PaR). Defines the practitioner-researcher model. Key reference for understanding how artistic practice constitutes legitimate academic inquiry.

40. **Haseman, B. (2006). A Manifesto for Performative Research.** *Media International Australia*, 118(1), 98-106.
    DOI: 10.1177/1329878X0611800113
    - Argues performative research is not merely qualitative research applied to arts — it is a third paradigm with its own protocols, principles, and validation procedures. The research output is the practice itself.

41. **Borgdorff, H. (2012). The Conflict of the Faculties: Perspectives on Artistic Research and Academia.** Leiden University Press.
    ISBN: 978-90-8728-162-7
    - Philosophical grounding for artistic research in the academy. Distinguishes research on/for/through the arts (paralleling Frayling's in/through/for taxonomy).

42. **Gray, C. (1996). Inquiry through Practice: Developing Appropriate Research Strategies.** *No Guru, No Method?* UIAH, Helsinki.
    URL: http://www.carolegray.net/Papers%20PDFs/ngnm.pdf
    - Early articulation of practice-based research in art and design. Foundational text in the UK tradition. Argues creative practice is a legitimate research strategy with methods stemming from artist/designer working practices.

43. **Candy, L. & Edmonds, E. (2018). Practice-Based Research in the Creative Arts: Foundations and Futures from the Front Line.** *Leonardo*, 51(1), 63-69.
    DOI: 10.1162/LEON_a_01471
    - Synthesizes decades of work on practice-based research methodology. Clarifies the nature of PBR and its contribution to new knowledge in creative arts.

44. **Candy, L. & Edmonds, E. (2002). Explorations in Art and Technology.** Springer-Verlag.
    DOI: 10.1007/978-1-4471-0197-0
    - Documents the creative process through artist-technologist collaborations. Bridges computational arts and HCI.

45. **Candy, L. & Edmonds, E. (2011). Interacting: Art, Research and the Creative Practitioner.** Libri Publishing.
    ISBN: 978-1-907471-48-3
    - Extended treatment of how art practice and research intertwine in computational arts.

## Performance & Games as Research

46. **Johansson, K., Robinson, R., Back, J., Bowman, S.L., Fey, J., Márquez Segura, E., Waern, A. & Isbister, K. (2024). Why Larp? A Synthesis Article on Live Action Roleplay in Relation to HCI Research and Practice.** *ACM Transactions on CHI*.

47. **Waern, A. & Back, J. (2015). Experimental Game Design.** In Lankoski & Björk (Eds.), *Game Research Methods: An Overview*, ETC Press, Ch. 20.

## The Showroom Tradition (Koskinen et al.)

57. **Koskinen, I., Zimmerman, J., Binder, T., Redström, J. & Wensveen, S. (2011). Design Research through Practice: From the Lab, Field, and Showroom.** Morgan Kaufmann/Elsevier.
    ISBN: 978-0-12-385502-2
    - Classifies constructive design research into lab, field, and showroom traditions. The **showroom** tradition — design research that produces exhibitions, performances, and public provocations — is where artist-led research sits. Gaver, Dunne & Raby, and Benford exemplify this tradition.

## Connections to Other Lineages

| Connected Lineage | Nature of Connection |
|---|---|
| **Research through Design** | Artist-led research is RtD's most radical expression — the "showroom" tradition. Benford's performance-led research and Gaver's ludic design are paradigm cases of RtD. |
| **Speculative/Critical Design** | Dunne & Raby's gallery-based provocations share the showroom tradition. Gaver co-authored cultural probes with Dunne. Gaver's ambiguity paper with Benford bridges both. |
| **More-than-Human Design** | Benford's recent multispecies work (robots, cats, humans) extends artist-led methods to more-than-human contexts. |
| **Soma Design** | Höök-Benford convergence (non-dualistic design, soma trajectories, somabotics) creates a new synthesis of body-centered and performance-led approaches. |
| **Material Speculation** | Wakkary's counterfactual artifacts parallel Gaver's deployed research artifacts, but material speculation foregrounds everyday use rather than exhibition. |
| **Slow Technology** | Gaver's long-term field deployments (electronic furniture for the curious home) connect to Hallnäs & Redström's slow technology. |
| **Participatory Design** | Cultural probes bridge participatory and artistic approaches. Pervasive games involve public participation. |
| **Design Fiction** | Performance-led research creates lived fictions (Blast Theory's mixed reality games). Connects to Lindley's anticipatory ethnography. |
| **Infrastructural Speculation** | Benford's "ethical implications of HCI's turn to the cultural" connects to Wong's values-oriented design, though from an artistic rather than policy perspective. |
| **Constructionism** | Practice-based research epistemology (Nelson, Haseman) parallels constructionism — both argue that making/doing produces knowledge. But PaR foregrounds artistic practice specifically. |
| **Prototyping as Research** | Gaver's research artifacts and Benford's performances are extreme forms of prototyping — the "prototype" IS the research, not a step toward a product. |

## Implications for the Genealogy

**Branching point:** Artist-led research branches from RtD at the "showroom" node (Koskinen et al. 2011), which was already implicit in Gaver's cultural probes (1999) and Benford's Blast Theory collaborations (from Desert Rain 1999 onward). The epistemological grounding comes from practice-based research in the arts (Haseman 2006, Borgdorff 2012, Nelson 2013), which provides the theoretical justification for why artistic practice constitutes research.

**Current convergence:** The Benford-Höök convergence (soma trajectories, non-dualistic design, somabotics) represents a major current synthesis, creating a new tradition that combines performance-led provocation with body-centered design. This is one of the most active branching points in contemporary design research.

**Key insight for the genealogy:** Artist-led research reveals that the showroom tradition is not a single lineage but a family of approaches (ludic design, performance-led research, speculative design) that share an exhibition/provocation orientation but differ in their relationship to artistic practice. Benford's work is the most explicitly artist-led; Gaver's is designer-led but artistically informed; Dunne & Raby's is design-as-art-provocation. The genealogy should distinguish these sub-strands within the showroom family.
