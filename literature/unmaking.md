# Literature Review: Unmaking

## Overview

Unmaking is an emerging concept in HCI and design research that challenges the field's foundational orientation toward making, creation, and novelty. Where design research traditionally asks "how do we make new things?", unmaking asks "what about the converse — the disassembly, decay, destruction, repair, and afterlives of what already exists?" The concept draws together threads from repair/maintenance studies, sustainable interaction design, posthuman design, material exploration, and participatory design into a coherent counter-narrative to making-as-progress.

Unmaking has three distinct but converging strands: (1) **repair and broken world thinking** (Jackson 2014) — foregrounding maintenance and breakdown as central to technological experience; (2) **material unmaking** (Song & Paulos 2021; Bell et al.) — designing for biodegradation, decay, and deformation as creative materials; and (3) **un/making as political practice** (Lindström & Ståhl 2020) — questioning progressivist imaginaries in design, connecting to feminist technoscience and decolonial design.

The concept is genealogically significant because it inverts the epistemological foundation of RtD: if making = knowing (constructionism), then **unmaking = a different kind of knowing** — knowledge produced through disassembly, decay, repair, and letting-go rather than through creation.

## Strand 1: Repair and Broken World Thinking

1. **Jackson, S.J. (2014). Rethinking Repair.** In T. Gillespie, P.J. Boczkowski, & K.A. Foot (Eds.), *Media Technologies: Essays on Communication, Materiality, and Society*, 221-239. MIT Press.
   DOI: 10.7551/mitpress/9780262525374.003.0011
   - **The foundational text.** Proposes "broken world thinking" — taking "erosion, breakdown, and decay, rather than novelty, growth, and progress" as starting points. Argues repair is central to technological and human experience, not a failure state to be designed away.

2. **Jackson, S.J. & Kang, L. (2014). Breakdown, Obsolescence and Reuse: HCI and the Art of Repair.** *Proceedings of CHI '14*.
   DOI: 10.1145/2556288.2557332
   - Empirical study of repair practices. Shows how breakdown and repair reveal the "propensities" of objects and open design possibilities that creation-focused thinking misses.

3. **Houston, L., Jackson, S.J., Rosner, D., Ahmed, S.I., Young, M. & Kang, L. (2016). Values in Repair.** *Proceedings of CHI '16*, 1403-1414.
   DOI: 10.1145/2858036.2858470
   - Repair as value-laden practice: what gets repaired, by whom, for whom reveals power structures. Connects repair studies to values-sensitive design.

4. **Rosner, D. & Ames, M. (2014). Designing for Repair? Infrastructures and Materialities of Breakdown.** *Proceedings of CSCW '14*, 319-331.
   DOI: 10.1145/2531602.2531692
   - Ethnographic study of repair practices. Connects repair to infrastructure studies and material engagement.

## Strand 2: Material Unmaking (Biodegradation, Decay, Deformation)

5. **Song, K.W. & Paulos, E. (2021). Unmaking: Enabling and Celebrating the Creative Material of Failure, Destruction, Decay, and Deformation.** *Proceedings of CHI '21*.
   DOI: 10.1145/3411764.3445529
   - Introduced unmaking as material design practice. Provides a vocabulary of unmaking operations (crack, split, dissolve, bulge, lean, sag) within 3D modeling tools. Draws on Auto-Destructive Art (Gustav Metzger). Demonstrates multi-material 3D printing process for designed decay.

6. **Song, K.W., Bell, F., Deshpande, H., Mandel, I., Wun, T., Alistar, M., Buechley, L., Ju, W., Kim, J., Paulos, E., Sabie, S. & Wakkary, R. (2024). Sustainable Unmaking: Designing for Biodegradation, Decay, and Disassembly.** *CHI EA '24*, 489:1-7.
   DOI: 10.1145/3613905.3636300
   - Workshop bringing together sustainability and unmaking. Argues unmaking is essential for sustainable HCI — designing for end-of-life, not just beginning-of-use.

7. **Song, K.W., Sabie, S., Jackson, S.J., Lindström, K., Paulos, E., Ståhl, Å. & Wakkary, R. (2025). Unmaking and HCI: Techniques, Technologies, Materials, and Philosophies beyond Making.** *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 31(6).
   DOI: 10.1145/3689047
   - **The comprehensive synthesis.** Maps unmaking across HCI: material techniques (decay, deformation), technologies (biodegradable electronics), materials (living organisms, compostables), and philosophies (broken world thinking, feminist technoscience, posthumanism). Establishes unmaking as a coherent research program.

## Strand 3: Un/Making as Political Practice

8. **Lindström, K. & Ståhl, Å. (2020). Un/Making in the Aftermath of Design.** *Proceedings of PDC '20 — Participation(s) Otherwise*, Vol. 1.
   DOI: 10.1145/3385010.3385021
   - Introduced un/making as political design practice at PDC (Participatory Design Conference). The double movement of making and unmaking — questioning what design has made and continues to make. Connected to feminist technoscience and decolonial critique.

9. **Lindström, K. & Ståhl, Å. (2023). Unmaking the Plastic Era.** STS research.
   - Extended un/making to environmental politics — how to unmake our dependence on plastic through design practice.

10. **Lindström, K. & Ståhl, Å. (2014). Patchworking Publics-in-the-Making.** Collaborative PhD thesis, Malmö University.
    - The foundational collaborative thesis behind the Un/Making Studio. Combines participatory design with feminist technoscience.

11. **Ståhl, Å. & Lindström, K. — Un/Making Studio.** Linnaeus University.
    URL: https://www.unmakingstudio.se/
    - Design research studio that "explores alternatives to progressivist imaginaries within design." Works with maintenance, repair, and composting as design methods. Part of the funded project "Un/Making Matters."

12. **Design+Posthumanism Network (2026). Un/Making What?** Presentation by Ståhl & Lindström.
    URL: https://designandposthumanism.org/2026/01/12/un-making-what/
    - Recent articulation: un/making as a way to rethink design beyond production and progress. Explicitly connected to posthuman design network.

## Strand 4: Unmaking + AI

13. **[Authors]. (2025). Unmaking-with AI: Tactics for Decentering through Design.** *ACM Transactions on CHI*.
    DOI: 10.1145/3685275
    - Extends unmaking to AI contexts. Aligns unmaking with decentering — using Lindström & Ståhl's "double movement" to analyze how AI systems can be unmade/decentered. Connects unmaking to more-than-human design.

## Precursors and Context

14. **Blevis, E. (2007). Sustainable Interaction Design: Invention & Disposal, Renewal & Reuse.** *Proceedings of CHI '07*, 503-512.
    DOI: 10.1145/1240624.1240705
    - **The precursor.** First systematic treatment of disposal and end-of-life in interaction design. Proposed a rubric for understanding material effects in terms of use, reuse, and disposal. Foundational for sustainable HCI. Unmaking builds directly on Blevis's insight that design must attend to what happens *after* use.

15. **Metzger, G. (1960s). Auto-Destructive Art.** Manifestos and artworks.
    - Song & Paulos (2021) cite Auto-Destructive Art (Gustav Metzger, 1960s) as artistic precedent — art designed to destroy itself. Connects unmaking to the artist-led research tradition.

16. **Wakkary, R. (2021). Things We Could Design: For More Than Human-Centered Worlds.** MIT Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-262-04468-4
    - Wakkary's more-than-human design theory provides philosophical context for unmaking: if things have their own agencies, then their decay, breakdown, and transformation are not failures but expressions of thing-being.

17. **Fry, T. (2009). Design Futuring: Sustainability, Ethics and New Practice.** Berg Publishers.
    DOI: 10.5040/9781474293488
    - "Defuturing" — how design destroys futures — provides the sustainability framework that unmaking responds to. If design futuring asks "how do we sustain futures?", unmaking asks "how do we undo what is already destroying them?"

## Workshops and Community Formation

18. **Sabie, S., Song, K.W., Parikh, T., Jackson, S.J., Paulos, E., Lindström, K., Ståhl, Å., Sabie, D., Andersen, K. & Wakkary, R. (2022). Unmaking@CHI: Concretizing the Material and Epistemological Practices of Unmaking in HCI.** *CHI EA '22*.
    DOI: 10.1145/3491101.3503721
    - First CHI workshop on unmaking. Brought together the material (Song, Paulos, Bell), philosophical (Jackson, Wakkary), and political (Lindström, Ståhl) strands. Asked: "Design is conventionally about making new things. What about the converse?"

19. **Un/Making Matters** — funded research project, Linnaeus University.
    URL: https://lnu.se/en/research/research-projects/project-unmaking-matters-maintenance-repair-and-composting/
    - Multi-year research project exploring maintenance, repair, and composting as design research methods. Run by Lindström & Ståhl.

## Connections to Other Lineages

| Connected Lineage | Nature of Connection |
|---|---|
| **Constructionism / RtD** | Unmaking inverts the epistemological claim: if making = knowing, then unmaking = a different kind of knowing. Challenges the field's creation bias. |
| **More-than-Human Design** | Things decay, break, transform — unmaking attends to these more-than-human processes rather than treating them as failure. Wakkary bridges both. |
| **Material Speculation** | Wakkary's "research products" deployed over time will eventually decay — unmaking extends material speculation into the afterlife of artifacts. |
| **Slow Technology** | Hallnäs & Redström's slow technology invites reflection; unmaking extends this temporal orientation to include entropy and decline. |
| **Sustainable Interaction Design** | Blevis (2007) is the direct precursor. Unmaking operationalizes Blevis's call to attend to disposal and end-of-life. |
| **Design Futuring** | Fry's "defuturing" = design destroying futures. Unmaking is the practice of undoing defuturing — designing for disassembly, composting, renewal. |
| **Participatory Design** | Lindström & Ståhl's un/making emerges from PD (PDC '20). Participatory unmaking = collectively deciding what to unmake. |
| **Decolonial Design** | Unmaking progressivist temporalities connects to decolonial critique of linear progress narratives in design. |
| **Artist-led Research** | Auto-Destructive Art (Metzger) is an artistic precursor. Benford's "aesthetic failure" shares the embrace of breakdown. |
| **Prototyping as Research** | Song & Paulos extend prototyping tools (3D modeling) with unmaking operations. Prototyping → making; provotyping → provoking; unmaking → un-prototyping. |
| **Infrastructural Speculation** | Jackson's broken world thinking + Wong's infrastructural speculation both attend to infrastructure — one through repair, the other through speculation. |

## Implications for the Genealogy

**Position:** Unmaking is a **counter-movement** — it doesn't branch from a single parent lineage but emerges as a critique of the entire making-oriented edifice of design research. It draws on:
- Repair studies (Jackson 2014) — from infrastructure/STS studies
- Sustainable HCI (Blevis 2007) — from environmental concern
- Feminist technoscience (Lindström & Ståhl) — from PD + feminist theory
- Material experimentation (Song & Paulos 2021) — from maker culture + art

**Temporal emergence:** Late 2010s–2020s. Jackson's "Rethinking Repair" (2014) is the philosophical foundation; Song & Paulos (2021) is the material turn; Lindström & Ståhl (2020) is the political turn; Song et al. (2025) TOCHI is the synthesis.

**Key insight:** Unmaking reveals a fundamental tension in design research's epistemology. If the field's knowledge-production is grounded in making (constructionism → RtD), then what kind of knowledge does *un*making produce? This is not merely a sustainability concern but an epistemological challenge that forces the field to reckon with what lies beyond its creation-oriented self-understanding.
