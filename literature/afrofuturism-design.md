## Foundational Works

1. **Dery, M. (1994). Black to the Future: Interviews with Samuel R. Delany, Greg Tate, and Tricia Rose.** In M. Dery (Ed.), *Flame Wars: The Discourse of Cyberculture*, Duke University Press, 179–222. (Originally in *South Atlantic Quarterly*, 92(4), 1993, 735–778.)
   DOI: 10.1215/00382876-92-4-735
   — The originating text. Coined "Afrofuturism" and posed the foundational question: "Can a community whose past has been deliberately rubbed out… imagine possible futures?"

2. **Eshun, K. (2003). Further Considerations of Afrofuturism.** *CR: The New Centennial Review*, 3(2), 287–302.
   DOI: 10.1353/ncr.2003.0021
   — Influential theorization; positioned Afrofuturism as counter-memory and counter-future against chronopolitical erasure. Power operates through the "management of the future."

3. **Womack, Y. L. (2013). Afrofuturism: The World of Black Sci-Fi and Fantasy Culture.** Lawrence Hill Books.
   ISBN: 978-1-61374-796-4
   — Accessible overview defining Afrofuturism as "an intersection of imagination, technology, the future and liberation."

4. **Nelson, A. (2002). Introduction: Future Texts.** *Social Text*, 20(2), 1–15.
   DOI: 10.1215/01642472-20-2_71-1
   — Introduced the *Social Text* special issue on Afrofuturism; positioned it within critical race studies and technology.

5. **Gilroy, P. (1993). The Black Atlantic: Modernity and Double-Consciousness.** Harvard University Press.
   ISBN: 978-0-674-07606-5
   — The Black Atlantic as theoretical context for Afrofuturism; modernity experienced differently from the African diaspora. Not Afrofuturist per se, but foundational for the movement.

## Afrofuturism in Design & HCI

6. **Bray, K. E., Harrington, C., Parker, A. G., Diakhate, N., & Roberts, J. (2022). Radical Futures: Supporting Community-Led Design Engagements through an Afrofuturist Speculative Design Toolkit.** *Proceedings of CHI '22*, Article 523.
   DOI: 10.1145/3491102.3501945
   — Key CHI paper. Developed "Building Utopia" Afrofuturist toolkit for participatory speculative design with Black communities.

7. **Ogbonnaya-Ogburu, I. F., Smith, A. D., To, A., & Toyama, K. (2020). Critical Race Theory for HCI.** *Proceedings of CHI '20*, Article 497.
   DOI: 10.1145/3313831.3376392
   — Applied critical race theory to HCI; Afrofuturism as one framework for racially aware design research.

8. **Benjamin, R. (2019). Race After Technology: Abolitionist Tools for the New Jim Code.** Polity Press.
   ISBN: 978-1-5095-2643-7
   — Technology as not neutral; racialized design requires Afrofuturist and abolitionist counter-design. "The New Jim Code" as concept.

9. **Costanza-Chock, S. (2020). Design Justice: Community-Led Practices to Build the Worlds We Need.** MIT Press.
   DOI: 10.7551/mitpress/12255.001.0001
   — Design justice framework centers marginalized communities; Afrofuturism as design justice in practice. Intersects with participatory and speculative design.

## Decolonial Speculative Practice

10. **Prado de O. Martins, L., & Oliveira, P. (2014). Questioning the "Critical" in Speculative & Critical Design.** Medium / A Parede.
    URL: https://medium.com/a-parede/questioning-the-critical-in-speculative-critical-design-5a355cac2ca4
    — Critiqued SCD's Eurocentrism and privilege; non-Western speculative traditions (including Afrofuturism) as response to this gap. Foundational decolonial design text.

11. **Oliveira, P., & Prado de O. Martins, L. (2015). Futuristic Gizmos, Conservative Ideals: On Anachronistic Design.** *Modes of Criticism*, 1.
    URL: https://modesofcriticism.org/futuristic-gizmos-conservative-ideals/
    — Extended the critique: speculative design reproduces colonial power structures unless deliberately decolonized.

12. **Schultz, T., Abdulla, D., Ansari, A., Canlı, E., Keshavarz, M., Kiem, M., Prado de O. Martins, L., & Vieira de Oliveira, P. J. S. (2018). What Is at Stake with Decolonizing Design? A Roundtable.** *Design and Culture*, 10(1), 81–101.
    DOI: 10.1080/17547075.2018.1434368
    — Foundational decolonizing design roundtable; Afrofuturism as one of several decolonial design frameworks. Established the Decolonising Design collective.

13. **Escobar, A. (2018). Designs for the Pluriverse: Radical Interdependence, Autonomy, and the Making of Worlds.** Duke University Press.
    DOI: 10.1215/9780822371816
    — "Autonomous design" and the pluriverse; design for multiple worldings including Afrodiasporic futures. Foundational decolonial design theory.

14. **Abdulla, D. (2018). Design Otherwise: Towards a Locally-Grounded Practice of Design.** PhD thesis, Goldsmiths, University of London.
    URL: https://research.gold.ac.uk/id/eprint/23130/
    — Explored design from non-Western perspectives; design "otherwise" as alternative to universalizing Western design paradigms.

15. **Tunstall, E. (Dori) (2023). Decolonizing Design: A Cultural Justice Guidebook.** MIT Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-262-04769-1
    — Practical guidebook for decolonizing design institutions and practice; case studies from 15+ years transforming design education.

16. **Ansari, A. et al. (2016). The Decolonising Design Manifesto.** Decolonising Design.
    URL: https://www.decolonisingdesign.com/
    — Manifesto establishing the Decolonising Design platform; called for design practice that refuses colonial logics.

17. **Ansari, A. et al. (2023). Pluriversal Futures for Design Education.** *She Ji: The Journal of Design, Economics, and Innovation*, 9(2), 179–196.
    DOI: 10.1016/j.sheji.2023.08.002
    — Recommendations for decolonizing design curricula from the Future of Design Education working group; pluriversal alternatives to Eurocentric canon.

    ISBN: 978-1-4985-1003-0
    — "Afrofuturism 2.0" extending beyond literary/film origins into design, technology, and activism.

19. **Barber, T. E. (2017). Cyborg Grammar? Reading Wangechi Mutu's Non-je ne regrette rien through Afrofuturism.** *NKA: Journal of Contemporary African Art*, 40, 84–93.
    DOI: 10.1215/10757163-3885858
    — Visual Afrofuturism in contemporary African art; relevant to design's visual speculation practices.

20. **Dillon, G. R. (Ed.) (2012). Walking the Clouds: An Anthology of Indigenous Science Fiction.** University of Arizona Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-8165-2882-2
    — Indigenous Futurisms anthology; parallel movement to Afrofuturism applying Indigenous perspectives to speculative futures.

## Equity, Positionality & Design

21. **Noel, L.-A. (2020). The Designer's Critical Alphabet.** Self-published toolkit.
    URL: https://www.designercriticalabet.com/
    — Card deck introducing critical theory concepts (including positionality, decolonization, intersectionality) to design practice and education.

22. **Noel, L.-A. (2023). Redesigning the Field.** Keynote, P&D Conference 2022. Published in conference proceedings.
    URL: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/374524359_Redesigning_the_field
    — Emphasized how designers must examine their own positionality to address systemic inequities in the field.

## Recent Developments (2020–2026)

23. **Harrington, C., Klassen, S., & Rankin, Y. A. (2022). "All That You Touch, You Change": Expanding the Canon of Speculative Design Towards Black Futuring.** *Proceedings of DIS '22*.
    DOI: 10.1145/3532106.3533523
    — Extended Afrofuturist speculative design beyond toolkits into "Black futuring" as sustained practice.

24. **To, A., Sweeney, W., Hammer, J., & Kaufman, G. (2020). "They Just Don't Get It": Towards Social Technologies for Coping with Interpersonal Racism.** *Proceedings of CSCW '20*.
    DOI: 10.1145/3392836
    — Technology design informed by Black experiences; speculative design for racial justice in social technologies.

25. **Appadurai, A. (2013). The Future as Cultural Fact: Essays on the Global Condition.** Verso Books.
    ISBN: 978-1-84467-985-5
    — "The capacity to aspire" is unequally distributed; futures-thinking as a cultural resource that marginalized communities have less access to.

26. **Benjamin, R. (Ed.) (2019). Captivating Technology: Race, Carceral Technoscience, and Liberatory Imagination in Everyday Life.** Duke University Press.
    DOI: 10.1215/9781478004493
    — Edited volume on how technology reproduces carceral logics; liberatory imagination as counter-design practice. Complements "Race After Technology" with broader scholarly contributions.

27. **Benjamin, R. (2024). Imagination: A Manifesto.** W. W. Norton.
    ISBN: 978-0-393-88134-5
    — Extended the abolitionist design critique into imagination as political practice; argued that imagination is unevenly distributed and must be deliberately cultivated.

28. **Harrington, C. N., Erete, S., & Piper, A. M. (2019). Dequeuing Health: Community-Based Participatory Design of a Health Information Technology Intervention.** *Proceedings of CHI '19*, Article 393.
    DOI: 10.1145/3290605.3300623
    — Community-based participatory design with older Black adults; demonstrated how equity-centered methods produce different design outcomes than standard HCD.

29. **Ansari, A. (2020). Plural Bodies, Pluriversal Humans: Questioning the Ontology of 'Body' in Design.** *Somatechnics*, 10(3), 286–305.
    DOI: 10.3366/soma.2020.0324
    — Challenged universalist assumptions about the body in design; argued for pluriversal understanding of embodiment drawing on South Asian and decolonial philosophy.

30. **Mazé, R. (2019). Politics of Designing Visions of the Future.** *Journal of Futures Studies*, 23(3), 23–38.
    DOI: 10.6531/JFS.201903_23(3).0003
    — Analyzed the politics of who gets to design futures; connected futures studies to design justice concerns. Whose futures are designed, and who designs them?

31. **Noel, L.-A., & Paiva, M. (2021). Learning to Recognize Exclusion.** *Journal of Usability Studies*, 16(2), 63–72.
    URL: https://uxpajournal.org/learning-recognize-exclusion/
    — Positionality and exclusion in design education; practical framework for recognizing how design excludes. Connected to the Designer's Critical Alphabet toolkit.

32. **Prado de O. Martins, L. (2014). Privilege and Oppression: Towards a Feminist Speculative Design.** *Proceedings of DRS '14*.
    URL: https://dl.designresearchsociety.org/drs-conference-papers/drs2014/researchpapers/72/
    — Feminist critique of speculative design's complicity with privilege; called for speculative practice grounded in lived experiences of oppression.

33. **Irani, L., Vertesi, J., Dourish, P., Philip, K., & Grinter, R. E. (2010). Postcolonial Computing: A Lens on Design and Development.** *Proceedings of CHI '10*, 1311–1320.
    DOI: 10.1145/1753326.1753522
    — Postcolonial theory applied to HCI; challenged design's implicit Western universalism. Foundational for the decolonial design movement.

34. **Bardzell, S. (2010). Feminist HCI: Taking Stock and Outlining an Agenda for Design.** *Proceedings of CHI '10*, 1301–1310.
    DOI: 10.1145/1753326.1753521
    — Feminist framework for HCI and design; pluralism, participation, ecology, embodiment, and self-disclosure as feminist design qualities. Foundational for equity-oriented design research.

## Connections to Other Lineages

- **Speculative Design** (parent): Afrofuturism is a culturally-specific speculative practice that challenges SCD's Western defaults (Prado & Oliveira 2014)
- **Design Fiction / World Building**: Afrofuturist world building creates culturally grounded fictional futures (Bray et al. 2022)
- **Participatory Design**: Afrofuturist PD centers Black communities as designers of their own futures (Harrington et al. 2022)
- **Design Justice**: Shared commitment to centering marginalized perspectives (Costanza-Chock 2020)
- **Foresight**: Afrofuturism challenges whose futures foresight anticipates (Appadurai 2013)
- **Indigenous Futurisms**: Parallel decolonial speculative movement from Indigenous perspectives (Dillon 2012)
- **More-than-Human Design**: Both challenge the assumed universality of the human subject in design

## Summary

Afrofuturism entered design research through two converging streams. The first is the cultural-theoretical lineage from Dery's (1994) coinage through Eshun's (2003) theorization of counter-futures and Womack's (2013) popular synthesis. The second is the decolonial design critique beginning with Prado & Oliveira's (2014) "Questioning the Critical," which exposed speculative and critical design's Eurocentrism, and crystallizing in the Schultz et al. (2018) roundtable and the Decolonising Design manifesto (Ansari et al. 2016).

The operationalization in HCI came through Bray et al.'s (2022) Afrofuturist speculative design toolkit at CHI, Harrington et al.'s (2022) "Black futuring" at DIS, and Ogbonnaya-Ogburu et al.'s (2020) critical race theory for HCI. These are supported by broader frameworks: Escobar's (2018) pluriversal design, Costanza-Chock's (2020) design justice, Benjamin's (2019) abolitionist technology critique, and Tunstall's (2023) practical decolonization guidebook.

Afrofuturism demonstrates that speculation is culturally situated — that inclusive futures require diverse speculative traditions beyond Western critical design. It connects to Indigenous Futurisms (Dillon 2012), pluriversal design education (Ansari et al. 2023), and the growing recognition in HCI that whose futures are imagined is itself a political act (Eshun 2003; Mazé 2019). The lineage challenges design research to ask not just "what future?" but "whose future?" and "who gets to imagine it?"
