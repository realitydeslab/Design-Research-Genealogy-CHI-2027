## Foundational Works

1. **Sterling, B. (2005). Shaping Things.** MIT Press.
   ISBN: 978-0-262-69326-7
   — Coined "design fiction"; argued design and science fiction converge in telling worlds rather than stories.

2. **Bleecker, J. (2009). Design Fiction: A Short Essay on Design, Science, Fact and Fiction.** Near Future Laboratory.
   URL: https://nearfuturelaboratory.com/essays/
   — Established design fiction as practice; combined Sterling's concept with Kirby's diegetic prototypes into a coherent methodology.

3. **Kirby, D. A. (2010). The Future is Now: Diegetic Prototypes and the Role of Popular Films in Generating Real-World Technological Development.** *Social Studies of Science*, 40(1), 41–70.
   DOI: 10.1177/0306312709338325
   — Coined "diegetic prototypes": technologies that exist only within a fictional world but demonstrate need, viability, and benevolence to audiences.

4. **Wolf, M. J. P. (2012). Building Imaginary Worlds: The Theory and History of Subcreation.** Routledge.
   DOI: 10.4324/9780203096949
   — Comprehensive theory of world building across media; "subcreation" (from Tolkien) as the creation of internally-consistent secondary worlds.

5. **Tolkien, J. R. R. (1947). On Fairy-Stories.** In *Essays Presented to Charles Williams*, Oxford University Press, 38–89.
   URL: https://coolcalvary.files.wordpress.com/2018/10/on-fairy-stories1.pdf
   — Originating theory of "subcreation" — creating secondary worlds with internal consistency. Foundational reference in design fiction world-building literature.

6. **Coulton, P., Lindley, J. G., Sturdee, M., & Stead, M. (2017). Design Fiction as World Building.** *Proceedings of Research through Design Conference 2017*, Edinburgh.
   DOI: 10.6084/m9.figshare.4746964
   — Key paper arguing Design Fiction is fundamentally a "world building" activity, not a storytelling one. Multiple artifacts describe "entry points" into a fictional world at different scales.

7. **Dunne, A., & Raby, F. (2013). Speculative Everything: Design, Fiction, and Social Dreaming.** MIT Press.
   ISBN: 978-0-262-01984-1
   — Speculative design as world-making; proposed futures exist as imagined worlds populated by designed artifacts.

## Extensions & Applications

8. **Coulton, P., Lindley, J. G., & Akmal, H. A. (2016). Design Fiction: Does the Search for Plausibility Lead to Deception?** *Proceedings of DRS '16*.
   DOI: 10.21606/drs.2016.148
   — Examined plausibility in design fiction; argued worlds must be internally consistent to be effective.

9. **Lindley, J., & Coulton, P. (2015). Game of Drones.** *Proceedings of CHI '15 Extended Abstracts*, 613–618.
   DOI: 10.1145/2702613.2725444
   — Design fiction using world-building techniques to explore drone futures; demonstrated multi-artifact world construction.

10. **Lindley, J., Coulton, P., & Sturdee, M. (2017). Implications for Adoption.** *Proceedings of CHI '17*, 265–277.
    DOI: 10.1145/3025453.3025742
    — Satirical design fiction demonstrating world building: a fictional world where design fiction is mainstream. Meta-commentary on the field.

11. **Lindley, J., & Coulton, P. (2016). Pushing the Limits of Design Fiction: The Case For Fictional Research Papers.** *Proceedings of CHI '16*, 4032–4043.
    DOI: 10.1145/2858036.2858446
    — Extended design fiction world building into academic paper format; fictional research papers as entry points to fictional worlds.

12. **Blythe, M. (2014). Research Through Design Fiction: Narrative in Real and Imaginary Abstracts.** *Proceedings of CHI '14*, 703–712.
    DOI: 10.1145/2556288.2557098
    — Introduced "imaginary abstracts" as design fiction method; pastiching academic format to build fictional research worlds.

13. **Blythe, M. (2017). Research Fiction: Storytelling, Plot and Design.** *Proceedings of CHI '17*, 5400–5411.
    DOI: 10.1145/3025453.3026023
    — Extended fictional research papers into "research fiction"; narrative world building within HCI research practice.

14. **Tanenbaum, J., Tanenbaum, K., & Wakkary, R. (2012). Steampunk as Design Fiction.** *Proceedings of CHI '12*, 1583–1592.
    DOI: 10.1145/2207676.2208279
    — Analyzed Steampunk as design fiction practice; DIY appropriation creates alternative historical worlds.

15. **Sturdee, M., Coulton, P., Lindley, J. G., Stead, M., Ali, H., & Hudson-Smith, A. (2016). Design Fiction: How to Build a Voight-Kampff Machine.** *Proceedings of CHI '16 Extended Abstracts*, 375–386.
    DOI: 10.1145/2851581.2892574
    — Workshop exploring design fiction as world building through visual methods; sketching and comics as entry points.

16. **Sturdee, M., Coulton, P., & Alexander, J. M. (2017). Using Design Fiction to Inform Shape-Changing Interface Design and Use.** *The Design Journal*, 20(Suppl. 1), S4546–S4558.
    DOI: 10.1080/14606925.2017.1352951
    — Applied design fiction world building to shape-changing interfaces; fictional worlds as contexts for testing emerging technologies.

17. **Candy, S., & Dunagan, J. (2017). Designing an Experiential Scenario: The People Who Vanished.** *Futures*, 86, 136–153.
    DOI: 10.1016/j.futures.2016.05.006
    — Detailed case study of experiential futures world building; creating immersive scenarios participants can inhabit.

18. **Elsden, C., Chatting, D., Durrant, A. C., Garbett, A., Nissen, B., Vines, J., & Kirk, D. S. (2017). On Speculative Enactments.** *Proceedings of CHI '17*, 5386–5399.
    DOI: 10.1145/3025453.3025503
    — Speculative enactments create temporary worlds that participants inhabit and respond to; participatory world building.

19. **Auger, J. (2013). Speculative Design: Crafting the Speculation.** *Digital Creativity*, 24(1), 11–35.
    DOI: 10.1080/14626268.2013.767276
    — Analyzed how speculative designs construct plausible worlds; craft of world building as design method.

## Recent Developments (2020–2026)

20. **Bleecker, J., Foster, N., Girardin, F., & Nova, N. (2022). The Manual of Design Fiction.** Near Future Laboratory.
    ISBN: 978-0-9905633-1-0
    — Comprehensive practitioner guide to design fiction as world building; creating artifacts from possible near futures.

21. **Coulton, P., & Lindley, J. G. (2019). More-Than Human Centred Design: Considering Other Things.** *The Design Journal*, 22(4), 463–481.
    DOI: 10.1080/14606925.2019.1614320
    — Extended world building beyond human-centred focus; fictional worlds populated by non-human actors and IoT assemblages.

22. **Coulton, P., Lindley, J., & Cooper, R. (2018). The Little Book of Design Fiction for the Internet of Things.** Lancaster University.
    URL: https://www.petrashub.org/the-little-book-of-design-fiction-for-the-internet-of-things/
    — Applied design fiction world building to IoT: creating fictional worlds populated by connected devices.

23. **Stead, M. (2017). Designing Game Worlds and Game Writing for Internet of Things Products and Services.** *Proceedings of Research through Design Conference 2017*.
    URL: https://figshare.com/articles/conference_contribution/Designing_Game_Worlds/4747036
    — Connected game world design to IoT product/service design; games as world-building template.

## Critical Perspectives

24. **Gonzatto, R. F., van Amstel, F. M. C., Merkle, L. E., & Hartmann, T. (2013). The Ideology of the Future in Design Fictions.** *Digital Creativity*, 24(1), 36–45.
    DOI: 10.1080/14626268.2013.772524
    — Critiqued ideological assumptions embedded in fictional worlds; worlds are not neutral but ideologically loaded.

25. **Wong, R. Y., & Khovanskaya, V. (2018). Speculative Design in HCI: From Corporate Imaginations to Critical Orientations.** In *New Directions in Third Wave HCI*, Springer, 175–202.
    DOI: 10.1007/978-3-319-73356-2_11
    — Mapped whose worlds get built: corporate vs. critical orientations in speculative world building.

26. **Tonkinwise, C. (2015). Design Fictions About Critical Design.** *Modes of Criticism*, 1.
    URL: https://modesofcriticism.org/design-fictions-about-critical-design/
    — Critiqued speculative/critical design's class and ethnic assumptions in world building; called for more accountable fictional worlds.

## World Building Theory from Adjacent Fields

27. **Jenkins, H. (2006). Convergence Culture: Where Old and New Media Collide.** NYU Press.
    ISBN: 978-0-8147-4295-2
    — Transmedia storytelling: building worlds across multiple media platforms. Directly relevant to Design Fiction's multi-artifact world building.

28. **Ryan, M.-L. (2014). Story/Worlds/Media: Tuning the Instruments of a Media-Conscious Narratology.** In M.-L. Ryan & J.-N. Thon (Eds.), *Storyworlds across Media*, University of Nebraska Press, 25–49.
    DOI: 10.2307/j.ctt1d9nkdg.6
    — Narratological theory of story worlds; relevant to understanding how fictional worlds function as design research contexts.

## Connections to Other Lineages

- **Design Fiction** (parent): World Building is the core activity of Design Fiction (Coulton & Lindley 2017)
- **Speculative Design**: Fictional worlds as the context for speculative proposals (Dunne & Raby 2013)
- **Foresight & Futures**: Scenario planning creates worlds (albeit less materially grounded); experiential futures (Candy) bridges both
- **Experiential Futures**: Immersive worlds that participants inhabit (Candy & Dunagan 2017; Elsden et al. 2017)
- **Anticipatory Ethnography**: Fictional worlds become sites for ethnographic analysis
- **Afrofuturism / Decolonial Design**: Challenges whose worlds get built (Wong & Khovanskaya 2018; Tonkinwise 2015)
- **More-than-Human Design**: World building expands to include non-human actors (Coulton & Lindley 2019)

## Summary

World Building in design research was articulated by Coulton, Lindley et al. (2017) as the fundamental activity underlying Design Fiction. Rather than storytelling, Design Fiction creates internally-consistent fictional worlds populated by multiple artifacts at different scales — what Tolkien called "subcreation" and Wolf (2012) theorized as imaginary world construction. The approach draws on Kirby's (2010) concept of "diegetic prototypes," Bleecker's (2009) founding essay connecting SF and design, and Sterling's (2005) original coinage.

The lineage extends from media theory (Jenkins's transmedia storytelling, Ryan's storyworlds) through experiential futures (Candy's immersive scenarios) to applied domains like IoT (Coulton et al. 2018) and shape-changing interfaces (Sturdee et al. 2017). Blythe (2014, 2017) and Lindley & Coulton (2016) pushed world building into academic format itself through fictional research papers and imaginary abstracts.

Critical perspectives from Gonzatto et al. (2013), Wong & Khovanskaya (2018), and Tonkinwise (2015) highlight that fictional worlds are never ideologically neutral — they embed assumptions about class, race, and power. This connects world building to decolonial critiques and questions of whose futures are imagined and materialized. The recent expansion to more-than-human design (Coulton & Lindley 2019) and the comprehensive Manual of Design Fiction (Bleecker et al. 2022) mark the maturation of world building as a recognized design research methodology.
