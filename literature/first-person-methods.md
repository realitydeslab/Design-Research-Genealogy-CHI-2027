# Literature Review: First-Person Methods in Design Research

## Overview

First-person methods — autoethnography, autobiographical design, and autoethnographic RtD — represent a growing methodological strand where the designer-researcher is simultaneously the subject. The researcher designs *for themselves*, lives with the design, and reports the experiential knowledge gained. This lineage connects soma design (Höök), ethnography (Suchman), and RtD (Frayling/Zimmerman) through the radical move of collapsing the designer–user distinction entirely. A CHI 2024 systematic literature review confirmed rapid growth in HCI autoethnographies, establishing this as a distinct methodological category.

**Three strands:**
1. **Autobiographical Design** (Neustaedter & Sengers 2012): designing and using technology for oneself, generating "detailed, nuanced, experiential understanding of a design space"
2. **Autoethnography in HCI** (O'Kane et al. 2014; Lucero 2018): adapting social science autoethnography for design — the researcher's lived experience as data
3. **Autoethnographic RtD** (Desjardins & Wakkary 2016): combining autoethnography *with* research through design — the making + living + reflecting cycle

## Autobiographical Design

1. **Neustaedter, C., & Sengers, P. (2012). Autobiographical Design in HCI Research: Designing and Learning Through Use-it-Yourself.** *Proceedings of DIS '12*, 514–523.
   DOI: 10.1145/2317956.2318034
   - Foundational paper. Defined autobiographical design: genuine use of a self-designed system over time. Not prototyping or Wizard-of-Oz — actual long-term use by the designer. Argued it provides "detailed, nuanced, and experiential understanding of a design space" unavailable through other methods. Established validity criteria: genuine need, real use, extended duration.

2. **Desjardins, A., & Ball, A. (2018). Revealing Tensions in Autobiographical Design in HCI.** *Proceedings of DIS '18*, 753–764.
   DOI: 10.1145/3196709.3196781
   - Critical examination through interviews with HCI researchers who have used autobiographical design. Identified tensions: intimacy vs. publishability, self vs. other, duration vs. productivity, personal vs. generalizable. Best practices for rigor and ethics.

3. **Yang, R., & Neustaedter, C. (2020). An Autobiographical Design Study of a Long Distance Relationship.** *Proceedings of DIS '20*, 127–140.
   DOI: 10.1145/3357236.3395467
   - Extended autobiographical design to relationship/intimacy contexts. Demonstrated the method's value for sensitive, hard-to-study phenomena.

## Autoethnography in HCI

4. **O'Kane, A. A., Rogers, Y., & Blandford, A. (2014). Gaining Empathy for Non-Routine Mobile Device Use Through Autoethnography.** *Proceedings of CHI '14*, 987–990.
   DOI: 10.1145/2556288.2557179
   - Early HCI autoethnography. Used self-study with mobile devices to gain empathy for non-routine use patterns. Demonstrated autoethnography's value for revealing taken-for-granted aspects of technology use.

5. **Lucero, A. (2018). Living Without a Mobile Phone: An Autoethnography.** *Proceedings of DIS '18*, 765–776.
   DOI: 10.1145/3196709.3196731
   - Autoethnographic study of going without a phone. Demonstrated how first-person deprivation reveals design assumptions embedded in everyday technology.

6. **Homewood, S., Karlsson, A., & Vallgårda, S. (2020). Removal as a Method: A Fourth Wave HCI Approach to Understanding the Experience of Self-Tracking.** *Proceedings of DIS '20*, 1779–1791.
   DOI: 10.1145/3357236.3395425
   - Proposed "removal" as a first-person research method: deliberately stop using technology to surface hidden dependencies and assumptions. Applied to menstrual tracking. Connected first-person methods to fourth-wave HCI.

7. **Rapp, A., & Tirabeni, L. (2020). Self-Tracking While Doing Sport: Comfort, Motivation, and the History of Self-Monitoring.** *Personal and Ubiquitous Computing*, 24, 173–191.
   DOI: 10.1007/s00779-019-01308-y
   - Autoethnographic study of self-tracking during sports. Extended first-person methods into quantified self territory.

## Autoethnographic Research through Design

8. **Desjardins, A., & Wakkary, R. (2016). Living In A Prototype: A Reconfigured Space.** *Proceedings of CHI '16*, 5274–5285.
   DOI: 10.1145/2858036.2858261
   - Combined autoethnography with RtD: designed IoT prototypes, deployed them in own home, lived with them for months, reflected on the experience. Established autoethnographic RtD as a distinct method combining making, living, and reflecting.

9. **Devendorf, L., Andersen, K., & Kelliher, A. (2020). Making Design Memoirs: Understanding and Honoring Difficult Experiences.** *Proceedings of CHI '20*, 1–12.
   DOI: 10.1145/3313831.3376345
   - Autobiographical textile/craft design as a way to process difficult life experiences. Extended first-person methods into making-as-meaning-making territory.

10. **Tsaknaki, V., Balaam, M., Ståhl, A., Valenti, P., Homewood, S., & Höök, K. (2019). Teaching Soma Design.** *Proceedings of DIS '19*, 1237–1249.
    DOI: 10.1145/3322276.3322327
    - First-person somatic exploration as design method. Designers attend to their own bodily sensations as design material. Bridges soma design and autoethnographic RtD.

11. **Tomico, O., Winthagen, V. O., & van Heist, M. M. G. (2012). Designing for, with, and through Yourself: Using First Person Research through Design in Investigating Social Wearables.** *Proceedings of Nordic Design Research Conference (Nordes)*, Article 54.
    - Early articulation of first-person RtD for wearables. The designer wears their own prototypes, generating embodied knowledge unavailable through user studies.

## Systematic Reviews and Methodological Reflection

12. **Lucero, A., Desjardins, A., Neustaedter, C., Höök, K., Hassenzahl, M., & Cecchinato, M. E. (2019). A Sample of One: First-Person Research Methods in HCI.** *DIS '19 Companion*, 385–388.
    DOI: 10.1145/3301019.3319996
    - Workshop paper defining the first-person methods research agenda. Identified autoethnography, autobiographical design, and autoethnographic RtD as the three main strands. Called for further methodological development.

13. **Epp, C., Klarkowski, M., & Wundersitz, L. (2024). Playing with Perspectives and Unveiling the Autoethnographic Kaleidoscope in HCI — A Literature Review of Autoethnographies.** *Proceedings of CHI '24*.
    DOI: 10.1145/3613904.3642355
    - Systematic literature review of HCI autoethnographies. Confirmed rapid growth. Categorized by topic, method, and contribution type. The most comprehensive survey to date.

14. **Sengers, P., Boehner, K., David, S., & Kaye, J. (2005). Reflective Design.** *Proceedings of the 4th Decennial Conference on Critical Computing*, 49–58.
    DOI: 10.1145/1094562.1094569
    - Precursor: reflective design as bringing critical reflection into technology design. First-person methods are one way to practice reflective design.

## Connections to Other Lineages

- **→ Soma Design (Höök):** First-person somatic exploration is inherently autoethnographic — the designer attends to their own body as design material. Tsaknaki et al. (2019) makes this explicit.
- **→ RtD (Zimmerman et al.):** Autoethnographic RtD (Desjardins & Wakkary 2016) is a specific mode of RtD where the designer is also the user/subject.
- **→ Ethnography (Suchman):** Autoethnography originates in anthropology/sociology (Ellis & Bochner, Adams et al.). HCI autoethnography adapts this for design contexts.
- **→ Slow Technology (Hallnäs & Redström):** Long-term first-person use (weeks to months) connects to slow technology's temporal orientation.
- **→ Unmaking (Song & Paulos):** Homewood's "removal as method" parallels unmaking — what do we learn by taking technology away?
- **→ Participatory Design:** First-person methods challenge PD's assumption that designers and users are different people. What happens when the designer *is* the user?
- **→ Reflective Design (Sengers et al.):** First-person methods operationalize reflective design's commitment to critical self-awareness.
- **→ Artist-Led Research (Benford):** Performance-led researchers often use their own experiences as data — autoethnography by another name.
