## Part I: Research Paradigms

Paradigms define the underlying philosophical stance on what design research *is*, what counts as *knowledge*, and how it is *legitimized*. These are not methodologies — they are the epistemological soil in which methodologies grow.

| Paradigm | Core Claim | Ontology | Epistemology | Axiology | Key Theorists | Design Manifestation |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Positivism / Post-positivism** | Knowledge is objective, measurable, generalizable | Single reality, mind-independent | Hypothesis-testing, empirical verification | Value-neutral | Comte, Popper, Campbell | Usability testing, controlled studies, HCI systems papers |
| **Interpretivism / Constructivism** | Meaning is socially constructed; researcher co-constructs understanding | Multiple realities, context-dependent | Thick description, lived experience, intersubjective | Value-laden; researcher reflexivity required | Berger & Luckmann, Geertz, Denzin | Ethnography in design, qualitative HCI, phenomenological design |
| **Pragmatism** | Knowledge is instrumental; validity = what works | Reality as experienced, action-oriented | Inquiry driven by consequences, reflection-in-action | Problem-solving; ethics of consequences | Dewey, Peirce, James, Schön | Research through Design, constructive design research |
| **Critical Theory** | Knowledge is always political; research should emancipate | Reality shaped by power structures | Ideology critique, reflexive inquiry, praxis | Explicitly value-laden; committed to justice | Frankfurt School, Habermas, Freire | Critical design, adversarial design, design justice, feminist HCI |
| **Practice Theory / Performativity** | Knowledge emerges from doing; practice is prior to theory | Reality as enacted through practice | Reflection-in-action, practice-as-research | Situated, embodied, tacit | Schön, Ryle, Bourdieu, Haseman | Practice-based research, performative research, RtD's showroom tradition |
| **Phenomenology** | Knowledge is grounded in first-person experience | Lived body as primary site of meaning | Bracketing, reduction, phenomenological description | Ethics of care, somatic attention | Husserl, Merleau-Ponty, Heidegger | Soma design, first-person methods, somaesthetic design |
| **New Materialism / Posthumanism** | Agency is distributed across human and nonhuman; design is always entangled | Flat ontology; more-than-human assemblages | Diffractive methodology; multispecies inquiry | Ethics of entanglement, care for nonhuman others | Haraway, Barad, Braidotti, Latour, Stengers | More-than-human design, thing ethnography, multispecies design |
| **Decolonial / Postcolonial Theory** | Western epistemologies are not universal; knowledge is geopolitically situated | Plural ontologies; epistemic plurality | Counter-knowledge, indigenous methodologies | Epistemic justice, self-determination | Mignolo, Quijano, Escobar, Wynter | Afrofuturism, decolonial design, design justice, pluriversal design |
| **Feminist Epistemology** | Knowledge is gendered; standpoint matters | Partial perspectives as situated knowledge | Standpoint epistemology, strong objectivity | Equity, care, intersectionality | Haraway, Harding, Collins, Bardzell | Feminist HCI, feminist design, participatory design (some strands) |
| **Futures Studies / Foresight Paradigm** | Alternative futures are knowable and constructible through structured imagination | Plural possible worlds | Scenario logic, causal layered analysis, ethnographic foresight | Anticipatory governance, preferred futures | Dator, Masini, Inayatullah, Slaughter | Design futures, speculative design, transition design |

## Part II: Methodologies

A *methodology* is a coherent research approach with an underlying epistemology, characteristic practices, and community of practice. Each methodology generates specific types of knowledge through specific methods.

### 2.1 Research through Design (RtD) Family

| Methodology | Core Definition | Epistemological Anchor | Founding Papers / Books | Key Community |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Research through Design (RtD)** | Design activity *is* the research; the artifact and its making generate knowledge | Pragmatism; constructionism | Frayling (1993); Zimmerman et al. (2007) CHI; Gaver & Bowers (2012) | CHI, DRS, Nordes |
| **Constructive Design Research** | RtD organized via lab/field/showroom taxonomy; constructing prototypes to advance design knowledge | Pragmatist; empirical | Koskinen et al. *Constructive Design Research* (2011) | Aalto, TU/e, Umeå |
| **Annotated Portfolio** | A collection of design examples with annotations that draw out generalizable insights | Abductive; comparative | Gaver & Bowers (2012) DIS; Löwgren (2013) Interactions | DIS, CHI |
| **Autoethnographic RtD** | Combine making artifacts AND deploying them in one's own life AND writing ethnographically about the experience | Phenomenological; first-person | Desjardins & Wakkary (2016); Tomico et al. (2012) | Umeå, TU/e |
| **Practice-Based Research (PaR)** | Artistic/creative practice as the vehicle of inquiry; knowledge *in* and *through* practice | Practice theory; performativity | Haseman (2006); Borgdorff (2012); Nelson (2013) | Fine art, performance, studio research |
| **Soma Design** | First-person somatic exploration as design method; designer's body as site of inquiry | Phenomenological; first-person | Höök (2018) *Designing with the Body*; Tsaknaki et al. (2019) | KTH Royal Institute, CHI |
| **Autobiographical Design** | Designer creates technology for genuine, long-term personal use as a form of inquiry | First-person; naturalistic | Neustaedter & Sengers (2012) CHI; Desjardins & Ball (2018) | CHI, CSCW |

### 2.2 Speculative & Critical Design Family

| Methodology | Core Definition | Epistemological Anchor | Founding Papers / Books | Key Community |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Speculative Design (SCD)** | Design as exploration of possible futures, not problem-solving; provocation over solution | Critical theory; futures paradigm | Dunne (1999) *Hertzian Tales*; Dunne & Raby (2013) *Speculative Everything* | RCA, Design Interactions |
| **Critical Design** | Design that questions societal norms, values, and assumptions through estrangement | Critical theory; ideology critique | Dunne & Raby (2001) *Design Noir*; Malpass (2017) *Critical Design in Context* | RCA, CHI, DRS |
| **Design Fiction** | Narrative-based speculation using fictional artifacts (diegetic prototypes) to imagine futures | Pragmatist-futures; narrative epistemology | Sterling (2005); Bleecker (2009); Lindley & Coulton (2015) CHI | SXSW, CHI, Nordes |
| **Adversarial Design** | Design that creates agonistic encounters, surfacing political conflict rather than resolving it | Agonistic democracy (Mouffe); critical theory | DiSalvo (2012) *Adversarial Design* (MIT Press) | CHI, DRS, design activism |
| **Critical Making** | Joining critical inquiry with hands-on making; making as a form of cultural critique | Pragmatist; critical theory | Ratto (2011) *The Information Society*; Hertz (2012) | Media arts, maker culture, CHI |
| **Material Speculation** | Functional, deployed prototypes that are simultaneously probes of critical questions | Critical-empirical hybrid | Wakkary et al. (2015) CHI; Wakkary (2021) *Things We Could Design* | Simon Fraser, CHI |
| **Infrastructural Speculation** | Speculative design methods applied to data governance, policy, and infrastructure | STS-inflected speculation | Wong (2018–); Wong & Mulligan (2019) FAccT | CSCW, FAccT, CHI |
| **Afrofuturism / Decolonial Speculative Design** | Speculative practice rooted in non-Western, marginalized futures; challenge SCD's Eurocentrism | Decolonial theory; Afrofuturism | Dery (1993); Eshun (2003); Harrington et al. (2022) CHI; Costanza-Chock (2020) | CSCW, CHI, design justice |
| **World-Building as Design Research** | Constructing internally-consistent fictional worlds as research contexts (not just artifacts) | Narrative epistemology; constructivist | Coulton & Lindley (2017); Tanenbaum et al. (2014) CHI | CHI, Nordes, game design |

### 2.3 Participatory Design (PD) Family

| Methodology | Core Definition | Epistemological Anchor | Founding Papers / Books | Key Community |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Participatory Design (PD)** | Workers/users as co-designers with equal authority; design for democracy | Critical theory; pragmatism | Nygaard (1975); Ehn (1988) *Work-Oriented Design*; Schuler & Namioka (1993) | PDC, CSCW, Scandinavian |
| **Co-Design** | Broad term for collaborative design across user/designer boundary (extends PD beyond workplace) | Constructivist; social | Sanders & Stappers (2008) *CoDesign*; Steen (2011) | Design Studies, CHI, PDC |
| **Agonistic PD** | PD embracing productive conflict and dissensus (vs. consensus) | Agonistic democracy (Mouffe) | Björgvinsson, Ehn & Hillgren (2012); DiSalvo (2012) | Malmö, PDC |
| **Infrastructuring** | PD as ongoing, relational sociotechnical infrastructure-building (not bounded projects) | STS; sociomateriality | Le Dantec & DiSalvo (2013) CHI; Bødker et al. (2017) PDC | CSCW, PDC |
| **Design Justice** | PD centered on those most marginalized; challenging structural inequity through design | Critical race theory; disability justice | Costanza-Chock (2020) *Design Justice* (MIT Press); Harrington et al. (2019) CHI | CSCW, CHI, activism |
| **Feminist Participatory Design** | PD foregrounding gender, power, and intersectionality | Feminist theory; care ethics | Rode (2011); Rode & Bhattacharya (2018); Bardzell (2010) CHI | CHI, CSCW, Feminist HCI |
| **Value-Sensitive Design (VSD)** | Systematically account for human values in the design of technology | Ethics; constructivism | Friedman, Kahn & Borning (2008); Friedman & Hendry (2019) *Value Sensitive Design* (MIT Press) | CHI, CSCW, iSchools |

### 2.4 More-than-Human Design Family

| Methodology | Core Definition | Epistemological Anchor | Founding Papers / Books | Key Community |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **More-than-Human Design (MtHD)** | Design that accounts for nonhuman agencies; things, animals, systems as co-designers | New materialism; posthumanism | Forlano (2017) *She Ji*; Giaccardi & Redström (2020) *Design Studies*; Wakkary (2021) | CHI, DRS, Nordes |
| **Posthuman HCI** | HCI centered on hybrid, cyborg, and posthuman subjectivities | Posthumanism; Haraway | Clarke & Dünser (2012); Frauenberger (2019) ACM TOCHI | CHI, NordiCHI |
| **Multispecies Design** | Design centering nonhuman animals, plants, and ecosystems as design stakeholders | Multispecies anthropology; new materialism | Westerlaken & Gualeni (2016); Benford et al. (2024) CHI; Clarke et al. (2023) | CHI, DRS |
| **Thing Ethnography** | Adopt the perspective of things/objects as ethnographic standpoint | Posthumanism; Latour; Harman | Giaccardi et al. (2016) CSCW; Martinussen (2015) | CSCW, CHI |
| **Ontological Design** | Designing changes what it means to be human; design as world-making | Heideggerian ontology | Willis (2006); Fry (2009) *Design Futuring* | DRS, design theory |
| **Unmaking / Un-design** | Design by deliberately removing, subtracting, or destroying designed objects | Post-growth; critical sustainability | Odom et al. (2018) DIS; Mages & Wakkary (2021) | CHI, DIS, sustainability |

### 2.5 Futures & Temporal Design Family

| Methodology | Core Definition | Epistemological Anchor | Founding Papers / Books | Key Community |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Design Futuring** | Design as anticipatory practice that prefigures alternative futures | Ontological design; critical theory | Fry (2009) *Design Futuring* (Berg); Fry (2020) *Defuturing* | DRS, design theory |
| **Experiential Futures** | Make futures tangible and inhabitable through designed experiences and scenarios | Foresight; enactivism; constructivism | Candy (2010) PhD; Candy & Dunagan (2017) *Futures* | IFTF, professional foresight, CHI |
| **Speculative Enactments** | Participants inhabit speculative scenarios, generating empirical data about imagined futures | Empirical-constructivist; ethnomethodology | Elsden et al. (2017) CHI; Ballard et al. (2019) | CHI, CSCW |
| **Transition Design** | Design for societal transitions toward more sustainable and equitable futures | Systems thinking; transition theory | Irwin (2015) *She Ji*; Irwin, Kossoff & Tonkinwise (2015) DRS | CMU DESIS, DRS |
| **Anticipatory Ethnography** | Ethnographic analysis of fictional artifacts and futures scenarios | Futures-oriented; ethnomethodological | Lindley, Sharma & Potts (2014) CHI; Coulton et al. (2017) | CHI |
| **Design for Slow Technology** | Design intentionally slow, calm, or reflective technologies | Post-efficiency; phenomenological | Hallnäs & Redström (2001) ACM TOCHI | Nordic HCI, tangible computing |

### 2.6 Ethnographic & Observational Family

| Methodology | Core Definition | Epistemological Anchor | Founding Papers / Books | Key Community |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Design Ethnography** | Ethnographic fieldwork conducted with explicit design intent | Interpretivism; anthropology | Blomberg et al. (1993); Suchman (1987) *Plans and Situated Actions* | CSCW, CHI, PARC |
| **Cultural Probes** | Designed probe kits sent to participants to gather evocative, subjective data about daily life | Interpretive; phenomenological | Gaver, Dunne & Pacenti (1999) ACM Interactions; Gaver et al. (2004) | CHI, DIS, Goldsmiths |
| **Experience Sampling Method (ESM)** | In-situ sampling of momentary experience at random intervals | Empirical-phenomenological | Csikszentmihalyi & Larson (1987); Hektner et al. (2007) | CHI, CSCW, psychology |
| **Diary Studies** | Participants record experiences over time; longitudinal naturalistic data | Interpretive; longitudinal | Bolger, Davis & Rafaeli (2003) *Psychological Methods*; Carter & Mankoff (2005) | CHI, CSCW |
| **Design Anthropology** | Merges design and anthropological inquiry; design as cultural practice | Social anthropology; interpretivism | Gunn, Otto & Smith (2013) *Design Anthropology*; Kjærsgaard & Otto (2012) | Design Studies, anthropology |
| **Autoethnography in HCI** | Researcher's lived experience as primary data for design inquiry | First-person; reflexive | O'Kane et al. (2014); Lucero (2018); CHI 2024 systematic review | CHI, CSCW |
| **Speed Dating** | Structured technique for rapidly evaluating multiple design concepts with users | Pragmatist; formative evaluation | Davidoff et al. (2007) CHI; Smulders et al. (2008) | CHI, UX practice |

### 2.7 Practice-Based & Artist-Led Family

| Methodology | Core Definition | Epistemological Anchor | Founding Papers / Books | Key Community |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Artist-Led Research** | Artists use their practice as mode of inquiry; artwork as knowledge claim | Practice theory; performativity | Frayling (1993); Sullivan (2005) *Art Practice as Research*; Biggs & Büchler (2011) | Fine art, RCA, studio doctorates |
| **Research through Art (RtA)** | Parallel to RtD but foregrounding aesthetic, affective, and imaginative dimensions | Aesthetic epistemology; practice-based | Elkins (2009) *Artists with PhDs*; Bolt (2016) | Fine arts research, A/r/tography |
| **Design Activism** | Design as a form of political activism; social change through making | Critical theory; action research | Fuad-Luke (2009) *Design Activism*; Clarke (2018) | DRS, social design |
| **Performative Research** | Practice-led research where performance/making is the mode of inquiry | Performativity (Butler/Barad); practice theory | Haseman (2006) *TAFE Journal*; Barrett & Bolt (2010) | Arts research, performance studies |

## Part III: Methods

*Methods* are specific techniques or procedures that can be employed across multiple methodologies.

### 3.1 Generative & Making Methods

| Method | Description | Associated Methodologies | Canonical Reference |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Prototyping** | Creating material or digital representations to explore and test design ideas | RtD, PD, SCD, constructive DR | Houde & Hill (1997) CHI; Lim et al. (2008) CHI; Buxton (2007) *Sketching User Experiences* |
| **Critical Making** | Joining hands-on making with critical inquiry to produce cultural critique | Critical design, DIY electronics, art | Ratto (2011) *The Information Society* |
| **Experience Prototyping** | Simulating experiences (not just artifacts) to explore design ideas | RtD, SCD, soma design | Buchenau & Suri (2000) DIS |
| **Speed Dating (design)** | Rapidly exploring multiple design concepts to evaluate feasibility and desirability | CHI systems, constructive DR | Davidoff et al. (2007) CHI |
| **Bodystorming** | Physical immersion and embodied role-play to generate design ideas | Soma design, participatory design | Oulasvirta et al. (2003) CHI; Burns et al. (1994) |
| **Co-design Workshops** | Structured participatory sessions where users and designers create together | PD, co-design, transition design | Sanders & Stappers (2008); Brandt et al. (2013) PDC |
| **Scenario-Based Design** | Using narrative scenarios to guide and evaluate design decisions | Interaction design, PD, futures | Carroll (2000) *Making Use*; Rosson & Carroll (2002) |
| **Wizard of Oz (WoZ)** | A human "wizard" simulates system behavior to enable user studies of not-yet-built systems | CHI systems, constructive DR | Kelley (1983); Dahlbäck et al. (1993) |
| **Speculative Prototyping / Diegetic Prototyping** | Making fictional artifacts that appear in speculative world-building | SCD, design fiction, world-building | Kirby (2010) *Social Studies of Science*; Bleecker (2009) |
| **Generative Toolkit** | Providing participants with visual/physical elements to externalize latent needs and dreams | PD, co-design, co-creation | Sanders & Stappers (2008); Hanington & Martin (2012) |

## Part IV: Knowledge Forms

Design research does not only produce findings in the conventional sense. This table maps the distinctive *forms of knowledge* (contributions) that design research methodologies produce.

| Knowledge Form | Definition | How Produced | Example Work | Reference |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Strong Concepts** | Mid-level design abstractions with generative potential beyond any single design instance | Abstracted from design practice across multiple projects | "Seamfulness," "Feed," "Social navigation" | Höök & Löwgren (2012) ACM TOCHI; DOI: 10.1145/2362364.2362371 |
| **Annotated Portfolios** | Collections of design exemplars with annotations that surface implicit design knowledge | Comparative analysis across a curated set of design works | Gaver (2012) portfolio of cultural probes | Gaver & Bowers (2012) DIS; DOI: 10.1145/2317956.2317960 |
| **Bridging Concepts** | Concepts that translate from theory *into* practice (complementary to strong concepts) | Identifying actionable bridges between theory and design | "Proxemics in design" from Hall's proxemics theory | Dalsgaard & Dindler (2014) CHI; DOI: 10.1145/2556288.2557143 |
| **Research Products** | Designed artifacts deployed in real homes/contexts for extended time; the artifact IS the knowledge | Long-term field deployment (weeks/months) | Wayve — AI wall clock for families | Odom et al. (2016) DIS; DOI: 10.1145/2901790.2901840 |
| **Design Exemplars / Precedents** | Worked examples that demonstrate how to approach a design problem | Building and reflecting on a single design case in depth | Placebo project (Dunne & Raby 2001) | Cross (2006) *Designerly Ways of Knowing*; Gaver (2012) |
| **Intermediate-Level Knowledge** | Umbrella term for knowledge between specific instances and universal theory | Varies (see above) | See all forms above | Löwgren (2013) Interactions; DOI: 10.1145/2427076.2427080 |
| **Ultimate Particulars** | Knowledge of *this* specific design situation; resists abstraction | Deep situational judgment; tacit expertise | Master designer's solution to a specific brief | Stolterman (2008) *She Ji*; DOI: 10.1016/j.sheji.2019.06.002 |
| **Theories of Design** | Systematic conceptual frameworks explaining design phenomena | Literature synthesis, empirical generalization | Schön's "reflection-in-action"; cross's "designerly ways" | Schön (1983); Cross (2001) |
| **Design Knowledge in Artifacts** | Tacit knowledge embedded in the design artifact itself; visible through examination | Making and reflection | "The object knows things we don't" | Wakkary (2021) *Things We Could Design* |
| **Propositional Knowledge via Design** | Explicit claims about people, technology, or design enabled by the design process | RtD methodology; constructive design research | "Slow technology creates reflective pauses" | Zimmerman et al. (2007); Hallnäs & Redström (2001) |
| **Constructive Knowledge** | Knowledge gained by constructing and exploring alternative possibilities | Constructive DR; world-building | Lab/field/showroom knowledge types | Koskinen et al. (2011) |
| **Implicature** | Tacit knowledge implied by design decisions, discoverable by others through use | Cultural probes and generative toolkits | Probe results that open lines of inquiry | Gaver, Dunne & Pacenti (1999) |
| **Performative Knowledge** | Knowledge produced *in the act* of performing/making; cannot be fully verbalized | Practice-based research; soma design | Somatic design explorations | Haseman (2006); Höök (2018) |
| **Critique as Knowledge** | The critical insight produced by confronting a design artifact with normalized assumptions | Critical design; adversarial design | "Placebo" project revealing assumptions about electronic objects | Dunne (1999); DiSalvo (2012) |

## Part VII: Emerging Trends (2024–2026)

Five macro-trends extending the genealogy:

1. **Self-Codification Wave** — Fields producing their own handbooks: soma design (Höök 2018), MtHD, speculative design. Methods becoming teachable.
2. **Generative AI Disruption** — AI as co-designer; challenges authorship, originality, and intermediate knowledge forms. Nascent: AI-mediated RtD, LLM-assisted futures scenarios.
3. **More-than-Human Acceleration** — MtHD moving from marginal to mainstream; multispecies design at CHI; animal-computer interaction as sub-field.
4. **Counter-Movement Consolidation** — Critiques of SCD's eurocentrism, elitism, and lack of rigor. Evaluation criteria emerging (Ringfort-Felner et al. 2023–2026).
5. **Handbook Maturation** — Methods once radical (speculative, somatic, participatory) now in textbooks. Codification = institutionalization = potential ossification.

### 8.1 Additional Paradigms

#### Additional Notes on Japanese Ma (間)

*Ma* is the Japanese concept of negative space, pause, interval, and emptiness as constitutive of aesthetic experience. In design, it operates as: (1) spatial negative space (architecture, product design), (2) temporal pause (music, performance, interaction timing), and (3) relational gap (the space between people or objects that defines their relationship). Unlike Western deficit-framing of absence, *ma* treats emptiness as productive and meaningful. Design implications: slow technology (Hallnäs & Redström), calm computing (Weiser & Brown 1995), breath in interaction (pause as signal), white space in visual design.
