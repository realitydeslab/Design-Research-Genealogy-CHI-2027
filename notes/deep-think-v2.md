## 5a. Position Statement

### Where This Paper Stands

This paper sits at the intersection of **meta-design-research** (studying design research itself), **scientometrics/bibliometrics** (systematic mapping of literatures), and **intellectual history** (tracing how ideas evolve and branch). It speaks primarily to the CHI/DIS/PDC community but draws methodology from information science (PRISMA) and epistemological framing from philosophy of design.

### What Our Genealogical Mapping Reveals That the Observatory Doesn't

The Design Research Observatory (Lindley & Coulton, Lancaster) provides a **synchronic** taxonomy: 21 concepts with cross-links, frozen in time. Our PRISMA scoping review adds three dimensions the Observatory cannot:

1. **Diachronic depth**: When did each connection form? What was the historical sequence? Who influenced whom, and in what order? The Observatory says "Design Fiction relates to Speculative Design" but doesn't document that Sterling coined the term in 2005, Bleecker formalized it in 2009, and Lindley & Coulton traced its first decade in 2015.

2. **Epistemological grounding**: The Observatory lists concepts but doesn't explain *why* design research produces knowledge. Our genealogy identifies constructionism as the missing epistemological foundation and traces how it implicitly undergirds RtD, constructive design research, critical making, and material speculation.

3. **Tension mapping**: The Observatory presents connections but not conflicts. Our genealogy maps productive tensions: formalization vs. openness (Zimmerman vs. Gaver), provocation vs. solution (speculative vs. participatory), human-centered vs. more-than-human, Western vs. pluriversal. These tensions are the evolutionary pressures driving methodological diversification.

4. **Completeness beyond curation**: The Observatory is curated by two scholars (Lindley & Coulton) from a particular position (Lancaster, Design Fiction/IoT). Our PRISMA review is systematic: we don't select what to include based on taste but follow a documented protocol. This surfaces lineages the Observatory underrepresents: feminist HCI, design justice, transition design, decolonial design.

### The Unique Contribution Beyond "We Made a Family Tree"

A genealogy is not just a map — it is a **causal explanation**. We explain *why* the field looks the way it does by tracing the intellectual, institutional, and philosophical forces that shaped it. The genealogy answers: Why did more-than-human design emerge in 2016–17 and not earlier? (Because ANT, posthumanism, and IoT converged to make nonhuman agency practically relevant.) Why does speculative design face a legitimacy crisis? (Because it inherited quality norms from three incompatible parent traditions: critical design, design fiction, and futures studies.)

## 5b. Contribution Statement

### Contribution 1: First Systematic Genealogical Mapping of Design Research Methodologies
**Evidence:** No prior work has used PRISMA methodology to systematically trace the branching, convergence, and cross-pollination of design research methods. Existing work is either focused on single lineages (Malpass 2017 on critical design; Bødker et al. 2022 on PD) or curated rather than systematic (the Observatory).
**So what:** Provides the field with a shared reference map for understanding how its methods relate to each other — essential as design research matures and diversifies.

### Contribution 2: Identification of Constructionism as the Missing Epistemological Foundation
**Evidence:** RtD repeatedly claims that "designing produces knowledge" but rarely cites the learning theory that explains why. We trace the constructionism → RtD connection explicitly and show how it grounds other lineages (critical making, material speculation, prototyping as research).
**So what:** Resolves a longstanding epistemological gap in design research methodology by providing the theoretical foundation the field implicitly relies on but hasn't articulated.

### Contribution 3: Documentation of the HCD → More-than-Human Paradigm Shift
**Evidence:** We map the specific intellectual trajectory from Norman (1988) through internal critiques (Bannon 1991; Dourish 2006; Bødker 2006) to posthuman design (Forlano 2017; Giaccardi & Redström 2020; Wakkary 2021). No prior work traces this full arc as a genealogical narrative.
**So what:** Makes explicit the most significant paradigm shift in contemporary design research, allowing the community to understand it as a coherent intellectual movement rather than a collection of individual departures.

### Contribution 4: Tension Mapping as Evolutionary Explanation
**Evidence:** We identify and analyze 6+ productive tensions that drive methodological evolution (formalization vs. openness, provocation vs. solution, human vs. nonhuman, etc.). These are not just observations but explanations of why new methods emerge.
**So what:** Reframes methodological debates as generative forces rather than problems to be resolved, providing a more productive frame for the field's ongoing self-organization.

### Contribution 5: Diachronic Complement to the Observatory's Synchronic Taxonomy
**Evidence:** Direct comparison between the Observatory's 21-concept map and our genealogical tracing reveals temporal sequences, causal relationships, and missing connections that a synchronic taxonomy cannot capture.
**So what:** Demonstrates the value of systematic scoping review methodology for mapping fields that are actively being self-organized through curated resources like the Observatory.

## 5c. Critique & Defense

### Critique 1: "Why does a genealogy matter? Who cares about the family tree?"
**Severity:** HIGH — the fundamental "so what" question
**Attack:** A genealogy is descriptive, not prescriptive. It tells us where methods came from but not where to go next. Reviewers will ask: "What does a practitioner do differently after reading this?"
**Defense:** Genealogies reveal hidden assumptions and unexamined connections. Knowing that material speculation bridges RtD and speculative design helps researchers position their work. Knowing that constructionism grounds RtD gives the field an epistemological vocabulary. Knowing that the HCD→MtHD shift follows a specific pattern helps predict and navigate future paradigm transitions.
**Preemptive action:** Include a "Implications for Practice" section showing concrete examples of how genealogical awareness changes methodological choices. Frame the genealogy as a "navigational tool" for researchers.

### Critique 2: "The PRISMA methodology is from medicine — is it appropriate for design research?"
**Severity:** HIGH — methodological legitimacy
**Attack:** PRISMA was developed for clinical evidence synthesis. Design research is not clinical. The search terms, inclusion criteria, and quality assessment may not translate.
**Defense:** PRISMA has been adapted for many non-clinical fields (education, social science, HCI). Our adaptation follows established precedents: Kitchenham & Charters (2007) for software engineering, Petticrew & Roberts (2006) for social science. We document all adaptations transparently. The alternative (narrative review) would be less rigorous and more vulnerable to selection bias.
**Preemptive action:** Explicitly document our PRISMA adaptation in the method section. Cite precedents for PRISMA in HCI (Brereton et al. 2007). Acknowledge limitations of the approach.

### Critique 3: "This is Eurocentric — the genealogy only traces Western design research traditions"
**Severity:** HIGH — the decolonial critique
**Attack:** The genealogy's roots (Frayling, Scandinavian PD, RCA) are overwhelmingly European. Non-Western design traditions are treated as add-ons (Afrofuturism, decolonial design) rather than independent genealogies.
**Defense:** We acknowledge this explicitly. Our search protocol necessarily reflects the literature's own biases — ACM DL, Scopus, and Web of Science are English-language databases that underrepresent non-Western scholarship. However, we include Afrofuturism, decolonial design (Escobar, Schultz et al.), and Indigenous speculative design (Eschrich et al. 2025) not as periphery but as active challenges to the genealogy's Western trunk. The genealogy documents Eurocentrism as a structural feature of the field, not just an oversight.
**Preemptive action:** Dedicate a section to "Genealogical Absences" — what the genealogy misses and why. Frame the Western bias as a finding about the field, not a limitation of our method alone. Include explicit calls for future work tracing non-Western design research genealogies.

### Critique 4: "The genealogy is incomplete — you missed X"
**Severity:** MEDIUM — inevitable for any mapping project
**Attack:** Where is somaesthetic design (Höök)? Autobiographical design (Neustaedter & Sengers)? Ludic design (Gaver)? Design activism (Fuad-Luke)? Any reviewer will know lineages we didn't cover.
**Defense:** A genealogy maps the major branches, not every twig. Our PRISMA protocol has explicit inclusion criteria that determine scope. We acknowledge missing lineages in the limitations and invite extensions.
**Preemptive action:** Include a "Known Gaps and Future Extensions" subsection listing lineages we identified but could not fully trace within scope. Frame the genealogy as extensible (a living map, not a final word).

### Critique 5: "The quality of your citation verification is uneven"
**Severity:** MEDIUM — operational concern
**Attack:** With 200+ papers across 18 lineages, some citations may be inaccurate, some DOIs may be wrong, and some characterizations may misrepresent the cited work.
**Defense:** All citations include DOIs or URLs for verification. Key foundational papers were read in full. Secondary citations are characterized based on abstracts and contextual use in the literature. We make our database available for community correction.
**Preemptive action:** Publish the full citation database as supplementary material. Flag any citations that are characterized based on secondary rather than primary reading.

### Critique 6: "You're just repackaging the Observatory — what's new?"
**Severity:** MEDIUM — novelty concern
**Attack:** The Observatory already maps these 21 concepts. Your paper just adds dates and citations.
**Defense:** See Position Statement above. The Observatory is synchronic; we are diachronic. The Observatory is curated; we are systematic. The Observatory maps connections; we map tensions, phases, and causal relationships. We also cover lineages the Observatory doesn't (feminist HCI, design justice, transition design). The Observatory is related work, not a predecessor that makes ours redundant.
**Preemptive action:** Position the Observatory explicitly as related work in the paper, with a dedicated comparison section.

## 5d. Observatory as Related Work

### The Observatory IS Related Work

The Design Research Observatory (https://observatory.designresearch.works/) is a curated, public resource created by Joe Lindley and Paul Coulton at Lancaster University (Imagination Lancaster). It defines 21 key design research concepts with glossary entries, cross-links, and bibliographies.

### How We Position Against It

| Dimension | Observatory | Our Paper |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Approach** | Curated by 2 scholars | Systematic PRISMA review |
| **Temporal frame** | Synchronic (concepts as they are now) | Diachronic (how concepts evolved) |
| **Structure** | Glossary with cross-links | Genealogical network with phases |
| **Scope** | 21 concepts | 21 Observatory concepts + feminist HCI, design justice, transition design, futuring |
| **Epistemology** | Mentions constructionism | Traces constructionism as foundational |
| **Tensions** | Not mapped | Explicitly mapped as evolutionary drivers |
| **Decolonial critique** | Mentions Afrofuturism | Centers decolonial critique as structural challenge |
| **Audience** | Practitioners and students | Researchers and methodologists |
| **Format** | Living web resource | Archival paper with fixed analysis |

### What Our Systematic PRISMA Review Adds

1. **Reproducibility**: Our method is documented and replicable; the Observatory's curation criteria are implicit.
2. **Completeness guarantees**: PRISMA requires documented search strategy, inclusion criteria, and PRISMA flow diagram. We can show what we searched and what we excluded.
3. **Temporal analysis**: We can date connections and trace sequences of influence — the Observatory cannot.
4. **Institutional analysis**: We can identify which departments, which conferences, which funding bodies shaped each lineage — the Observatory does not.
5. **Tension analysis**: We explicitly map conflicts and competing frameworks — the Observatory presents harmonious connections.

### Acknowledging the Observatory's Strengths

The Observatory has strengths we should acknowledge:
- **Accessibility**: Web-based, free, evolving — reaches a broader audience than our paper
- **Curation quality**: Lindley and Coulton are expert practitioners, not just analysts
- **Cross-disciplinary bridging**: Written for practitioners as well as researchers
- **Living resource**: Can be updated; our paper is frozen at publication

### Recommended Framing

"The Design Research Observatory (Lindley & Coulton) provides an invaluable curated map of the field. Our genealogical mapping is complementary: where the Observatory shows what the field looks like *now*, we trace how it *got here* — through which intellectual lineages, institutional channels, and productive tensions. We use the Observatory's 21-concept taxonomy as scaffolding and extend it with systematic PRISMA methodology, temporal analysis, and tension mapping."
