# Design Research Genealogy: Draft Mapping v3 (Complete)

## Overview

This document maps the genealogical relationships between design research methodologies, informed by the Design Research Observatory's 21-concept taxonomy, exhaustive literature searching (500+ papers across 22 literature files), and person-based deep research on key researchers in each lineage. The genealogy spans from 1970s Scandinavian PD through to the posthuman/multispecies turn of the 2020s, covering **20+ interconnected lineages** organized into epistemological, methodological, speculative, participatory, observational, temporal, practice-based, and contextual layers.

**Key changes from v2:** v3 adds two major new lineages — **Artist-led / Performance-led Research** (Benford, Gaver showroom, Höök soma design) and **Infrastructural Speculation** (Wong) — and integrates practice-based research epistemology as a second epistemological foundation alongside constructionism. The total literature base has expanded from ~200 to 500+ papers.

## Structural Layers

### Layer 1: Epistemological Foundations

**Constructionism** (Papert 1980; 1991)
- Making = learning = knowledge production
- Provides the epistemological justification for RtD's central claim
- Cross (1982, 2001) "designerly ways of knowing" articulates this for design specifically
- Schön (1983) "reflection-in-action" provides the practice-theoretical complement

**Practice-Based Research Epistemology** (Haseman 2006; Borgdorff 2012; Nelson 2013)
- *NEW in v3.* Artistic/performative practice as a legitimate mode of knowledge production
- Haseman (2006) "A Manifesto for Performative Research" — argues for a third research paradigm beyond quantitative and qualitative
- Borgdorff (2012) "The Conflict of the Faculties" — research on/for/through the arts (paralleling Frayling)
- Nelson (2013) "Practice as Research in the Arts" — comprehensive framework for PaR
- Provides epistemological grounding for the showroom tradition that constructionism alone cannot

### Layer 2: Methodological Core

**Research through Design (RtD)** (Frayling 1993 → Zimmerman et al. 2007)
- The central methodology from which speculative, critical, material, temporal, and artist-led approaches branch
- Koskinen et al. (2011): **lab/field/showroom** taxonomy for constructive design research
- Gaver & Bowers (2012) annotated portfolios vs. Höök & Löwgren (2012) strong concepts = competing intermediate knowledge forms

**Prototyping as Research** (Houde & Hill 1997 → Buchenau & Suri 2000 → Lim et al. 2008)
- The material practice through which RtD produces knowledge
- Ratto (2011) "critical making" extends prototyping into critical inquiry
- Rosner (2018) craft + computation as research

**Prototyping → Provotypes** (Mogensen 1992 → Boer & Donovan 2012)
- Provocative prototypes: high-fidelity artifacts deployed to provoke reactions and challenge assumptions
- Mogensen (1992) coined in Scandinavian PD; Boer & Donovan (2012) revived at DIS
- **Bridging concept**: connects PD's participatory ethics + critical design's provocation + prototyping's material practice
- Distinct from speculative design: provotypes are ethnographically grounded and participatory, not gallery-exhibited

### Layer 3: Speculative Branch

**RtD → Speculative Design** (Dunne 1999 → Dunne & Raby 2013)
- Design for provocation rather than problem-solving
- The PPPP futures cone (adapted from Voros 2003 foresight framework)
- Quality/legitimacy crisis: Ringfort-Felner et al. (2023→2025→2026) three-paper thread documenting the field's attempt to codify evaluation criteria

**Speculative Design → Design Fiction** (Sterling 2005 → Bleecker 2009 → Lindley & Coulton 2015)
- Narrative-based speculation; diegetic prototypes (Kirby 2010)
- Design Fiction as World Building (Coulton & Lindley 2017)

**Design Fiction → World Building** (Coulton et al. 2017)
- Internally-consistent fictional worlds as design research contexts
- Distinct from storytelling: worlds, not narratives, are the unit of analysis

**Design Fiction + Ethnography → Anticipatory Ethnography** (Lindley, Sharma & Potts 2014)
- Hybrid method: ethnographic analysis of design fiction artifacts
- Extends ethnographic temporal scope into the future

**RtD → Critical Design** (Dunne 1999 → Bardzell & Bardzell 2013 → Malpass 2017)
- Design as questioning societal norms
- Malpass (2017) distinguishes associative, speculative, and critical strands
- Pierce (2021) "frictional design" — design that deliberately generates friction

**Speculative Design → Afrofuturism / Decolonial Design** (Dery 1993 → Eshun 2003 → Harrington et al. 2022)
- Decolonial speculative practice challenging SCD's Eurocentrism
- Prado & Oliveira (2014); Schultz et al. (2018); Escobar (2018); Costanza-Chock (2020)
- Ansari — decolonial design theory

**RtD + Speculative Design → Material Speculation** (Wakkary et al. 2015)
- Functional prototypes deployed in everyday settings as critical inquiry
- Bridges gallery-based speculation and situated research
- Wakkary (2021) "Things We Could Design" — book-length treatment

**Speculative Design + Infrastructure Studies → Infrastructural Speculation** (Wong 2018–)
- *NEW in v3.* Speculative design methods applied to data governance, privacy, and infrastructure
- Wong & Mulligan (2019) "Bringing Design to the Privacy Table" — bridging design and policy
- Wong et al. (2020) "Expanding Modes of Reflection in Design Futuring"
- Branching point circa 2018-2020: speculative design meets infrastructure/values studies
- Key researchers: Wong, Khovanskaya, Fox, Merrill, Sengers, Pierce, Mulligan

### Layer 4: Participatory Branch

**Participatory Design (PD)** (Scandinavian 1970s → Ehn 1988 → Schuler & Namioka 1993)
- Parallel trunk, not a branch of RtD — predates Frayling
- Democratic participation as design principle
- Cultural probes (Gaver, Dunne & Pacenti 1999) bridge PD and RtD

**PD ↔ Co-Design** (Sanders & Stappers 2008)
- Bidirectional relationship; sometimes interchangeable
- Co-design expands PD beyond Scandinavian workplace tradition

### Layer 5: Observational Branch

**Ethnography** (Suchman 1987 → Button & Dourish 1996 → Crabtree et al. 2012)
- Root method entering HCI through workplace studies
- Dourish (2006) "implications for design" critique
- Bell (2006+) cultural ethnography at Intel — anticipatory anthropology

**Ethnography + More-than-Human Design → Thing Ethnography** (Giaccardi et al. 2016)
- Imagining object perspectives; things as co-ethnographers
- Practical entry point for more-than-human design
- Nicenboim — more-than-human data ethnography

### Layer 6: Temporal/Reflective Branch

**Slow Technology** (Hallnäs & Redström 2001)
- Reflection and calmness over speed and efficiency
- Extended by Odom, Wakkary et al. through long-term deployed artifacts
- Connects to Gaver's long-term field deployments (electronic furniture)

**Foresight** (Slaughter 1995 → Voros 2003 → Candy 2010)
- Professional futures discipline providing systematic methods
- Horizon scanning, scenario planning, backcasting, CLA
- Candy's experiential futures bridges foresight and design

**Design Futuring / Transition Design** (Fry 2009 → Irwin 2015 → Celik & Kaya 2025)
- Fry: "defuturing" — how design destroys futures through unsustainable practices
- Willis (2006) ontological designing — "design designs us back"
- Irwin, Tonkinwise, Kossoff (CMU) — transition design as sustainability-oriented practice
- 120+ papers mapped in exhaustive futuring literature review

### Layer 7: Practice-Based / Artist-Led Branch

*NEW in v3. This layer was missing from v2 entirely.*

**RtD Showroom → Artist-Led Research** (Benford & Blast Theory 1999– → Benford & Giannachi 2011)
- Performance-led research: creating, touring, and studying public performances as HCI research
- Benford et al. (2013) "Performance-Led Research in the Wild" — the methodological manifesto
- 20+ year Nottingham Mixed Reality Lab × Blast Theory collaboration
- Key works: Can You See Me Now? (2001), Uncle Roy All Around You (2003), Desert Rain (1999)

**Artist-Led → Uncomfortable Interactions** (Benford et al. 2012)
- CHI Best Paper. Deliberately designing discomfort as part of powerful cultural experiences
- Directly challenges the usability paradigm
- Extends to "aesthetic failure" (Hazzard et al. 2019) and "contesting control" (Benford et al. 2021)

**Artist-Led → Trajectories** (Benford & Giannachi 2009)
- Framework for designing coherent journeys through user experiences
- Derived from performance experience; became influential beyond art contexts

**RtD Showroom → Ludic Design** (Gaver 2004–)
- Cultural probes (1999), Drift Table (2004), electronic furniture for the curious home
- Gaver (2012) "What Should We Expect from Research Through Design?" — annotated portfolios
- Ambiguity as a resource for design (Gaver, Beaver & Benford 2003)
- Interaction Research Studio (Goldsmiths → Northumbria)

**RtD Showroom → Soma Design** (Höök 2017– → Höök 2018 book)
- Somaesthetic interaction design — designing with and for the felt, living body
- Draws on Shusterman's somaesthetics (pragmatist philosophy)
- Höök (2018) "Designing with the Body" (MIT Press) — the definitive text

**Artist-Led + Soma Design → Somabotics** (Benford × Höök 2021–)
- Current convergence point: Höök et al. (2021) "Unpacking Non-Dualistic Design"
- Tennent et al. (2021) "Articulating Soma Experiences Using Trajectories"
- Benford is now UKRI Turing AI Fellow exploring "Somabotics"

**Pervasive Games & LARP as Research** (Waern, Back, Isbister)
- Related strand: games/LARP as design research contexts
- Johansson et al. (2024) "Why Larp?" — synthesis of LARP × HCI

### Layer 8: Contextual Layer (Paradigms)

**Human-Centered Design (HCD)** (Norman 1988 → ISO 9241)
- The dominant paradigm; the origin point the posthuman turn responds to

**HCD → [challenged by] → More-than-Human Design** (Forlano 2017 → Giaccardi & Redström 2020 → Wakkary 2021)
- The most prominent paradigm shift in the genealogy
- Draws on Haraway, Barad, Braidotti, Latour
- Benford et al. (2024) "Designing Multispecies Worlds" extends this into artist-led contexts

**HCI Three Paradigms** (Harrison et al. 2007) → "Fourth Wave" (Frauenberger 2019)

**Feminist HCI** (Bardzell 2010) — cross-cutting lens intersecting all branches

**Design Justice** (Costanza-Chock 2020) — cross-cutting lens intersecting PD + futures + decolonial

## Seven Key Insights for the Paper

1. **The genealogy is a layered network, not a tree.** Design research methods exist in epistemological, methodological, speculative, participatory, observational, temporal, and practice-based layers that cross-connect extensively.

2. **Two epistemological foundations, not one.** Constructionism grounds why *making* produces knowledge; practice-based research epistemology grounds why *artistic practice* produces knowledge. Together they support the full range of RtD.

3. **The showroom tradition is a family, not a single lineage.** Ludic design (Gaver), performance-led research (Benford), speculative design (Dunne & Raby), and soma design (Höök) share an exhibition/provocation orientation but differ in their relationship to artistic practice, embodiment, and everyday life.

4. **The HCD → More-than-Human shift is the field's primary paradigm transition.** Documented through ANT, posthumanism, thing ethnography, and now multispecies artist-led research.

5. **Speculative design is diversifying into domain-specific branches.** Material speculation (everyday settings), infrastructural speculation (policy/governance), design futuring (sustainability/transitions), and Afrofuturism (decolonial futures) represent increasingly specialized applications of speculative methods.

6. **Active convergence points signal the field's future.** Somabotics (Benford × Höök), multispecies design (artist-led + MtHD), and design futuring (speculative + sustainability) are where new methods are currently being synthesized.

7. **The field is actively trying to codify itself.** The quality of speculation thread (Ringfort-Felner et al.), the Design Research Observatory (Lindley & Coulton), recent taxonomies (Snow et al. 2025, Cardenas et al. 2025, Celik & Kaya 2025), and now our genealogy represent meta-level efforts at self-organization.
